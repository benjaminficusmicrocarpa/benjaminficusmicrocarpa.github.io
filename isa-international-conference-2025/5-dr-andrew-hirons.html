üéØ Conference Overview

One of the greatest sources of tree mortality in the urban landscape is drought, or water deficit. Therefore, water management in urban landscapes is critical for tree establishment and ongoing tree performance.

üíß The Critical Role of Water

üß¨ Water in Tree Physiology

‚ñº

Water is fundamental to every aspect of tree biology and function:

  • Structural component: 70-95% of non-woody tissue and ~50% of wood
  • Photosynthesis substrate: Essential for splitting water to release oxygen
  • Transport medium: Solvent for gases, minerals, and growth regulators
  • Nutrient acquisition: Critical for mineral uptake from soil

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Only 1-5% of water absorbed by trees is retained in biomass‚Äîthe rest is transpired to the atmosphere!

üå°Ô∏è Atmospheric Demand & Vapor Pressure Deficit

‚ñº

The atmosphere exerts a powerful "suction force" on trees through vapor pressure deficit (VPD). This demand varies throughout the day:

Daily Atmospheric Demand Pattern

üåÖ Morning (low VPD) High humidity

üåô Night (low VPD) Minimal demand

Air water potential can easily drop below -100 MPa, creating an enormous gradient that pulls water from the tree. Sap flow mirrors this atmospheric demand with fine temporal resolution.

üíÄ Mechanisms of Tree Mortality

‚ñº

Dr. Hirons presented research from Nate McDowell's team identifying two interconnected pathways to death:

Mechanism Description Timeline Evidence
üö∞ Hydraulic Failure Progressive loss of water transport due to embolism Early stage Crown thinning, vessel blockage
üç¨ Carbon Starvation Depletion of energy reserves as photosynthesis shuts down Late stage NSC depletion in roots/stems

üîó Interconnection

Trees can't move sugars (carbon) without water as the transport medium. Hydraulic failure accelerates carbon starvation by cutting off sugar distribution.

ü¶† Biotic Amplifiers

Fungi and pathogens attacking fine roots accelerate hydraulic failure, while pest damage increases metabolic demand when carbon is already scarce.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Finding: Research across 26 diverse species shows hydraulic failure is a more ubiquitous driver of tree mortality than carbon depletion alone, though they work in tandem.

üìä IoT Sensors & Sap Flow Research

üåä Understanding Sap Flow

‚ñº

Dr. Hirons demonstrated how sap flow measurement provides unprecedented insight into tree water use. The technique uses the heat ratio method for its superior accuracy:

üî¨ How It Works

  1. Three probes are inserted into the tree: one heater and two temperature sensors (upstream and downstream)
  2. A heat pulse is introduced into the sapwood
  3. The ratio of heat arrival times at upstream vs. downstream probes indicates sap velocity
  4. No movement = heat reaches both probes simultaneously; upward flow = downstream probe heats faster
  5. Data is corrected for small wounding and converted to sap flux density

Sap Flow Mirrors Atmospheric Demand

Fine-resolution data (every 10 minutes) reveals how tree water use tracks VPD:

Warm, dry conditions

Cool, humid conditions
Low

üí° "You can read the weather based on sap flow‚ÄîI can tell when it rained, how long it rained, which were the cool days, which were the warm days."

üéØ Case Study: Irrigation Optimization

A tree nursery was irrigating at 2-3 AM on a timer. Sap flow data revealed:

  • ‚Ä¢ Water was draining below root zones on sandy soil before trees could use it
  • ‚Ä¢ Solution: Turn on irrigation 10 minutes before sap flow typically starts
  • ‚Ä¢ Result: Water stayed in root zone, massive efficiency gain, cost savings

üö® Early Problem Detection

When an irrigation solenoid failed, sensors detected the anomaly on day one‚Äîbefore any visual wilting. Without sensors, the failure might not be noticed until day ten when trees are already severely stressed.

üí° "Visual tree analysis is almost totally redundant. By the time you can see something going wrong, it's really already lost it. Sensors are much more attuned."

üéØ Ensure Water Reaches Roots

"It sounds simple, but it's also simple to get wrong:"

  • ‚Ä¢ For newly planted trees, water the root ball, not the surrounding native soil
  • ‚Ä¢ Avoid watering pavement or mulch while missing the actual root zone
  • ‚Ä¢ Use soaker hoses or drip irrigation for efficient delivery
  • ‚Ä¢ Create a shallow watering basin to direct water over roots
  • ‚Ä¢ For established trees, water the drip line (crown edge), not the trunk

üåæ Competition Management

Grass and weeds compete aggressively for water, especially during establishment:

  • ‚Ä¢ Mulch provides dual benefit: suppresses weeds while conserving water
  • ‚Ä¢ For meadow/prairie plantings, use compatible groundcovers that don't compete heavily

‚öñÔ∏è Avoid Over-Irrigation

‚ñº

"Too much water is also a bad thing‚Äîit's not really too much water, it's not enough oxygen."

üö´ Waterlogging Consequences

  • ‚Ä¢ Water displaces oxygen in soil pore spaces
  • ‚Ä¢ Roots require oxygen for respiration and nutrient uptake
  • ‚Ä¢ Prolonged saturation causes root death and decay
  • ‚Ä¢ Anaerobic conditions favor harmful pathogens (e.g., Phytophthora)
  • ‚Ä¢ Symptoms mimic drought: wilting, yellowing, dieback

üéØ Monitoring Soil Moisture

Use sensors or simple manual checks to avoid both under- and over-watering:

  • ‚Ä¢ Soil moisture sensors: Real-time data on volumetric water content
  • ‚Ä¢ Feel test: Soil should feel moist but not soggy; form a ball but crumble easily
  • ‚Ä¢ Observation: Standing water or constantly wet soil = too much
  • ‚Ä¢ Goal: Keep soil near field capacity, not saturation

üßÇ Manage Salinity in Irrigation Water

‚ñº

Dr. Hirons warned: "Ensure that the osmotic potential of the soil solution doesn't hinder root absorption."

‚ö†Ô∏è Salinity Challenges

  • ‚Ä¢ Grey water irrigation: Often contains high salt levels from detergents, soaps
  • ‚Ä¢ Coastal areas: Saltwater intrusion into groundwater
  • ‚Ä¢ De-icing salts: Roadside runoff accumulates in urban soils
  • ‚Ä¢ Arid regions: Natural salts concentrated by evaporation

How Salinity Affects Water Uptake

üíß Freshwater (low salts)
  • ‚úÖ High osmotic potential (less negative)
  • ‚úÖ Easy for roots to absorb water
  • ‚úÖ Normal cellular function
üßÇ Saline water (high salts)
  • ‚ùå Low osmotic potential (very negative)
  • ‚ùå Roots must work harder to absorb water
  • ‚ùå Can cause "physiological drought"

Even if soil is wet, high salt concentration can prevent trees from accessing water‚Äîeffectively creating drought conditions.

üîß Salinity Management

  • ‚Ä¢ Test water sources: Measure electrical conductivity (EC) before irrigating
  • ‚Ä¢ Leach salts: Apply extra water periodically to flush salts below root zone
  • ‚Ä¢ Select tolerant species: Some trees handle moderate salinity better
  • ‚Ä¢ Avoid grey water for sensitive species: Use fresh water when possible
  • ‚Ä¢ Improve drainage: Prevent salt accumulation in poorly draining soils

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Point: Salty water can be "more of a problem than a solution" for urban tree irrigation. Always assess water quality before use.

üìã Practical Action Checklist

‚ñº

‚úÖ Design & Planning Phase

  • Specify minimum soil volumes based on mature tree size (20-60+ m¬≥)
  • Incorporate structural soils, soil cells, or continuous trenches
  • Select species matched to site water availability and climate projections
  • Design stormwater integration to maximize tree access to rainfall

‚úÖ Planting & Establishment

  • Amend soil with compost or clay powder to improve water-holding capacity
  • Apply 3-4 inches of mulch in 3-6 ft radius, avoiding trunk contact
  • Install soaker hoses or drip irrigation targeting root zones
  • Water deeply (12-18") to encourage deep rooting

‚úÖ Monitoring & Maintenance

  • Install IoT sensors (soil moisture, dendrometers, sap flow) on sentinel trees
  • Connect sensors to dashboard for real-time feedback
  • Schedule irrigation based on sensor data and weather forecasts, not timers
  • Check for equipment failures (e.g., solenoid valves) using sensor anomalies
  • Test irrigation water for salinity; avoid grey water on sensitive species

‚úÖ Adaptive Management

  • Review sensor data trends to identify chronic stress before visual decline
  • Adjust irrigation timing to coincide with sap flow initiation (early morning)
  • Avoid over-irrigation; maintain soil near field capacity, not saturation
  • Update species palettes as climate projections evolve
