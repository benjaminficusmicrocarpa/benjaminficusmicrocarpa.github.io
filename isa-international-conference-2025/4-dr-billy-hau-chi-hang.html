Research Overview

üéØ Project Objectives

This three-year study (2021-2024) by The Conservancy Association and The University of Hong Kong's School of Biological Sciences, funded by HSBC's Climate Solutions Partnership, addresses critical knowledge gaps in Hong Kong's role in carbon sequestration and biodiversity conservation.

Primary Goals:

  • Quantify carbon storage capacity across different forest vegetation types in Hong Kong
  • Compare traditional field measurements with advanced remote sensing (LiDAR) techniques
  • Identify native tree species with optimal carbon sequestration and biodiversity value
  • Provide evidence-based recommendations for urban forestry and reforestation strategies

üå≤ Forest Carbon Storage Context

Forest ecosystems store approximately 861 ¬± 66 GtC globally in above-ground biomass, with tropical and subtropical forests contributing the largest share. Climate change impacts on forest structure, species composition, and mortality directly threaten this carbon storage capacity.

The IPCC emphasizes that nature-based solutions, including forest restoration and protection, can provide up to 30% of the carbon mitigation needed to limit warming to 1.5¬∞C, while simultaneously addressing biodiversity loss.

Hong Kong's subtropical forests face similar challenges: increased typhoon intensity, altered precipitation patterns, and rising temperatures may affect native tree growth rates and carbon accumulation. However, well-designed restoration using climate-resilient native species can enhance both carbon storage and biodiversity.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Vicious Cycle

Climate change and biodiversity loss form a mutually reinforcing feedback loop:

Biodiversity Loss ‚Üí Climate Change

Destruction of forests, wetlands, and other ecosystems releases stored carbon and reduces natural carbon sinks, intensifying greenhouse gas accumulation and global warming.

Climate Change ‚Üí Biodiversity Loss

Rising temperatures, altered precipitation patterns, and extreme weather events degrade habitats, disrupt ecological interactions, and push species beyond their physiological limits, causing population declines and extinctions.

üå≤ Breaking the Cycle: Nature-Based Solutions

Forest restoration and biodiversity conservation simultaneously address both crises by enhancing carbon sequestration, increasing ecosystem resilience, and providing multiple co-benefits for human well-being.

Research Methodology

üî¨ Dual-Method Approach

This study employed both traditional field measurements and advanced remote sensing to validate accuracy and compare methodologies:

üå≤ Method 1: Field Measurements

Advantages:

  • Species identification enables accurate wood density calculation (¬±50% variation)
  • Direct measurement of DBH and height
  • Proven reliability over decades of forestry science

Disadvantages:

  • Labor-intensive (requires trained personnel)
  • Time-consuming for large-scale surveys
üì° Method 2: Remote Sensing (LiDAR)

Advantages:

  • Rapid data collection across large areas
  • 3D forest structure mapping
  • Repeatable for long-term monitoring

Disadvantages:

  • Cannot identify species (major limitation)
  • Significant lab time for data processing/segmentation
  • Requires field validation
‚úÖ Study Conclusion

Both methods showed similar results with small disagreement, validating traditional field measurements as reliable despite higher manpower input. Species identification remains critical for accurate carbon estimation.

Research validation: The dual-method validation approach has been documented in peer-reviewed publications. For methodological details on handheld mobile laser scanning in subtropical forests, see Remote Sensing 2025.

üìê Plot Establishment Protocol

Plot Dimensions: 20m √ó 20m (400 m¬≤) ‚Äî standard forestry survey unit

Corner Marking: Permanent aluminum poles positioned using surveyor-grade laser beams for high-precision GPS coordinates

Boundary Delineation: Temporary measuring tapes outlined boundaries during active surveys; additional markers placed every 5 meters along plot edges for grid-based sampling

Permanent Plots: 90 plots established with permanent infrastructure to enable decadal re-measurement for growth rate and carbon accumulation studies

üîÑ Long-Term Monitoring Value

Permanent plots enable future researchers to track forest dynamics, carbon sequestration rates, and climate change responses over time ‚Äî transforming this from a snapshot study into a multi-decade monitoring program.

Wood Density Determination

Wood density is critical for accurate biomass calculation (varies up to 50% between species). The study employed a two-tier approach:

1. Literature Values: Species-specific data extracted from the Global Wood Density Database for well-documented taxa.
2. Direct Sampling: For Hong Kong species lacking published data, increment borer cores (pith-to-bark samples) were collected at tree-felling sites (partnership with Chun Wo Development Holdings Ltd.), dried, measured with digital calipers, and weighed to calculate density.

Note: All wood density data consolidated in the project's published reference book for future researchers.

üßÆ Biomass Calculation Methodology

Trees store carbon through photosynthesis, converting CO‚ÇÇ into cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin ‚Äî the primary structural components of wood.

Component Functions and Carbon Storage Relationship

Royal Society of Chemistry. Citation: Kumaravel, Sangeetha & Thiruvengetam, Prabaharan & . K, Karthick & Selvasundrasekar, Sam Sankar & Karmakar, Arun & Kundu, Subrata. (2020). Green and sustainable route for oxidative depolymerization of lignin: New platform for fine chemicals and fuels. Biotechnology Progress. 37. 10.1002/btpr.3111.

  • Cellulose: Primary structural polymer providing tensile strength. High cellulose content contributes to wood density and long-term carbon stability. Cellulose molecules form crystalline regions that resist decomposition, making it a critical component for durable carbon storage.
  • Hemicellulose: Amorphous polysaccharide that binds cellulose and lignin. Acts as a matrix material providing flexibility. While less stable than cellulose, it contributes to overall biomass and carbon content, though it decomposes faster in forest carbon cycling.
  • Lignin: Complex aromatic polymer providing rigidity and decay resistance. Higher lignin content correlates with greater structural stability and slower decomposition rates, making it essential for long-term carbon storage. Lignin-rich woods (typical in many hardwoods) maintain carbon stocks longer in forest ecosystems.

Carbon Storage Implication: The relative proportions of these components affect both the quantity of carbon stored (all three contain carbon) and the stability of that carbon (lignin and cellulose provide more persistent storage than hemicellulose). Wood density, influenced by these proportions, directly correlates with carbon storage per unit volume.
