üéØ Conference Overview

Over the past three years, our research team has studied Hong Kong's forests to assess their role in carbon sequestration amid climate change discussions. The project, launched in September 2021 by the Conservancy Association and HKU's School of Biological Sciences, is part of HSBC's 'Climate Solutions Partnership' and aims to provide scientific data for future plantation and emission reduction strategies.

The team surveyed 90 sites, collecting data from nearly 20,000 trees across various vegetation types, including Fung Shui Woods, secondary forests, plantations, and shrublands.

  • HKU School of Biological Sciences: World-class research institution with expertise in tropical ecology

‚öñÔ∏è Field Measurements vs Remote Sensing

‚ñº

The study employed both traditional field surveys and cutting-edge remote sensing technology to cross-validate results and assess the effectiveness of each approach for future large-scale monitoring.

‚úÖ Field Measurements

Advantages:
  • Species identification enables accurate wood density calculations
  • Direct DBH and height measurements provide ground truth
  • Can assess tree health and biodiversity simultaneously
  • Established methodology with proven accuracy
Limitations:
  • Labor-intensive and time-consuming
  • Limited spatial coverage per survey period
  • Weather-dependent fieldwork windows

üì° Remote Sensing (LiDAR)

Advantages:
  • Rapid data collection across large areas
  • Creates detailed 3D forest structure maps
  • Repeatable for monitoring change over time
  • Cost-effective for landscape-scale assessments
Limitations:
  • Cannot identify tree species directly
  • Requires extensive lab processing time
  • Wood density must be assumed or averaged
  • Up to 50% error if species-specific density unknown

üîç Study Findings

The research found small disagreement between the two methods when wood density was properly accounted for, validating both approaches. However, field measurements remain essential for:

  • Calibrating remote sensing models with ground truth data
  • Building species-specific allometric equations
  • Providing wood density databases critical for accurate carbon calculations

üéØ Recommended Approach

A hybrid methodology combining initial comprehensive field surveys to establish baselines and calibrate models, followed by remote sensing for efficient monitoring and updates, offers the best balance of accuracy and efficiency for Hong Kong's forest carbon accounting.

üåç Climate Change & Biodiversity

üîó The Vicious Cycle

‚ñº

Climate change and biodiversity loss are deeply interconnected in a reinforcing cycle. When we lose biodiversity and destroy ecosystems like wetlands and forests, we contribute to more severe climate change. Conversely, as climate changes with altered rainfall patterns and rising temperatures, it further erodes biodiversity.

üìâ Global Wildlife Decline

WWF's Living Planet Index 2024 reveals catastrophic losses:

  • 73% average decline in monitored wildlife populations since 1970
  • 85% decline in freshwater species populations
  • 95% decline in Latin America & Caribbean regions
  • Approaching tipping points for tropical forests and coral reefs

üå°Ô∏è IPCC Climate Findings

AR6 Synthesis Report (2023) confirms with high to very high confidence:

  • Widespread adverse impacts on terrestrial ecosystems
  • Freshwater ecosystem degradation globally
  • Ocean ecosystem stress and changes
  • Heat, malnutrition, and wildfire harm increasing

‚ö†Ô∏è Four Main Drivers of Biodiversity Loss

üèûÔ∏è Habitat Loss

Deforestation, wetland drainage, and land conversion destroy critical ecosystems faster than they can regenerate.

üé£ Overexploitation

Unsustainable harvesting of timber, fish, and wildlife depletes populations beyond recovery thresholds.

üè≠ Pollution

Industrial emissions, agricultural runoff, and plastic waste contaminate ecosystems and disrupt food webs.

ü¶é Invasive Species

Non-native species outcompete local flora and fauna, disrupting millennia of ecological balance.

üîÑ Breaking the Cycle

Nature-based solutions like forest restoration address both crises simultaneously by sequestering carbon while providing habitat for biodiversity. Hong Kong's potential to restore 30% of degraded habitats (40% current degraded vegetation) represents a significant opportunity to reverse these trends locally.

üå≤ Native Tree Species for Carbon Sequestration

The research identified 100 native tree species with excellent carbon sequestration potential and biodiversity value. These species are categorized by mature size and evaluated across multiple criteria to guide urban forestry, roadside planting, and large-scale reforestation efforts.

üìã Selection Criteria

Each species was assessed for:

  • Carbon Performance: Growth rate, wood density, and potential mature biomass
  • Biodiversity Value: Food resources (fruits, nectar) for wildlife, habitat structure
  • Maintenance Requirements: Water needs, pest resistance, pruning frequency
  • Climate Resilience: Typhoon tolerance, drought resistance, heat tolerance
  • Urban Suitability: Root system compatibility with infrastructure, air pollution tolerance

üéØ Planting Recommendations

Large tree species are best suited for:

  • Country Parks & Nature Reserves: Maximum carbon storage with minimal infrastructure conflict
  • Large Urban Parks: Select species with controlled root systems (e.g., Camphor over Banyan)
  • Coastal Reforestation: Wind-resistant species like Polyspora for typhoon-prone areas
  • Spacing: Minimum 8-10m between large trees to allow full canopy development
  • Long-term Planning: Reserve adequate space for 50-100 year mature size

ü¶ã Biodiversity Value Beyond Carbon

Small trees play irreplaceable ecological roles that large trees cannot fulfill:

  • Understory Habitat: Create vertical structure diversity essential for forest birds and mammals
  • Year-Round Resources: Many species flower/fruit when larger trees are dormant, providing critical food during scarcity
  • Microhabitat Creation: Dense branching at lower heights serves insects, reptiles, and small mammals
  • Succession Facilitation: Pioneer species prepare degraded soils for climax forest species
  • Urban Wildlife Corridors: Low stature fits under power lines while maintaining connectivity between parks

üèòÔ∏è Ideal Applications

  • Residential Gardens: 3-5m spacing in small yards for privacy and biodiversity
  • Schoolyards & Playgrounds: Safe heights for children's areas with educational value
  • Green Roofs & Planters: Species tolerating shallow substrates and weight constraints
  • Restoration Understory: Plant beneath canopy of large tree sites to accelerate forest structure complexity
  • Utility Corridors: Below power lines where medium trees still too tall

üî¨ High-Density Families (>0.65 g/cm¬≥)

Species from families like Fabaceae and Fagaceae offer multiple benefits:

  • Greater carbon storage: ~30% more C per unit volume than low-density species
  • Enhanced durability: Harder wood resists decay, locking carbon for centuries
  • Typhoon resistance: Stronger wood fibers better withstand extreme winds
  • Lower maintenance: Less prone to branch failure and storm damage

üåø Low-Density Families (<0.55 g/cm¬≥)

Species with lighter wood still valuable for specific applications:

  • Faster growth rates: Rapid volume accumulation can offset lower density
  • Pioneer role: Quick establishment on degraded sites prepares for climax species
  • Biodiversity champions: Often superior fruit/flower production (e.g., Ficus)
  • Reduced typhoon risk: Lighter canopy = less wind resistance, but more breakage risk

üì° Remote Sensing Technology

‚ñº

Handheld LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) technology was employed to create detailed 3D maps of forest structure and test its potential for efficient, large-scale carbon monitoring as a complement to labor-intensive field surveys.

üõ∞Ô∏è LiDAR Technology Overview

LiDAR emits rapid laser pulses (hundreds of thousands per second) and measures return time to calculate distances, creating a "point cloud" of millions of 3D coordinates representing forest surfaces.

How It Works:
  1. Operator walks through plot with handheld scanner
  2. Laser pulses penetrate canopy gaps
  3. Multiple returns captured: canopy top, mid-story, ground
  4. GPS/IMU sensors track scanner position/orientation
  5. Software merges millions of points into 3D model
Data Outputs:
  • Canopy height models (CHM)
  • Individual tree crown segmentation
  • Estimated DBH from stem profiles
  • Above-ground biomass maps
  • Forest structure metrics (density, gaps, layers)

Integration with Satellite Imagery & Deep Learning:

  • Satellite Multispectral: Landsat/Sentinel for vegetation indices (NDVI, EVI) indicating forest health/density
  • High-Resolution Imagery: WorldView/Pleiades for detailed canopy structure analysis

üéØ Study Conclusion: Hybrid Approach Recommended

The research found small disagreement between field and remote sensing methods when properly calibrated, but neither is a complete replacement for the other:

  • Optimal Strategy: Comprehensive field surveys at representative plots (5-10% of area) to establish baselines, build allometric equations, and create wood density databases
  • Scale-Up: Remote sensing (LiDAR + satellites + ML) for efficient wall-to-wall mapping and annual monitoring of changes
  • Periodic Re-calibration: Revisit field plots every 5-10 years to validate models, update equations for climate-driven growth changes, and assess long-term carbon accumulation
  • Hong Kong Application: 90 permanent plots provide robust calibration for territory-wide carbon maps, with capacity to track Northern Metropolis and other development impacts

üßÆ Carbon Calculation Process

‚ñº

Converting field measurements of tree dimensions into carbon storage estimates requires a multi-step scientific process based on established forestry methods and IPCC guidelines.

üå≥ From Tree to Carbon: The Complete Pathway

Step 1: Tree Volume Estimation

Simple cylindrical approximation is inadequate for actual tree forms. The study uses allometric equations that relate tree dimensions to biomass based on empirical data from harvested trees.

General Allometric Form:

AGB = a √ó DBH^b √ó H^c √ó WD^d

Where:

  • AGB = Above-ground biomass (kg)
  • DBH = Diameter at breast height (cm)
  • H = Tree height (m)
  • WD = Wood density (g/cm¬≥)
  • a, b, c, d = Empirically derived coefficients from destructive sampling
Step 2: Apply Species-Specific Equations

Hong Kong study prioritized local equations where available, supplemented by regional tropical/subtropical models:

Data Source Coverage Example
Hong Kong species-specific ~20 common species Camphor, Banyan, Schima
South China regional ~50 additional species Fagaceae, Lauraceae families
Pan-tropical equations Remaining species Chave et al. 2014 models
Step 3: Biomass to Carbon Conversion

Tree biomass consists of multiple elements. The carbon fraction must be calculated:

Carbon (kg) = AGB (kg) √ó 0.47

Standard conversion factor: 47% of dry biomass is carbon

This is the IPCC default based on extensive analysis of tree tissue composition across species. Some studies use 0.45-0.50 depending on tissue type, but 0.47 is standard for whole-tree estimates.

Step 4: Scale Up to Hectare Level

Individual tree carbon values are summed across all trees in the 400m¬≤ plot, then extrapolated:

Carbon per hectare = (Plot total carbon / 400m¬≤) √ó 10,000m¬≤/ha

Example from Fung Shui Woods:

  • 400m¬≤ plot contained: 6,528 kg carbon in above-ground biomass
  • Scaling factor: 10,000 / 400 = 25√ó
  • Result: 163,200 kg C/ha = 163 tonnes C/ha
Step 5: Below-Ground Biomass Estimation

Roots also store significant carbon but are impractical to excavate. Standard approach uses root-to-shoot ratios:

BGB = AGB √ó R

Where R (root-to-shoot ratio) varies by forest type:

  • Tropical forests: R = 0.24 (IPCC Tier 1 default)
  • Hong Kong application: BGB adds ~24% to total biomass
  • Example: 163 Mg C/ha above-ground + 39 Mg C/ha below-ground = 202 Mg C/ha total
Step 6: Uncertainty Analysis

All measurements and equations have inherent error. The study quantifies uncertainty:

Error Sources:
  • Measurement precision: ¬±5% for DBH/height
  • Allometric equation fit: ¬±10-20% depending on species
  • Wood density variation: ¬±10-50% if species-specific data unavailable
  • Spatial variation: ¬±15-30% among plots in same forest type

Reported values include ¬±standard error: e.g., "163 ¬± 20 Mg C/ha" indicates 95% confidence interval

üìä Worked Example: Calculating One Tree's Carbon

Let's calculate carbon storage for a mature Camphor tree (Cinnamomum camphora):

Measured values:

  • DBH = 60 cm
  • Height = 18 m
  • Wood density = 0.55 g/cm¬≥

Step 1 - Apply allometric equation (Chave et al. 2014 moist tropical):

AGB = 0.0673 √ó (WD √ó DBH¬≤ √ó H)^0.976

AGB = 0.0673 √ó (0.55 √ó 60¬≤ √ó 18)^0.976

AGB = 0.0673 √ó (35,640)^0.976

AGB = 0.0673 √ó 31,845

AGB = 2,143 kg

Step 2 - Convert to carbon:

Carbon = 2,143 kg √ó 0.47

Carbon = 1,007 kg = 1.01 tonnes C

Step 3 - Add below-ground:

BGB Carbon = 1.01 √ó 0.24 = 0.24 tonnes C

Total Carbon = 1.01 + 0.24 = 1.25 tonnes C

üí° This single 60cm tree stores carbon equivalent to ~3 months of average Hong Kong person's emissions (4.3 tonnes CO‚ÇÇ/year)

üåç CO‚ÇÇ Equivalence (Why Reports Differ)

Climate reports discuss CO‚ÇÇ rather than carbon. Conversion accounts for oxygen atoms:

CO‚ÇÇ = Carbon √ó (44/12) = Carbon √ó 3.67

Molecular weight basis: CO‚ÇÇ molecule = 1 carbon (12 g/mol) + 2 oxygen (16 g/mol each) = 44 g/mol

Example using Camphor tree above:

  • 1.25 tonnes C stored
  • Equivalent to 1.25 √ó 3.67 = 4.6 tonnes CO‚ÇÇ removed from atmosphere
  • This is why forest carbon storage appears different in C vs CO‚ÇÇ terms

üî¨ Validation Methods

How do scientists verify these calculations are accurate?

  • Destructive Sampling: Occasionally harvest entire trees, dry and weigh all components to validate equations (limited by conservation constraints)
  • LiDAR Cross-Validation: Compare field-based estimates with independent remote sensing biomass models
  • Long-term Monitoring: Re-measure permanent plots to track carbon accumulation rates over decades
  • Global Network: Hong Kong results compared to 70+ ForestGEO sites worldwide using identical protocols
  • Published Peer Review: Methods and results scrutinized by international experts before publication

‚öñÔ∏è Policy Barriers & Solutions

Barrier 1: Procurement defaults to exotic species

Solution: Update government Works Bureau Technical Circulars to specify "native species preferred unless justified otherwise." Require landscape architects to document why exotics chosen if used.

Barrier 2: Limited nursery supply of diverse natives

Solution: Government 5-year contract with 3-5 nurseries guaranteeing purchase of 50,000+ native seedlings annually. Predictable demand incentivizes production scaling. AFCD Tai Lung expansion to focus on native propagation.

Barrier 3: Fragmented responsibility across 10+ departments

Solution: Establish inter-departmental "Urban Greening Coordination Office" reporting to Chief Secretary. Annual targets for native species adoption, departments scored on progress.

Barrier 4: Public perception that exotic = attractive

Solution: "Hong Kong's Natural Heritage" public education campaign. Showcase flowering natives (Bauhinia championii, Rhododendron farrerae). Celebrity endorsements, school programs, nature documentary series.

‚úÖ Quick Win Pilot Projects (2026-2027)

Demonstrate feasibility and build momentum with high-visibility early successes:

  • Nathan Road Native Corridor: Replace 200 aging exotic street trees with mixed native species over 2km stretch. Monitor for 3 years, showcase reduced maintenance costs.
  • Victoria Park Retrofit: Plant 500 native understory trees and shrubs beneath existing canopy. Create "biodiversity trail" with interpretive signage on carbon/wildlife benefits.
  • Hung Shui Kiu New Town: Northern Metropolis first development phase mandates 90% native species in all public green spaces. Becomes model for subsequent phases.
  • Corporate Campus Competitions: Award for best native species landscaping among Science Park, Cyberport, HKIA tenants. Winner gets carbon offset certification and publicity.
  • Country Park "Carbon Zones": Dedicate 100ha each in Tai Mo Shan, Plover Cove, Lantau for intensive native reforestation. Annual carbon accumulation monitoring published.
