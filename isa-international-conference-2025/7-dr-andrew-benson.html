üìãPresentation Abstract

A long history of research and experience has informed current best practices on the timing and methods used during branch pruning. However, comparatively little is known about root pruning.

To improve our understanding, three roots from each of 32 mature Platanus √ó acerifolia trees were selected and severed in October 2020 and April 2021, representing southern hemisphere spring and autumn pruning timings, respectively.

Roots were pruned internodally (leaving a stub) or at a junction (to a side root). Three years after these treatments were imposed, pruned roots were re-exposed using an air cultivation tool, excised, and root regrowth responses were recorded.

Roots were then dissected and the area of discoloured/dysfunctional wood around the pruning cut measured. This presentation explores the results of the experiment, including how the growth of new roots produced following pruning was affected by the position of the pruning cuts, and how the season the roots were cut affected the discoloured area of the dissected roots.

Spring Hypotheses:

Finding 1: Diameter of New Roots

üìè Result: No Significant Difference

New root diameter ranged from 1-3mm across all treatments, appearing as "beardy" or "fleshy" non-lignified roots.

Comparison factors tested:

  • Internode vs. Junction: Not significant
  • Spring vs. Autumn: Not significant
  • Interaction effects: Not significant

üí° Interpretation: Regardless of where or when roots were cut, the new roots that regrew were consistently small-diameter feeder roots. This consistency is actually quite interesting as a non-significant result‚Äîit suggests root pruning timing and location don't affect the type of roots produced, only the quantity and distribution.

Finding 2: Number of New Roots ‚≠ê

üìà Result: SIGNIFICANT DIFFERENCE by Location

INTERNODE (Stub)
Significantly MORE roots

‚úì Higher new root production

JUNCTION (Reduction)
Significantly FEWER roots

Lower new root production

üå± Comparison to Branch Pruning

This response mirrors what happens with branch pruning:

  • Topped branch (stub): Produces ring of epicormic shoots from cambium
  • Reduction cut (to side branch): Side branch assumes terminal role, fewer epicormic shoots
  • Root response: Stubs (internode) produce significantly more new roots than reduction cuts (junction)

ü§î Possible Explanations

Hypothesis 1: Hydraulic Compensation

Junction cuts: The remaining side root "wakes up" with increased hydraulic activity to compensate for lost capacity.

Internode cuts: The stub has no nearby "friend" to help, so it must grow many new roots if it wants water.

Hypothesis 2: Hormonal Control

Plant hormones (cytokinins, etc.) from upstream portions of junction-cut roots may suppress new root growth when they reach the pruning cut.

Note: Not tested in this study; further research needed.

Finding 4: Hydraulic Capacity Recovery ‚≠ê

üíß CSA Regrowth Percent (Crude Proxy for Hydraulic Recovery)

By summing the cross-sectional areas of all new roots and expressing it as a percentage of the original parent root's cross-sectional area, we get an estimate of how much water-conducting capacity has been recovered.

üèÜ Result: Internode cuts recovered significantly MORE hydraulic capacity than junction cuts

Why Does This Matter?

Higher Recovery = Better

More cross-sectional area means:

  • Greater ability to move water from soil
  • Faster tree recovery from pruning
  • Better support for canopy demands

Internode Advantage

Because internode cuts produced more new roots (Finding 2), the cumulative cross-sectional area was also greater, even though individual root diameters were the same (Finding 1).

Supporting the Previous Findings

This result reinforces Finding 2:

  • Internode roots ‚Üí More new roots ‚Üí More total CSA ‚Üí More hydraulic recovery
  • Junction roots ‚Üí Fewer new roots ‚Üí Less total CSA ‚Üí Side root compensates

üéØ Key Finding: Roots pruned in AUTUMN had significantly LESS internal discoloration than those pruned in SPRING

ü§î Possible Explanations (Hypotheses)

Hypothesis 1: Soil Temperature & Fungal Activity

Initial thought: Cold winter soil temperatures might completely suppress fungal activity.

Expert consultation: Wood decay experts advised that cold doesn't stop fungi, but it may slow them down.

Christchurch winters: Very cold with significant frost and snow.

Interpretation: Slower fungal colonization in autumn-pruned roots may have given trees more time to compartmentalize effectively, resulting in less discoloration three years later.

Hypothesis 2: Seasonal Carbohydrate Flux

Deciduous tree physiology: London Plane trees move carbohydrates seasonally.

Autumn scenario:

  • Trees losing leaves
  • Carbohydrates moving downward for storage
  • Energy immediately available at root pruning site
  • Trees can quickly mobilize defenses (compartmentalization)

Spring scenario:

  • Carbohydrates moving upward to support leaf growth
  • Energy not immediately available at root pruning site
  • Compromised ability to compartmentalize quickly
  • Fungi have more time to colonize before defenses activate

Result: Autumn-pruned roots had energy reserves in the right place at the right time.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note

These are hypotheses only. Neither was directly tested in this experiment. Further research is needed to confirm the mechanisms behind seasonal differences in compartmentalization success.

Finding 6: Aspect Ratio

üìè Aspect Ratio and Internal Discoloration

Aspect Ratio Definition: The ratio of the side root diameter to the removed root diameter (for junction cuts).

Range tested: 0.3 to 1.8 (similar to oak studies by Ed Gilman and Jason Grabosky)

üéØ Result: NO SIGNIFICANT RELATIONSHIP between aspect ratio and internal discoloration

Why This Matters

For branch pruning: Aspect ratio is critical. A ratio >2:1 often leads to poor compartmentalization.

For root pruning: Within the range tested (0.3-1.8), aspect ratio didn't significantly affect discoloration.

Practical implication: When making reduction cuts on roots, arborists don't need to worry as much about precise aspect ratio targets (within this range) as they do with branches.

Supporting Research

Ed Gilman and Jason Grabosky's work on oak species found similar results with a comparable aspect ratio range, also showing non-significant relationships to decay.

üí° Interpretation: Roots and branches may compartmentalize differently. The aspect ratio "rule" that applies to aerial pruning may not apply underground‚Äîat least not within the ranges tested here.

Recommendation 1: When to Prune Roots

üçÇ AUTUMN PRUNING

Where possible, root pruning should be undertaken in AUTUMN to:

  • ‚úì Minimize the area of dysfunctional wood inside the root
  • ‚úì Minimize new root regrowth

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Caveat

It's not always possible to coordinate the timing of root pruning with seasonal schedules. Emergency work, construction deadlines, and other factors may dictate timing. However, when you have flexibility, autumn offers advantages.

Southern vs Northern Hemisphere

This study was in New Zealand (Southern Hemisphere):

  • Autumn = April
  • Spring = October

Northern Hemisphere equivalent:

  • Autumn = September/October
  • Spring = March/April

Adjust timing based on your local climate and deciduous tree senescence patterns.

Recommendation 2: Minimizing Root Regrowth

‚úÇÔ∏è TO MINIMIZE NEW ROOT GROWTH

Prune back to a side root (JUNCTION CUT / REDUCTION CUT)

When Would You Want to Minimize Regrowth?

üö∞ Scenario 1: Sewer/Pipe Conflicts

Tree roots have invaded a sewer pipe or drainage system. You want to:

  • Clear the pipe
  • Cut the roots
  • Prevent the tree from putting its roots back into the pipe

Solution: Make reduction cuts back to side roots to minimize regrowth at that specific location.

üèóÔ∏è Scenario 2: Permanent Infrastructure

Root pruning is required for permanent infrastructure installation (foundations, utilities, hardscaping) where future root conflicts are undesirable.

Solution: Reduction cuts will limit regrowth while maintaining the side root's function.

How to Execute

Reduction Cut to Side Root
Similar to Branch Pruning

Identify a suitable side root and make a clean cut just beyond the branch collar, similar to proper branch reduction techniques.

‚ö†Ô∏è Note on Aspect Ratio

Unlike branch pruning, aspect ratio (within the range of 0.3-1.8 tested) did not significantly affect internal discoloration. You don't need to be as strict about this measurement for roots as you would for branches.

Recommendation 3: Maximizing Root Regrowth

üå± TO MAXIMIZE NEW ROOT GROWTH

Leave a stub (INTERNODE CUT)

When Would You Want to Maximize Regrowth?

üöö Scenario 1: Tree Transplanting

When moving a tree, you inevitably sever roots. To help the tree recover quickly:

  • Leave root stubs rather than clean cuts to junctions
  • Stubs will produce more new feeder roots
  • Greater hydraulic capacity recovery
  • Faster establishment in new location

‚è≥ Scenario 2: Temporary Conflicts

Emergency underground work or temporary utility installation where you want the tree to make a quick recovery:

  • Leave stubs to maximize regrowth
  • Backfill with good quality soil
  • Tree will aggressively regrow roots

Expected Response

Based on study results:

  • Significantly more new roots than reduction cuts
  • Most roots emerge within 10mm of the cut
  • Ring of callus formation around wound
  • Greater total cross-sectional area recovery
  • Roots preferentially grow in lower quadrants (if soil moisture is favorable there)

Best Practices

  • Make clean cuts with sharp tools
  • Cut perpendicular to root direction
  • Backfill with quality soil (preferably native to site)
  • Moisten soil to encourage root initiation
  • Consider timing: autumn may still be preferable to minimize decay

Summary: Decision Tree for Root Pruning

üå≥ Quick Reference Guide

STEP 1: Determine Your Goal

üéØ Goal A: Minimize Internal Decay

‚Üí Prune in AUTUMN

Less discoloration, better compartmentalization

üéØ Goal B: Minimize New Root Growth

‚Üí Make REDUCTION CUTS to side roots

Use for: Sewer conflicts, permanent infrastructure

üéØ Goal C: Maximize New Root Growth

‚Üí Leave STUBS (internode cuts)

Use for: Transplanting, temporary conflicts, quick recovery

üí° Can You Combine These?

YES! For optimal results, consider combining recommendations:

  • Transplanting in autumn + leave stubs = Minimal decay + maximum regrowth
  • Sewer clearing in autumn + reduction cuts = Minimal decay + minimal regrowth
