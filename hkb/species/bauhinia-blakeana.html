Bauhinia blakeana

Hong Kong Orchid Tree 洋紫荊 · 白花洋紫荊

Morphological Description 形態描述

A medium-sized semi-deciduous tree reaching 8–15 m in height, with a spreading, rounded crown and a short, often gnarled trunk. The bark is grey-brown, smooth when young, becoming slightly fissured with age. Branches are slender, drooping, and pubescent when young. The distinctive bilobed leaves are 7–15 cm across, coriaceous, with a characteristic cleft extending one-third to one-half of the lamina length, giving each leaf the appearance of a camel's hoof—the genus name honours the Bauhin brothers, sixteenth-century twin botanists.

The large, fragrant flowers are 10–15 cm across with five oblanceolate petals in shades of purple-red to deep magenta, darkening with age, often with darker veins radiating from the base. The petals overlap slightly, and the upper petal is conspicuously broader and more richly coloured. Five fertile stamens and a long, curved style are present, but the pollen is sterile and no viable seed is set. Flowers are borne in terminal or axillary racemes of 6–12, appearing primarily from late October through April.

This sterile hybrid—thought to be a natural cross between B. purpurea and B. variegata—was first discovered around 1880 near the ruins of a house on the shore of Tolo Harbour by a French missionary, later identified and described by S.T. Dunn in 1908. It became Hong Kong's official floral emblem in 1965 and appears on the flag and coat of arms of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region. Being completely sterile, it reproduces only by vegetative means (cuttings, air-layering) and is widely planted as a street and park tree throughout the territory.

Distribution & Habitat 分佈與生境

Bauhinia blakeana is not known from the wild in any natural habitat. All existing specimens derive from the original clone discovered in Hong Kong, propagated vegetatively and distributed worldwide as an ornamental. It is now one of the most widely planted tropical and subtropical street trees across southern China, Southeast Asia, India, Hawaii, and parts of tropical Africa and the Americas.

In Hong Kong, it is ubiquitous as an urban landscape tree, extensively planted along major roadways such as Nathan Road (彌敦道), in Victoria Park, Kowloon Park, the Kadoorie Estate, and across university campuses. It tolerates the heat island effect of dense urban areas, moderate air pollution, and compacted, alkaline soils, though it performs best in deep, well-drained, fertile soils with regular moisture.

The original tree at Pokfulam is believed to have perished, but a successor was planted near the site. A heritage specimen is maintained in the Hong Kong Zoological and Botanical Gardens.
