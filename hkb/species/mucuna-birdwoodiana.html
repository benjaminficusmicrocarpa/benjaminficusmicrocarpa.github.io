Morphological Description 形態描述

A massive evergreen woody liana (climbing vine) that can reach 20–30 m in length, with stems exceeding 30 cm in diameter in mature specimens. The bark is grey-brown, rough, and deeply furrowed on older stems, exuding a dark red sap when cut—hence the vernacular name "chicken-blood vine" (雞血藤). Young shoots are densely covered with short, appressed, tawny hairs. The trifoliate compound leaves have leathery, glossy, broadly elliptic leaflets 8–15 cm long and 4–8 cm wide, with an obtuse to rounded apex and a cuneate base, glabrous above, sparsely pubescent below.

The extraordinary cauliflorous inflorescences emerge directly from old woody stems, bearing dense, pendulous racemes 20–40 cm long with 20–50 individual flowers. Each flower is papilionaceous, 3–5 cm long, and remarkably resembles a small bird in flight—the standard petal forming the "body," the wings the outstretched "wings," and the keel the "tail"—hence the celebrated Chinese name 禾雀花 ("rice-sparrow flower"). The flowers are creamy white to pale green, sometimes tinged pink or yellowish, with a faintly sweet fragrance. Pollination is accomplished by carpenter bees and other large-bodied Hymenoptera.

The fruit is a large, oblong, woody legume 10–20 cm long, constricted between the 2–4 seeds, densely covered with irritant, golden-brown, urticating hairs. Seeds are flattened-ovoid, 2–3 cm across, dark brown to black, with a prominent hilum. The species is endemic to southern China (Guangdong, Guangxi, Fujian, Yunnan) and northern Vietnam. In Hong Kong, it is found in lowland to mid-elevation ravine forests, particularly at Tai Po Kau, Shing Mun, and Kadoorie Farm.

Distribution & Habitat 分佈與生境

Mucuna birdwoodiana is a characteristic species of mature, secondary lowland and ravine forests in southern China. It requires a supporting canopy structure to climb into the upper forest stratum, and is therefore an indicator of relatively undisturbed, well-developed forest ecosystems.

In Hong Kong, the most celebrated populations occur at Tai Po Kau Nature Reserve (大埔滘自然護理區), where mature lianas of impressive girth can be observed draping from tall Machilus and Castanopsis canopy trees. Significant populations also persist in the Shing Mun (城門) catchment forest, on the lower slopes of Kadoorie Farm and Botanic Garden (嘉道理農場暨植物園), in Tai Lam Country Park (大欖郊野公園), and scattered through the hillside woodlands of the Sai Kung Peninsula.

The species favours deep, moist, humus-rich soils in sheltered ravines and valley bottoms at 50–500 m elevation. It tolerates moderate shade in the juvenile stage but requires canopy access (full sunlight) for prolific flowering. The flowering display in March–April draws significant public attention, with the Tai Po Kau "sparrow-flower trail" being a popular seasonal walk.
