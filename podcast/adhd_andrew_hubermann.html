üíä Treatment Approaches

  • üß¨ Ritalin (Methylphenidate) Chemistry: Blocks dopamine reuptake transporters, increasing synaptic dopamine concentration in prefrontal cortex and striatum.

    Biology: Enhances signal-to-noise ratio in attention networks by strengthening task-relevant neural firing while suppressing background activity.

    "Ritalin, also called methylphenidate, is very similar to amphetamine speed... they're structurally and chemically very similar." - Huberman
    :     Similar to amphetamine/speed
  • ‚ö° Adderall Chemistry: Combination of four amphetamine salts that reverse dopamine and norepinephrine transporters, causing massive neurotransmitter release.

    Biology: Creates artificial focus by flooding attention circuits with dopamine, allowing task networks to dominate over default mode networks.

    "Adderall is basically a combination of amphetamine and dextro amphetamine... their net effect in the brain and body is essentially the same as street stimulants." - Huberman
    :     Amphetamine + dextroamphetamine combination
  • üò¥ Modafinil/Armodafinil Chemistry: Weak dopamine reuptake inhibitor with additional effects on histamine, norepinephrine, and orexin systems.

    Biology: Promotes wakefulness and focus through multiple pathways, less addictive than traditional stimulants but still increases dopaminergic signaling.

    "Unlike Ritalin and Adderall, Modafinil and armodafinil are weak dopamine reuptake inhibitors and that's how they lead to increases in dopamine." - Huberman
    :     Weak dopamine reuptake inhibitors
  • üèÉ Behavioral Training Mechanism: Strengthens prefrontal cortex connections through repeated activation of attention circuits during challenging tasks.

    Biology: Promotes neuroplasticity in task-directed networks, teaching the brain to coordinate default mode and attention networks properly.

    "Taking stimulants as a child... allows those children to learn what focus is and to sort of follow or enter that tunnel of focus." - Huberman
    :     Focus exercises, visual attention tasks
  • üêü Omega-3 Fatty Acids Chemistry: DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) and EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) integrate into neuronal membranes, affecting membrane fluidity and neurotransmitter function.

    :     300mg+ DHA daily

Evidence-based techniques to enhance attention and reduce distractibility

Visual Focus Training

17-minute exercise: Practice switching between focused and panoramic vision to reduce "attentional blinks" and improve sustained attention. This single session can provide near-permanent improvements in focus ability.

Protocol: Alternate between tunnel vision (focusing on a single point) and panoramic vision (wide peripheral awareness) for structured intervals.
üëÅÔ∏è‚Äçüó®Ô∏è

Blink Control

Conscious blinking: Control your blink rate while focusing on objects at different distances. Blinking affects time perception and attention through dopamine-mediated mechanisms.

Science: Blink rate is controlled by dopamine and directly influences your perception of time intervals and attention spans.
üèÉ‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è

Movement First

Pre-focus activity: Do physical movement before focus tasks to help eliminate restlessness and enhance ability to sit still and concentrate.

Application: Even a few minutes of physical activity can significantly improve subsequent focus performance in both children and adults.
üì±

Limit Screen Time

Digital boundaries: Limit smartphone use to 60 mins/day (teens) or 2 hours/day (adults) to preserve natural attention spans and prevent ADHD-like symptoms.

Impact: Excessive smartphone use creates rapid context switching that trains the brain for distractibility and reduces sustained attention capacity.
