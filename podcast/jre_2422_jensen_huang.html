Jensen Huang's Journey

Born in Taiwan üáπüáº

Early 1960s

Born in Taiwan, moved to Thailand where his father worked as a chemical and instrumentation engineer at an oil refinery.

Sent to America at Age 9 üéí

1973-1974

During political unrest in Thailand, parents sent Jensen and his brother to Tacoma, Washington to live with an uncle they'd never met. Enrolled at Oneida Baptist Institute in Clark County, Kentucky - the poorest county in America.

"My first thought was, gosh, there are a lot of cigarette butts on the ground. A hundred percent of the kids smoked... My roommate was seventeen years old, just turned seventeen. And he was jacked... he had all this tape all over his body. Turned out he was in a knife fight."

Life in Rural Kentucky üè´

1973-1975

Youngest student in a tough boarding school. Cleaned toilets for 100 boys as his chore. Brother worked tobacco farms. Tried smoking at age 9, chose popsicles instead. Communicated with parents via monthly cassette tape recordings sent by mail.

"I was nine years old. I was cleaning toilets for a dorm of a hundred boys. I cleaned more bathrooms than anybody."

Parents Arrive in America üë®‚Äçüë©‚Äçüë¶

1975

Parents came to the US in their late 30s with only suitcases and pocket money. Father found job through newspaper classifieds as consulting engineer. Mother worked as a maid. Family started from absolute scratch.

"My parents are incredible... they left everything behind and all they had was their suitcase and the money that they had in their pocket when they came to the United States. This is the American dream. I'm the first generation of the American dream."

Engineering Education üéì

1980-1984

Graduated in 1984 at the very beginning of the PC Revolution and microprocessor era - perfect timing for what would become his life's work.

NVIDIA Founded üöÄ

1993

Co-founded NVIDIA with mission to create new type of computing for problems normal computers couldn't solve. Initial challenge: the applications didn't exist yet because the technology didn't exist. Classic chicken-and-egg problem.

"We had a mission statement for a company that has no chance of success... If we created this thing that can solve problems, you actually have to go create the problem."

The Sega Partnership ‚ö°

1993-1995

Traveled to Japan, convinced Sega to partner: NVIDIA would build chip for Sega's game console in exchange for Sega porting games to PC and funding. This initial success nearly destroyed the company when their technology approach proved fundamentally flawed.

Near-Death Experience üíÄ

1995

Discovered all three major technology choices were wrong: inverse vs forward texture mapping, curved vs flat surfaces, sorted vs Z-buffer architecture. Company running out of money. Had to tell Sega CEO the technology didn't work and asked to be released from contract while still needing the $5M payment.

"I was 33 years old, I still had acne... I said, Listen, I've got some bad news for you. First, the technology that we promised you doesn't work. And second, we shouldn't finish your contract... And third, even though you're gonna let me out of the contract, I still need the money. Because if you didn't give me the money, we'd vaporize overnight."

The $5M Miracle üôè

1995

Sega CEO Shoichiro Irimajiri (former Honda USA CEO) invested $5M despite zero probability of return, simply because "Jensen was a young man he liked." This kept NVIDIA alive. Today that stake would be worth ~$1 trillion, but Sega sold at $300M IPO valuation.

"If you invested that $5 million in us, it is most likely to be lost. But if you didn't invest that money, we'd be out of business and we would have no chance."

Learning from Textbooks üìö

1995

With $200 in pocket, bought three $60 Silicon Graphics textbooks. Gave them to architects: "Read that and let's go save the company." Team learned best-known methods but reimplemented from first principles, creating modern 3D graphics architecture.

The Emulator Gamble üé∞

1996-1997

Company down to fumes. Took half remaining cash (~$500K) to buy IKOS emulator machine - from a company going out of business. Used it to test chip design without expensive fab runs. Convinced TSMC's Morris Chang to go straight to production without testing physical chips first - unprecedented in industry.

"Nobody has ever taped out a chip that worked the first time. And nobody starts production without looking at it... But I knew that if I didn't start the production, I'd be out of business anyways."

RIVA 128 Success üéØ

1997

Chip worked perfectly on first production run. Revolutionary performance/cost ratio for 3D gaming. NVIDIA became fastest-growing tech company in history from $0 to $1B. The emulator methodology they developed to save the company is now standard industry practice worldwide.

"That methodology that we developed to save the company is used throughout the world today."

The CUDA Revolution üî¨

2005-2006

Added CUDA to graphics chips, doubling costs with zero immediate revenue. Stock price crashed from $12B to $2-3B valuation. Board supported the vision despite market not understanding. This "wrong" decision enabled entire AI revolution 7 years later.

AlexNet Breakthrough üß†

2012

Geoffrey Hinton's students (Ilya Sutskever, Alex Krizhevsky) trained AlexNet neural network using two GTX 580 graphics cards in SLI configuration - the same setup Joe Rogan used for Quake. This was the "Big Bang of modern AI" and proved NVIDIA's GPU architecture was perfect for deep learning.

"That GTX 580 SLI was the revolutionary computer that put deep learning on the map. That was 2018 and you were using it to play Quake... That was the moment. That was the big bang of modern AI."

First DGX to OpenAI üéÅ

2016

Built DGX-1 supercomputer ($300K, cost billions to develop). Announced at GTC to complete silence - nobody wanted it. Elon Musk was only customer, for his non-profit OpenAI. Jensen personally delivered first unit to San Francisco office with Ilya Sutskever and team present.

"I had no purchase orders. Not one. Nobody wanted to buy it. Nobody wanted to be part of it. Except for Elon... I just spent a few billion dollars building this thing, cost three hundred thousand dollars and the chances of a nonprofit being able to pay for this thing is approximate zero."

DGX Spark to Elon üì¶

2024-2025

Nine years after DGX-1, delivered DGX Spark to Elon at SpaceX: same 1 petaflop performance, size of small book, $4,000 instead of $300,000. Perfect demonstration of Moore's Law on steroids.

Longest-Running Tech CEO üëë

Present (33 years)

Still leads NVIDIA as longest-running tech CEO in the world. Works 7 days/week, every waking moment. Reads thousands of emails daily. Maintains mindset of being "30 days from going out of business" despite multi-trillion dollar valuation. Both children work at NVIDIA.

"I have a greater drive from not wanting to fail than the drive of wanting to succeed... I'm not ambitious... I just want to stay alive, Joe. I want the company to thrive... The feeling I'm not having this morning is no different than the feeling I had this morning when I woke up, that you're going to be out of business soon."

+

+

Just as cybersecurity works through global collaboration despite constant attacks, AI safety will function similarly. Your AI defends against their AI.

"Your AI is conscious and my AI is conscious... maybe your AI wants to do something surprising. My AI is so smart that it won't be surprising to my AI. It's much more like cybersecurity... We all work together as one. The moment something has been breached, it is shared by everybody."

  • ‚Ä¢ Constant cyber attacks happening right now globally

  • ‚Ä¢ Cybersecurity community shares threats instantly

"If it's like life form... all life forms don't agree. My life form is going to want to be the super life form. Now we have disagreeing life forms, we're back again to where we are."

Universal Basic Income: Both Scenarios Can't Exist

+

Huang points out logical inconsistency in UBI debate:

üí∞ Scenario A: Universal Wealth
