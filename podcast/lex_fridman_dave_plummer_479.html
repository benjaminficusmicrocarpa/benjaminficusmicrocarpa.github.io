Early Computing Adventures

"I rode my bike in 5th or 6th grade. So I was about 11 to the local Radio Shack and you know, they had the standard component stereo systems and everything else Radio Shack had, but they had a stack of boxes that was labeled computer."

Dave's first encounter with computing was serendipitous. Radio Shack employees hadn't even set up their new TRS-80 Model 1 yet, so young Dave volunteered to do it himself.

TRS-80 Model 1 Specifications:
‚Ä¢ CPU: Z80 @ 1.77 MHz
‚Ä¢ RAM: 4KB (Level 1 BASIC)
‚Ä¢ Storage: Cassette tape
‚Ä¢ Display: 12" black & white monitor
‚Ä¢ Price: $600 (1977)

The Challenge: Tandy used identical 5-pin DIN connectors for power, video, and cassette - plug them wrong and you'd destroy the machine!

The Discovery: Dave tried typing English commands into BASIC. Commands like print 2+2 worked perfectly, but complex English sentences didn't translate to code.

"The first thing I did was overheat the floppy drive on it, which is unfortunate because it wasn't a warranty machine."

Without a working floppy drive, Dave was forced to use cassette storage and learned machine language the hard way.

6502 Machine Language Challenges:
‚Ä¢ No assembler - entering opcodes in hex
‚Ä¢ No code relocation - sequential programming only
‚Ä¢ Adding code meant manual jump tables
‚Ä¢ First project: Galaga clone (now lost)

The Tragic Loss: Dave accidentally copied a blank floppy onto his data floppy, destroying weeks of work on his first major program.

"There was a huge amount of guilt because you destroy several weeks of work and you know, it was because you rushed or you did something stupid, or you made an unwise choice."

Why Commodore 64? Dave considers it the greatest computer ever built due to its massive influence - the sales numbers are "hard to believe" even by medium estimates.

"There's no moment when I dropped out. You just go less and less and less until you realize it's gonna be embarrassing if I show up."

Dave's high school dropout wasn't a dramatic decision - it was a gradual fade that many can relate to.

The Gas Dip Incident: Working night shift at 7-Eleven, Dave had to measure gasoline levels using a long wooden stick. In -40¬∞F weather, he dropped the stick and regrabbed it, getting "about a thousand splinters" in his hands.

"I realized I don't want to do this for a whole life. I knew that... That next time I'm going to do it differently. And then how ludicrous that is hits me about three seconds later."

The Comeback: At age 21, Dave approached his high school principal asking to return. The principal initially refused due to age and capacity, but Dave's persistence paid off.

Recovery Strategy:
‚Ä¢ Convinced principal with logical argument
‚Ä¢ Completed 3-4 missing classes
‚Ä¢ Proceeded to university computer science
‚Ä¢ Learned to "do it for me" - intrinsic motivation

Task Manager: The Legend

The 87KB Masterpiece

"I started writing it at home and I got kind of the basics up and running... when I brought it in house, then I was able to call things like NtQuerySystemInformation or NtQueryProcessInformation and get the real answers very quickly."

Technical Specs

  • ‚Ä¢ Original size: 87KB
  • ‚Ä¢ No C runtime linking
  • ‚Ä¢ Manual object constructors
  • ‚Ä¢ Multi-threaded for reliability
  • ‚Ä¢ Hamming code-inspired optimization

Usage Statistics

  • ‚Ä¢ ~2 billion users monthly
  • ‚Ä¢ Shipped in NT4 (1996)
  • ‚Ä¢ Still largely original code
  • ‚Ä¢ Works on 32+ CPU systems
  • ‚Ä¢ Scales to modern hardware

"You don't assume much. Right. If you're going to call the shell to run an app, well, that could be a network path that's on a TCP IP share that takes 90 seconds to time out. So anytime you do any kind of API call like that, that could take time, you're going to wind up doing it on a separate thread."

Reliability Design Principles:
1. Never assume API calls will complete quickly
2. Use separate threads for potentially blocking operations
3. Implement custom Hamming code-like dirty bit tracking
4. Optimize for minimal repainting (cell-level updates)
5. Prioritize resizing capability over feature bloat

The Resize Obsession: Dave's biggest personal goal was making Task Manager resize smoothly to any size - a seemingly simple feature that required careful architectural planning.

Modern Evolution: Today's Task Manager is 4MB instead of 87KB, but the core reliability remains from Dave's original architecture.

Why Rebooting Fixes Everything

Two major factors make rebooting so effective:

Understanding Autism in Tech

"The fundamental theory of thought for autism is called monotropism. And basically what that means is that my brain does one thing. It does it very intensely, and then when it's done, I can move on and do something else."

Aspect Challenge Strength Strategy
Focus Hard to acquire initially (ADHD component) Incredible depth once locked in Noise-canceling headphones, minimize distractions
Communication Missing social cues, literal interpretation Direct, honest feedback Explicit communication, written confirmation
Problem Solving Difficulty with ambiguous requirements Deep technical analysis, pattern recognition Clear specifications, iterative feedback
Career Traditional interviews may not showcase abilities Portfolio-driven work speaks for itself "Sell what you can do, not yourself"

"I will go home later and replay all of the moments where I had choices to make and probably the most uncomfortable ones first to find out what did I do wrong in that moment? What did I miss? What was the other person thinking?"

Post-Processing Algorithm:
1. Replay social interaction moments
2. Focus on uncomfortable/choice points
3. Analyze: What did I miss?
4. Consider: What was the other person thinking?
5. Plan: How to improve next time
6. Decide: Do I need to follow up?

Real Example: Dave didn't thank craftsmen who restored his 1969 car. Ten years later, he realized the oversight and sent an email to the shop owner.

"I sent an email to the manager... And he wrote back, he's like, I've thought of that moment often."

"Everything you do in a social interaction, from waving my hands to taking facial expressions, to tone of voice to posture, it's a huge contrivance and it's work. It comes natural to most people... But for somebody on the spectrum, you've got to fake it all."
