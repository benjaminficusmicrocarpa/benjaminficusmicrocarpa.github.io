📋 Sermon Overview

Central Theme: The invitation to follow Jesus involves a complete overhaul of our thinking, values, and behaviours. It's counterintuitive and seems risky. Through the disciples' misguided ambition and Luke's Beatitudes, Pastor Brett reveals how Jesus redefines greatness — not through power, comfort, success, and recognition, but through servanthood, sacrifice, endurance, and faithfulness. This upside-down kingdom is not a burden; it is the path to freedom.

Series Context: This is the fourth sermon in "The Way" — a Lent series tracing the life and teaching of Jesus as we prepare for Easter. Week 1: Jesus called ordinary people. Week 2: His temptation in the wilderness. Week 3: Mercy, not sacrifice. This week: the upside-down kingdom — how Jesus redefines greatness and calls us to a counter-cultural life.

🌟 Main Takeaways

  1. The early church "turned the world upside down" — Paul and Silas were described as disruptive to the status quo. Following Jesus is not a moral upgrade; it's a complete life reversal.
  2. Even the disciples misunderstood greatness — James and John asked for the top seats in the kingdom. The other ten were indignant — not because the request was wrong, but because they wanted those seats too.
  3. Jesus redefines greatness as servanthood — "Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant." It's an entirely different org chart.
  4. A new covenant, a new command, a new movement — Jesus ushered in grace over law, love over transaction, and the church as a counter-cultural community.
  5. The Beatitudes contrast two kingdoms — Woe to those who live for power, comfort, success, and recognition. Blessed are those who embrace weakness, sacrifice, grief, and rejection for Christ.
  6. The danger of "now" — Three of the four "woes" emphasise the present: "already received," "well fed now," "laugh now." Building your life on what is temporary leads to regret.
  7. Jesus is not just an example — He equips — If Jesus is only an example, His commands become a crushing burden. But He dwells in us, giving new capacity, new motivation, new strength.

🔄 Turning the World Upside Down

Before they were called Christians, followers of Jesus were known as people of the way — drawing from Jesus' claim: "I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me." That radical, exclusive claim provided the moniker for the early church.

Paul and Silas arrived in Thessalonica and spent three Sabbaths reasoning with people in the synagogues about who Jesus was and why He came. Some immediately converted. Others were outraged — this was disruptive to their spiritual and political expectations.

📖 Acts 17:6 (ESV)
"These men who have turned the world upside down have come here also."
This label — "turning the world upside down" — was meant as an accusation. But it's an apt description of what Jesus desires for every one of us.

What Jesus desires is not simply a moral upgrade. Not just some theological enlightenment. He wants to turn your life upside down — your loves, passions, desires, priorities, all driven by the world and the flesh, completely inverted. If you've been a Christian for some time, you should be able to look back and see an almost unrecognisable version of yourself.

⚡ Radical Statements from Jesus

📖 John 12:25 (NIV)
"Anyone who loves their life will lose it, while anyone who hates their life in this world will keep it for eternal life."
A call to "hate your life" — not about hating your job or circumstances, but disowning the values this world sets in place for you to pursue.
📖 Luke 6:35 (NIV)
"But love your enemies, do good to them, and lend to them without expecting to get anything back."
So radical. "I don't even really pray for my enemies. You want me to love them? Give money to them and walk away from it?" Jesus goes to extreme measures to get our attention.
📖 Colossians 1:13 (NIV)
"For he has rescued us from the dominion of darkness and brought us into the kingdom of the Son he loves."
There are only two kingdoms. Conversion to faith is a transfer from the world's way of thinking, valuing, acting, and prioritising to a completely new approach to life.

This kind of faith is not a minor change. Not Judaism 2.0. Jesus came to fulfil the law and usher in an entirely new way of thinking. His words are radical, intimidating. They cause us to shrink back. "You want me to forgive 70 times seven? Just on and on? That's unnatural." Exactly. It's supernatural.

👑 James, John, and the Redefinition of Greatness

Among the twelve disciples, there was an inner circle — Peter, James, and John — who got extra access to Jesus: hearing Him pray, watching Him live, observing His ministry up close. And yet, having seen all this, James and John came to Jesus with a remarkable request:

📖 Mark 10:35-37 (NIV)
"Then James and John, the sons of Zebedee, came to him. 'Teacher,' they said, 'we want you to do for us whatever we ask.' 'What do you want me to do for you?' he asked. They replied, 'Let one of us sit at your right and the other at your left in your glory.'"
They'd had a pre-meeting before the meeting. They wanted the vice-president positions in Jesus' kingdom — still thinking of it as a political power structure.

It's easy to look back and think, "Are you kidding me?" But how do we treat Jesus? Most of our prayers are probably more about how He can meet our immediate needs than about intercession for others or worship for who He is. We often treat Him like a genie or a Santa Claus. James and John were just the ones with the courage to say it.

Jesus responded with remarkable patience: "You don't even know what you're asking." He asked if they could drink the cup He would drink — metaphorically speaking of His suffering and death. They overestimated their commitment: "Oh yes, we can." Of course, they would later abandon Him.

😤 The Indignation of the Ten

The other ten disciples overheard and became indignant. But notice: they weren't offended that James and John asked the wrong thing. They were offended that they tried to get a step above them. "You're leaving us out? You think you're better than us?" It reveals that none of the disciples truly understood Jesus' new kingdom.

🔄 "Not So with You"

📖 Mark 10:42-45 (NIV)
"Jesus called them together and said, 'You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many.'"
Three words that change everything: "Not so with you." Jesus redefines greatness from the top of the org chart to the bottom — from being served to serving.

Servant and slave. That's not what they aspired to. They wanted to have servants and slaves. But Jesus says greatness is defined in an entirely different way. It is possible to be great in the world's eyes but not in God's. And it is possible to be great in God's eyes but not the world's.

📜 New Covenant, New Command, New Movement

✝️ A New Covenant

Jesus' death and resurrection ushered in a new covenant — a new connection with God. It would no longer be about fastidiously adhering to Old Testament laws. It would be about understanding grace: a concept that doesn't make sense in a worldly view. The world is transactional — you study hard, you get good grades; you work hard, you get a promotion. But grace is not transactional. You get what you don't deserve. You are extended mercy instead.

❤️ A New Command

📖 Mark 12:30-31 (NIV)
"Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength... Love your neighbour as yourself."
A new capacity and desire to express love to God with every fibre of your being, and having sensed God's love for you, to love all people — above you, beside you, below you.

Not just the lovable. Not just those who can love you back or do you a favour. All people. Everyone created in the image of God, with intrinsic value. God calls His church to be the practical expression of that love — emotionally, practically, everywhere, without limits.

⛪ A New Movement

This new command ushered in a new movement: the church — the ecclesia, the "called-out ones." People extricated from the world, gathering together around a common calling, living in a counter-cultural way. For 2,000-plus years, big churches, small churches, cathedrals, home churches, many languages — it may look different, but the message is the same: We believe in Jesus and all that He said and claimed.

⚖️ The Beatitudes: Woe vs. Blessed

Perhaps Jesus' most famous sermon is the Beatitudes — recorded in a longer version in Matthew 5 and a shorter version in Luke 6. (Jesus repeated His sermons — Pastor Brett took great heart from this.) In Luke's account, Jesus makes direct comparisons between a worldly life and this other kingdom, this upside-down success.

⚠️ The Woes: Warning Against Worldly Values

📖 Luke 6:24-26 (NIV)
"But woe to you who are rich, for you have already received your comfort. Woe to you who are well fed now, for you will go hungry. Woe to you who laugh now, for you will mourn and weep. Woe to you when everyone speaks well of you, for that is how their ancestors treated the false prophets."
Jesus is not judging — He is pleading. "I have pity on you if you embrace simply the values of the world. Don't do it." He is preventing regret.

And lest anyone in the room say "Oh, that's not me" — we are all rich. We sit in air conditioning. We're about to go eat lunch. We are in the top five per cent of global wealth. Jesus says: be careful. These are all normal things to pursue. He's not against you doing well. His warning is: if this is what you live for, you'll be disappointed.

Note the emphasis on "now": "You have already received your comfort. You are well fed now. You who laugh now." There's an over-emphasis on the present, with no regard for God's master plan over all of eternity. Our 80-ish years are a tiny dot on the line of eternity — and we lose focus thinking this dot is all that matters.

The Greek word for "laugh" (γελάω / gelaō): It carries the nuance of gloating — relishing in your own victory at the expense of others. Your political party wins and you rub it in. You win a competition and mock the loser. Not simple joy, but self-congratulatory triumph.

✨ The Blessings: The Upside-Down Kingdom

📖 Luke 6:20-23 (NIV)
"Looking at his disciples, he said: 'Blessed are you who are poor, for yours is the kingdom of God. Blessed are you who hunger now, for you will be satisfied. Blessed are you who weep now, for you will laugh. Blessed are you when people hate you, when they exclude you and insult you and reject your name as evil, because of the Son of Man.'"
All the things we work hard to avoid — poverty, hunger, grief, exclusion — and Jesus says there is blessing in them. The upside-down kingdom.

Who wants grief? Insult? Exclusion from the party? Poverty? And yet Jesus says there's blessing in these things you work so hard to avoid.

In the life of God's people, it will be seen, first of all, as a remarkable reversal of values. The people of God will prize what the world finds pitiable and suspect what the world finds desirable.

This doesn't mean you pursue grief or poverty. You don't throw away your money to be poor. But to prize sacrifice, to value humility, to see the presence of God rushing in during suffering — that is the mark of the upside-down kingdom. To be "poor in spirit" (as Matthew will tell us) is to have a humble disposition toward God: "God, I bring you nothing. I have nothing to offer. And yet you love me still."

⚡ Where the Power Comes From

In both Matthew and Luke, Jesus was on a mountainside — where revolutionaries would hide to escape authorities. But He came down to the people. And Luke tells us:

Luke 6:19: "The people all tried to touch him, because power was coming from him and healing them all." — People were touching His robe and being healed. A watershed moment where Jesus' public ministry took off. Power was emanating from Him, as if to say: from this point on, there is a new kingdom.

🤔 Is Jesus Only an Example?

Is Jesus an example to follow? Seems like an easy answer. But if He is only an example, He will be a burden to you — because you won't be able to do it. You won't forgive your enemies over and over. You won't love those who don't love you back. You won't absorb insult without being riled up. At some point you'll either check out ("This Christian life is unattainable") or fake it and feel like an imposter.

Jesus is not only your example. It is through Christ dwelling in you — the one who transferred you from the kingdom of darkness to the kingdom of light — that you receive a new capacity, a new source of strength, a new grounding, a new motivation. He never asks you to do something He doesn't also equip you to do.

🕊️ Freedom, Not Burden

The invitation is to live according to the freedom God offers, with eternity in view. You may not get the respect you deserve in this life. That person may never apologise. But there's a God in heaven who sees every infraction, balances the scales, and meets out judgment — and you can leave room for Him.

I got unfair treatment by God — I got heaven and I didn't deserve it. I like when it works in that direction. And so you can endure, you can persevere, you can be gracious, kind, generous, encouraging — even if it's not reciprocated.

— Pastor Brett

You can live above the comparison trap. You can live beyond the transactional world. You can come before God with open hands. It's an upside-down way of living, and it doesn't come natural — you have to re-correct yourself often. But there is freedom there.

⏳ The Danger of "Now"

In three of the four woes, Jesus emphasises the present tense:

  1. "You have already received your comfort"
  2. "You are well fed now"
  3. "You who laugh now"

The danger is an over-emphasis on the present — what you can see, touch, and count — with no regard for God's master plan over all of eternity. Our lives, however long, represent a tiny dot on the line of eternity. And yet we work and grind and amass, trying to prove to the world that we matter.

The mirage analogy: Remember that thing you really wanted to buy? You thought about it, saved for it, finally got it. How long before the newness wore off? Worldly satisfaction is like a mirage — it appears to be water on the horizon, but the closer you get, the further it moves. You never find the satisfaction you thought you would.

🔑 The Key Distinction

Jesus is not against you doing well. He's not against being powerful, comfortable, successful, or having a good reputation. His warning is this: If this is what you live for, you'll be disappointed. If this is what you build your life around, you will eventually regret it.

You climbed that ladder of success, only to find it was leaning against the wrong wall.

— A common saying referenced by Pastor Brett

The alternative? Live according to the freedom God offers, with eternity in view. This is not all there is. And in that perspective, you find the power to endure, forgive, be generous, and live above the comparison trap.

🎯 Living in the Upside-Down Kingdom

Jesus doesn't just describe a counter-cultural reality — He invites you into it. And He doesn't leave you to figure it out on your own. Through His Spirit dwelling in you, He provides the power to live this way. Here are practical steps for embracing the upside-down kingdom.

🔄
Audit Your Definition of Greatness

How do you define success? Is it titles, income, recognition, followers? Jesus says greatness is serving others. This week, ask: Where am I climbing a ladder that's leaning against the wrong wall?

🤲
Come to God Empty-Handed

To be "poor in spirit" is to approach God without a résumé. Not "God, here's what I bring to the partnership," but "God, I have nothing to offer. It is by your grace alone." Practise this posture in prayer this week.

⏳
Think Beyond "Now"

The world screams: grab it now, enjoy it now, prove yourself now. Jesus says: think in terms of eternity. When you're tempted to chase the immediate, ask: Will this matter in light of eternity?

🕊️
Leave Room for God

That person who hurt you may never apologise. Instead of demanding justice yourself, leave room for a God who sees every infraction and balances the scales. You can endure because you already received the ultimate unfair treatment — heaven, undeserved.

💪
Lean on Christ's Power, Not Your Own

If following Jesus feels impossible, it's because it is — on your own. He's not just an example; He equips. Ask the Holy Spirit for the power to forgive, love, and serve in ways that go beyond your natural ability.

🪞
Identify Your "James and John" Moments

Where do you treat Jesus like a genie — handing Him a wish list instead of worshipping Him for who He is? Shift your prayers from "here's what I need" to "here's who you are" — and watch your perspective change.

🎯 This Week's Challenge

Choose one area where your life looks too similar to the world's values — power, comfort, success, or recognition. Identify one concrete step to live counter-culturally in that area this week. It might be serving someone below you on the org chart, sacrificing comfort for someone else's benefit, celebrating someone else's success instead of comparing, or standing firm in your faith even when it's socially costly. Write it down. Pray over it. And then do it — not in your own strength, but leaning on Christ's power in you.
