When our spiritual lives stagnate, the first warning signs appear in our prayer life. Discover the most powerful prayer ever prayed and how it can revitalize your relationship with God.

The Prayer of Surrender
Jesus modeled the most powerful prayer in the Garden of Gethsemane: "Not my will, but yours be done."

Spirit vs. Flesh

💡 Central Message

A big prayer is not big because of what you pray for or how eloquently you pray—it's big because of your heart. The prayer of surrender ("not my will, but yours be done") has the power to transform your entire spiritual life, but only when you truly wrestle with what you desire and live in obedience to God's will.

"Going a little farther, he fell with his face to the ground and prayed, 'My Father, if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me. Yet not as I will, but as you will.'"
— Matthew 26:39 (NIV)

📊 Sermon Structure

🤲 Part 1: Jesus's Example - Wrestling with God ▼

Key Scripture: Matthew 26:36-39

Jesus went to the Garden of Gethsemane, overcome with sorrow "to the point of death." He prayed all night, pleading with God: "If it is possible, may this cup be taken from me." The "cup" refers to the cup of God's wrath—the full punishment for sin that Jesus would drink on our behalf, leading to separation from the Father.

💡 Key Insight: Jesus didn't just casually say "not my will." He wrestled with God for hours, fully processing what He desired (to avoid separation from the Father) before surrendering. This makes His prayer powerful—not empty words, but genuine surrender after deep wrestling.

😴 Part 2: The Disciples' Failure - Spirit Willing, Flesh Weak ▼

Key Scripture: Matthew 26:40-41

The disciples boldly declared they would die for Jesus, yet they couldn't even stay awake for one hour when He asked them to. Jesus said: "The spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak." This wasn't just about physical exhaustion—it was about the gap between their intentions and their actions.

💡 Key Insight: Most of the disciples weren't present at Jesus's crucifixion. They said they'd follow Him to death, but they couldn't even carry His cross, die alongside Him, or bury Him. When we can't obey God in small things now, what makes us think we'll obey when times get tough?

🙏 Part 3: Praying the Prayer of Surrender ▼

Two Requirements for Genuine Surrender:

  1. "Not My Will" - Truly think about what you desire. Process it. Wrestle with God. Plead and petition. Only then, after genuine wrestling, say "not my will."
  2. "Thy Will Be Done" - Are you already living according to God's will? If you aren't obeying God's small commands now (love your neighbor, steward finances, forgive others), what makes you think you'll obey when the cost is greater?

⚠️ Warning: This is a dangerous prayer. It may cost you what's most important to you—even your life itself. The disciples eventually gave their lives as martyrs because they learned to truly pray this prayer.

💬 Full Sermon Breakdown

🎯 Introduction: When Prayer Becomes Routine ▼

Pastor Kevin begins by acknowledging that this Fresh Start series is perfect for both new believers and longtime Christians who may have lost their initial fire. He emphasizes that when your prayer life stalls or stagnates, the rest of your spiritual life will follow suit.

He shares his personal struggle: "I have never been someone who prays big elaborate prayers, big bold prayers. When I pray, I'm pretty safe. I pray small prayers." He prays for good health and smart physicians instead of miraculous healing; for jobs and promotions instead of divine provision.

"I've always wanted to be someone who can pray big, bold prayers."
— Pastor Kevin

He then shares the story of Ted in Singapore—a man who ministered to disadvantaged youth. When 50 kids showed up instead of 10 for a Christmas party, Ted prayed boldly, believing God would provide. God impressed on him to go fishing, and he caught bucket after bucket of fish miraculously. Pastor Kevin reflects: "If I were in his shoes, my first instinct is not to pray... For Ted, prayer was his first and only option."

💡 Main Point: The Prayer of Surrender ▼

Pastor Kevin introduces the central thesis:

💡 What Makes a Prayer "Big"?

"A big prayer is not a big prayer because you're praying for a miracle. And a big prayer is not a big prayer because you stand up and you pray in a very eloquent way. A big prayer is a big prayer because of your heart."

That heart is captured in the prayer of surrender💡 Prayer of Surrender: A prayer where you genuinely wrestle with God about your desires, fully process what you want and why, and then sincerely say "not my will, but yours be done"—while already living in obedience to God's will in your daily life.. He points to Matthew 26, where Jesus prays in the Garden of Gethsemane before His crucifixion.

🤲 Section 1: Jesus - Wrestling with God ▼

The Setting: Garden of Gethsemane

The word Gethsemane🌿 Gethsemane: Hebrew for "olive press"—a place where olives are crushed and pressed to extract oil. A powerful metaphor for the long, grueling, excruciating prayer Jesus was about to pray. means "olive press"—a fitting metaphor for the crushing, agonizing prayer Jesus was about to pray.

"He took Peter and the two sons of Zebedee along with him, and he began to be sorrowful and troubled. Then he said to them, 'My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death. Stay here and keep watch with me.'"
— Matthew 26:37-38 (NIV)

Why Was Jesus in Anguish?

Pastor Kevin explores what caused Jesus such overwhelming sorrow. He shares a personal analogy: when he and his wife did long distance for 18 months, every taxi ride to the airport brought "a wave of sorrow" because of impending separation. Similarly, Jesus was facing separation—not from the disciples, but from the Father.

The Cup of Wrath

"Going a little farther, he fell with his face to the ground and prayed, 'My Father, if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me. Yet not as I will, but as you will.'"
— Matthew 26:39 (NIV)

The "cup" refers to the cup of wrath☕ Cup of Wrath: According to Psalm 75:7-8, this is "a cup full of foaming wine mixed with spices" that "all the wicked of the earth drink down to its very dregs." It represents the full punishment reserved for the wicked—drinking it to the dregs means experiencing the complete, undiluted punishment, including all the worst parts. mentioned in Psalm 75:7-8. Pastor Kevin explains: "This cup is reserved for us. But Jesus' mission is to drink that cup on our behalf."

💔 The Most Devastating Aspect

"God is a holy God, which means he can't be around sin. So the most devastating aspect of sin in my book is that we are separated from God... For Jesus Christ, who had been intimately connected with God the Father for all of eternity from before creation, this was infinitely more excruciating."

Jesus Wrestled All Night

We learn from verse 40 that Jesus prayed for at least one hour in this first round, then came back a second and third time. "All through the night he was pleading with God... Jesus wrestled with God. He kept praying on and over and over."

"When he says 'not my will,' Jesus knew what he wanted and why he wanted it. I don't think that for many of us, we are the same. We like to throw in this phrase at the end of our prayers because it sounds nice. But we don't truly think about it."
— Pastor Kevin
😴 Section 2: The Disciples - Spirit Willing, Flesh Weak ▼
"Then he returned to his disciples and found them sleeping. 'Couldn't you men keep watch with me for one hour?' he asked Peter. 'Watch and pray so that you will not fall into temptation. The spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak.'"
— Matthew 26:40-41 (NIV)

The Cryptic Warning

Pastor Kevin was initially puzzled: "Why is physical exhaustion a temptation? Why are the disciples being reprimanded in this way? And then what is this cryptic line: 'The spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak'?"

He explains that "flesh" in Scripture refers to the selfish part of us💭 Flesh (Biblical Definition): Not literal physical flesh, but that selfish part of us bent toward self-glory and self-preservation. When Jesus says "the spirit is willing but the flesh is weak," He's pointing out the gap between our good intentions and our actual obedience. bent toward self-glory and self-preservation. But falling asleep doesn't seem selfish—so what's really happening?

Going Back to the Last Supper

Before Gethsemane, at the Last Supper, Jesus predicted all the disciples would fail Him:

"Truly I tell you, this very night, before the rooster crows, you will disown me three times. But Peter declared, 'Even if I have to die with you, I will never disown you.' And all the other disciples said the same."
— Matthew 26:34-35 (NIV)

Pastor Kevin emphasizes the often-overlooked detail: "And all the other disciples said the same." It wasn't just Peter who would fail—all of them would.

😢 The Tragic Reality

"They all had the intention to follow Jesus to the cross. They said in a very spirited way, 'Lord, we will die for you.' And then Jesus gives them a simple and small instruction: 'Stay awake.' And they can't do that. If they can't even stay awake for him, how could they ever stand with him through what he was going to have to go through?"

The Most Tragic Thing

Pastor Kevin shares what he finds most heartbreaking about the crucifixion:

  • Who carried the cross? A random guy named Simon—not a disciple
  • Who died on Jesus's left and right? Two thieves—not disciples
  • Who took His body down and buried Him? Joseph of Arimathea—not a disciple
  • Apart from John, the disciples were nowhere to be seen
"Here they said they would follow him to death... They're not even there. They can't even stay awake. Spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak."
— Pastor Kevin
💭 Application 1: Not My Will - Truly Process Your Desires ▼

Pastor Kevin challenges us to stop saying "not my will" as mere lip service:

💼 Example: Praying for a Job

You apply for a job and pray: "God, please give me that job. But not my will, but yours be done."

But wait—WHY do you want that job?

  • Is it because the company's values align with yours?
  • Would it give you greater fulfillment and belonging?
  • So what you truly want is fulfillment and purpose?
  • Are you lacking that in other areas of your life?

"I'm just running through a line of questionings to get at the heart of what you truly desire."

"When you gloss over the things you want, when you don't really think through what it is your will is, when you say the words 'not my will,' it makes it empty. Powerless."
— Pastor Kevin

⚡ What Makes for a Powerful Prayer of Surrender

"When you have truly, truly thought through the things that you desire. When you truly consider what your heart motivations are, why you want those things, when you have wrestled with God, when you have contended with him for a long time. And then after all of that fighting, all night of prayer, then you say, 'Not my will.' That's when it's powerful. Otherwise, it's just lip service."

✅ Application 2: Thy Will Be Done - Live It Now ▼

Pastor Kevin uses a personal story to illustrate this point:

"My son and I were cooped up at home because of the typhoons... my son was just in a mood. I'd say, 'Kirby, please don't jump off the couch,' and he jumps off the couch... And it got to the point where I said, 'Kirby, you are not listening to daddy.' And he turns around and he says almost immediately, 'I do listen to daddy. I listen to daddy all the time.' And he said it with so much conviction, so much sincerity, he truly believed that he listened well."
— Pastor Kevin

Aren't we the same?

We say: "Thy will be done, Lord, we will follow you. We will listen to wherever you guide us."

God says: "Okay, so sell everything and go follow me." → We say no.

God says: "Go help that guy in the corner." → Too inconvenient.

God says: "Turn the other cheek and not seek revenge." → No.

God says: "Forgive 70 times 70 times." → That's figurative, Lord...

💯 The Hard Truth

"And yet, we still believe that we listen well. We can't be obedient now. What makes us think we can be obedient when the times get tough? What makes us think we will be obedient just because we utter those words in a prayer?"

"Saying thy will be done is meaningless unless you are already living in that way. Otherwise, it's just lip service."

Pastor Albert's Wisdom

When Pastor Kevin first arrived in Hong Kong, he shared his grand vision for young adult ministry with Pastor Albert. Pastor Albert responded with no facial expression, maybe a smirk, and said three words that became foundational to Pastor Kevin's ministry:

"Talk is cheap."
— Pastor Albert

"There's enough talkers in ministry," Pastor Kevin reflects. The same is true in our prayer lives—there are enough people saying "thy will be done" without actually doing God's will.

🎁 Benefits: What This Prayer Brings ▼

1. Greater Open-Handedness

"Open-handed meaning you're not grasping onto your plans. You're not obsessed with your goals in life. If you realize a plan, you're pleased. That's awesome. If you don't realize it, you're also okay."

2. Contentment in All Situations

"You begin living life with a level of contentment that Paul talks about in the book of Philippians, to be content in all situations, whether living in plenty or living in want."

3. Peace and Margin

"Greater peace, less anxiety. Greater margin, less busyness. Greater hope, less despair."

4. Outward Focus on God's Mission

"It actually helps you not fixate on yourself anymore... it's only until we stop fixating on ourselves that we realize, whoa, there's a whole world out there. There's needs elsewhere. And only until you do that do you really see how God is moving around the world, around you. And only then do you see what he wants you to care about."

🎯 Understanding Your Calling

"My definition of calling is pretty simple: It's responding to a need in the world related to the mission of God."

  • Moses was called to rescue Israel (God's mission to bless nations through Israel)
  • Jonah was called to preach to Nineveh (God's mission of compassion to all nations)
  • Paul was called to the Gentiles (God's mission to save all who call on Jesus)

"To see what that is, we have to turn our hearts and our focus outward. The prayer of surrender helps with that."

⚠️ Final Warning

✨ Practical Application

🙏 How to Pray the Prayer of Surrender

This isn't a formula, but a genuine heart posture. Here's how to approach it:

🔍 Step 1: Identify What You Truly Desire ▼

Don't rush past this. Take time to really examine what you want and why.

Questions to ask yourself:

  • What am I really praying for? (Be specific)
  • Why do I want this?
  • What deeper need or desire is driving this request?
  • If God said no to this specific request, what would I truly be losing?
  • Is this about my comfort, my glory, my control—or about God's kingdom?
"When you gloss over the things you want, when you don't really think through what it is your will is, when you say the words 'not my will,' it makes it empty."
— Pastor Kevin
🤲 Step 2: Wrestle with God in Prayer ▼

Prayer isn't about being polite—it's about being honest.

What wrestling looks like:

  • Don't just pray once—pray repeatedly, like Jesus did all night
  • Be brutally honest with God about your fears, desires, and struggles
  • Ask God if there's another way (Jesus did: "if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me")
  • Plead your case—God can handle your emotions and questions
  • Listen for His response in Scripture, through the Holy Spirit, and in wise counsel
"Going a little farther, he fell with his face to the ground and prayed, 'My Father, if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me. Yet not as I will, but as you will.'"
— Matthew 26:39 (NIV)
💭 Step 3: Say "Not My Will" with Full Understanding ▼

Only after wrestling can you truly say "not my will."

This isn't giving up or being passive. It's active trust. It means:

  • I've fully processed what I want
  • I understand why I want it
  • I've wrestled with God about it
  • And now I'm choosing to trust His plan over mine

⚡ The Power of This Prayer

"When you have truly, truly thought through the things that you desire... when you have wrestled with God, when you have contended with him for a long time... then you say, 'Not my will.' That's when it's powerful."

✅ Step 4: Live "Thy Will Be Done" in Daily Obedience ▼

The prayer is meaningless if you're not already obeying God's known will.

Audit your life:

  • Are you loving your neighbor? Or just tolerating people?
  • Are you stewarding your finances according to God's principles?
  • Are you forgiving those who've hurt you? Or holding grudges?
  • Are you serving the poor and marginalized?
  • Are you pursuing holiness in your personal life?
  • Are you generous with your time, talents, and treasure?
"We can't be obedient now. What makes us think we can be obedient when the times get tough?... Saying thy will be done is meaningless unless you are already living in that way."
— Pastor Kevin

Start Small

The disciples couldn't even stay awake. Don't make grandiose promises. Start with small acts of obedience:

  • Help someone today who inconveniences you
  • Forgive someone who doesn't deserve it
  • Give generously when it costs you something
  • Speak truth when it's uncomfortable
  • Choose God's way over your comfort

☕ The Cup of Wrath - Full Context

It is God who judges: He brings one down, he exalts another. In the hand of the Lord is a cup full of foaming wine mixed with spices; he pours it out, and all the wicked of the earth drink it down to its very dregs.
— Psalm 75:7-8 (NIV)

What is the "cup of wrath"?

The cup represents the full punishment that is reserved for those who are wicked—for all of humanity who have sinned. It's not just a sip of judgment; it's drinking the entire cup "down to its very dregs."

What does "to its very dregs" mean?

When you brew something with mixed spices, there's inevitably residue that settles at the bottom—the worst parts. Drinking something "to its dregs" means consuming the entire cup, including all that bitter, unpleasant sediment. It's drinking every last drop of punishment—experiencing the fullness of what we deserve for our sin.

Why was Jesus so anguished about this cup?

This cup was reserved for us, but Jesus' mission was to drink it on our behalf. It wasn't just about physical pain or death—it was about being separated from God the Father. God is holy and cannot be around sin. So when Jesus became sin for us, He experienced the most devastating consequence: separation from the Father with whom He'd been intimately connected for all eternity.

☕ The Gospel in This Cup

Jesus drank the cup of wrath—the full punishment for our sin—down to its very dregs so that we don't have to. He experienced complete separation from God so that we can be reconciled to the Father forever. This is why Jesus prayed so desperately in Gethsemane—He knew exactly what this cup would cost Him.
