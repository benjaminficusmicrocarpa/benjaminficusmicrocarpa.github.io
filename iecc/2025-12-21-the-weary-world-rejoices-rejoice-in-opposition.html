üî≠ The Star of Bethlehem: An Astronomical Investigation

Based on research by Rick Larson's documentary "The Star of Bethlehem"

Problem #1: How did they know a king was born in Israel?

‚ñº

What the wise men saw was not actually a star‚Äîit was what the ancients called a "wandering star," which we now know as planets.

Around 3 BC:

  • Jupiter (the King Planet - largest of all planets)
  • Passed by Regulus (the King Star)
  • Within the constellation Leo (the Lion)
  • In a triple conjunction (passing three times)

Jerusalem was the capital of Judah, which was represented by a lion. So:

The King Planet + circling the King Star + in the constellation of a Lion = A new king born in Israel

For people in Babylon who looked to the stars for messages, this was not far-fetched.

Problem #2: How was it the brightest star they'd ever seen?

‚ñº

About nine months after Caesar's census (around the time Jesus was born):

  • Venus caught up with Jupiter in the night sky
  • The two planets came so close they appeared as one single star to the naked eye
  • The light from both planets amplified each other
  • It became the brightest thing they had ever seen in their lifetime
This was their cue. This is when they knew something had happened. So they began their 5-6 month journey to Jerusalem.

Problem #3: How does a star stop over a location?

‚ñº

Retrograde Motion: Planets don't move in a linear fashion across the night sky. They move in a loopy direction because Earth's orbit is faster or slower than other planets.

Example: Like driving on a highway next to a slower car‚Äîit looks like that car is moving backwards. That's retrograde motion.

When the wise men arrived in Jerusalem around November/December 2 BC:

  • Jupiter was moving across the sky
  • It stopped in retrograde motion
  • Directly over Bethlehem
Computer programs that recreate the ancient night sky confirm this astronomical phenomenon occurred exactly as described.

  1. In Babylon: The wise men see Jupiter circle Regulus three times in the constellation Leo ‚Üí They know a king will be born in Judah
  2. Nine months later: Venus catches up to Jupiter, creating the brightest star ever seen ‚Üí The king is born
  3. 5-6 months journey: They travel from Babylon to Jerusalem
  4. Arrival in Jerusalem: Jupiter moves ahead of them in retrograde motion and stops over Bethlehem
