The Story of Two Banners

A Memorial of Uncommon Valor

The Battle of Iwo Jima in 1945 produced one of the most iconic images in American history: six Marines raising the flag on Mount Suribachi. That photograph, and the bronze statue it inspired, carries an inscription in gold letters: "Uncommon valor was a common virtue" along with the Marine Corps motto "Semper Fidelis" (Always Faithful).

Pastor Rick notes three important elements of this memorial: it took more than one person to raise the pole, the pole was raised high so people could see what was on top, and on top was a flag celebrating victory for a country. These same elements appear in our story from Exodus.

Understanding God's Name: Jehovah Nissi

The Journey of God's Name

YHWH (Yahweh) - The Tetragrammaton

The proper name of God in Hebrew consists of four consonants: Y-H-W-H (◊ô◊î◊ï◊î). This is the personal, covenant name of God revealed to Moses at the burning bush (Exodus 3:14-15). Out of reverence, many Jewish people would not pronounce this name aloud.

Adonai - Lord

As a substitute for the unspoken name YHWH, Jewish readers would say "Adonai" (meaning "Lord" or "Master") when they encountered the four consonants in Scripture. This practice preserved the sanctity of God's personal name.

Jehovah - A Medieval Rendering

Over centuries, scribes combined the consonants of YHWH with the vowels of Adonai, creating a hybrid form. When this was Latinized in medieval times, Y became J and W became V, resulting in "Jehovah." While not the original pronunciation, it became a reverent way to refer to God's covenant name.

LORD (Capitalized) - English Translation

In most English Bibles, when you see "LORD" in all capital letters, it represents the Hebrew name YHWH/Yahweh. This distinguishes it from "Lord" (with lowercase letters), which usually translates Adonai or refers to Jesus as Lord in the New Testament.
