I AM: The Good Shepherd

Jesus declares Himself as the loving Messiah who knows His sheep by name, who lays down His life for them, and who leads them into abundant life.

Overview

üåü Key Message

In John 10, Jesus continues His discourse from being the door to revealing Himself as the Good Shepherd. Using imagery deeply rooted in Old Testament prophecy and the familiar occupation of shepherding, Jesus declares to the religious leaders that He is the loving Messiah God's people had been waiting for.

This message comes during the Festival of Dedication (Hanukkah), a time when Israel remembered pushing out bad leadership and reconsecrating the temple. In this context of longing for good leadership both spiritually and politically, Jesus audaciously claims: "I am the good shepherd."

Main Sermon

Pastor Eric begins by explaining how powerful statements gain their meaning from context. Just like Darth Vader's "I am your father" only has shock value when you know the story leading up to it, Jesus' "I am" statements are rich with meaning when we understand the context.

This passage in John 10 continues from chapter 9, where Jesus addresses religious leaders who have failed as shepherds of God's people. The setting is winter during the Festival of Dedication (Hanukkah), a time commemorating when the Jewish people overthrew bad leadership 200 years prior and reconsecrated the temple after Antiochus Epiphanes desecrated it.

There's an air of expectation‚Äîremembering past deliverance from bad leadership while currently under Roman rule, and internally experiencing spiritual leaders who aren't serving the people well.

When Jesus says "I am the good shepherd," He's implicitly saying the current leaders are not. He compares them to hired hands who don't truly care for the sheep‚Äîthey're in it for personal benefit and flee when danger comes.

Jesus references Ezekiel 34, where God condemns Israel's shepherds: "You eat the fat, you clothe yourselves with the wool, you slaughter the fat ones, but you do not feed the sheep. The weak you have not strengthened, the sick you have not healed, the injured you have not bound up, the strayed you have not brought back, the lost you have not sought."

This is a little bit of an awkward moment. Jesus walks in and says, "I am the good pastor." It's offensive, right? It's a lot offensive. But this is Jesus in love confronting the leaders who weren't doing what they should be doing.

This is a call to anyone entrusted with "sheep"‚Äîwhether a team at work, children, a care group, or aging parents. Good leadership means laying down your life, caring for those who go astray, healing the hurt, and nourishing others, sometimes giving of yourself so they can live.

Being called sheep is offensive at first, but then humbling. Drawing from shepherds-turned-pastors Phillip Keller and W. Phillip Keller, Pastor Eric describes sheep as:

  • Easily lost: No sense of direction, can't find their way even when the fold is in sight
  • Distracted: See luscious grass and wander off the path without meaning to
  • Panicky: When they realize they're lost, every sound becomes threatening, every shadow looks like the enemy
  • Defenseless: Can't run well, have no defensive weapons like horns or fangs
  • Helpless: Can't even get up when on their backs; will die without intervention

We can replace these descriptions with ourselves: "I get easily lost and lose my sense of direction in life. I see something shiny, something new, something exciting, and soon I'm off the path. When I panic, everything becomes a threat. I feel defenseless, powerless to affect change in a world I cannot control."

Left to my own devices, I often need rescuing. I'm hopeless in this greater world without someone interceding. And we find out that the lot of any one of us in life is dependent on who we allow to be the shepherd.

‚Äî Pastor Eric

The imagery of the Good Shepherd finding His lost sheep is beautiful‚Äîthe shepherd joyfully carries the lamb on his shoulders back home. But what the image doesn't show is what happened minutes before: the sheep running away, panicking, not recognizing that the shepherd is the best thing for it.

Sometimes shepherds have to tackle sheep and bind their legs to rescue them. Even when a good God comes after us, we panic, fearing we might lose what we're chasing, not knowing enough to recognize the shepherd's swift movements are intended for our good.

We can listen to a million different voices: accomplishment, achievement, production, mattering, security, being right. But Jesus calls us by name‚Äîand when He does, everything changes:

  • Saul, Saul ‚Äî why are you persecuting me?
  • Martha, Martha ‚Äî there's one thing that matters
  • Mary ‚Äî at the empty tomb, one word and she sees Him
  • Lazarus ‚Äî called out of death itself

The Good Shepherd calls His sheep by name because He knows them, loves them, and is trying to rescue them. When we listen to His voice above all others, it changes everything.

Jesus warned of "wolves in sheep's clothing"‚Äîpeople who look like they're among you but whose intention is evil. But Jesus Himself is the opposite: the Shepherd in sheep's clothing.

He is the Good Shepherd who laid down His life, who when the wolves came after the sheep, took the attack on Himself. He died in place of the other sheep. In His three decades of life, He was always the sheep who listened to the Good Shepherd (the Father), showing us what obedience, faithfulness, trust, and faith look like.

He is the shepherd in sheep's clothing. He was the sheep who died in our place, and He was the sheep who showed us what obedience and faithfulness and trust and faith looked like by listening to His Father.

‚Äî Pastor Eric

Revelation beautifully captures this dual nature: "There is a lamb in the midst of the throne, He will be their shepherd, and He will guide them to springs of living water, and God will wipe away every tear from their eyes."

It's freeing to admit we don't have it all together, that we can't control everything, that we make decisions out of fear. We're not made to be in control. There is One who is, who loves us, who died for us‚Äîand that's the Good Shepherd.

The Life Without Lack

Dallas Willard's reflections on Psalm 23 reveal what it means to live under the care of the Good Shepherd.

"The Lord is my shepherd"

I'm in the care of someone else. I'm not the one in charge. I've taken my kingdom and surrendered it to the kingdom of God. I am living the with-God life.

"I shall not want"

That's the natural result. I shall not lack anything. That's what Jesus teaches: "Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness and everything else will be added."

"He makes me lie down in green pastures"

What kind of sheep lies down in a green pasture? A sheep that has eaten its fill. If a sheep is in a green pasture and she's not full, she'll be eating, not lying down.

"He leads me beside still waters"

A sheep that is being led beside still water is a sheep that is not thirsty.

"He restores my soul"

The broken depths of my soul are healed and reintegrated in a life in union with God, the eternal kind of life.

"He leads me in paths of righteousness"

The effect of the restoration of my soul is that I walk in paths of righteousness on his behalf, as a natural expression of my renewed inner nature.

"I will fear no evil"

A life without lack is one that carries no fear of evil. Our confidence in God soars far above wants and fears. Imagine what that would be like: no fear of life, aging or death, disease or hunger, no fear of any person or creature, not even the loss of all your possessions.

"For you are with me"

The complete sufficiency of the life without lack is based upon the presence of God. And he is most clearly and fully present to us in Jesus Christ, Emmanuel, God with us.

"Your rod and your staff, they comfort me"

I know from experience that the rod and the staff represent the shepherd's strength, his protective care.

"You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies"

Since I love my enemies, I would not feast upon a delicious meal in their presence and let them stand there hungry. The abundance of God's provision and safety in my life is so great, I would invite them to enjoy what God has prepared for me.

"You anoint my head with oil"

God is not only interested in my having something wonderful to eat, but in blessing me with a life that is full and free and powerful in him, including clothing, furnishings, joyful experiences, and deep relationships.

"My cup overflows"

I have more than my cup will hold, so much that I can be as generous as my shepherd without fear of ever running out.

"Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life"

This is the description of the eternal life available to us now in the kingdom of heaven: the abundant with-God life that comes from following the shepherd, where we dwell and abide with God in the fullness of his life, a life in which all the promises of Christ's gospel are realized.

Because of this, we have no reason to be anxious. The world is a perfectly safe place for us to be.

Application

üéØ For Those Who Lead

God calls us to lead like the Good Shepherd, not like the bad shepherds condemned in Ezekiel 34. Whether you lead a team at work, parent children, guide a care group, or care for aging parents, you are entrusted with "sheep."

Good shepherds:

  • Lay down their lives for those entrusted to them
  • Strengthen the weak and heal the sick
  • Pursue those who go astray with love
  • Nourish and feed their flock
  • Sometimes give of themselves so others can live

Bad shepherds:

  • Feed themselves at the expense of the sheep
  • Take money, prestige, anything that could go to others
  • Flee when danger comes
  • Care more about personal benefit than the flock

Ask yourself: Am I leading with justice, fairness, care, and love in a way that emulates the Good Shepherd? Or am I acting like a hired hand who's in it for personal gain?

  • I don't have it all together
  • I can't control everything
  • I get easily lost and distracted
  • I make decisions out of fear and respond out of panic

The lot of any sheep depends on the type of shepherd who owns it. The quality of your life depends on whose voice you're listening to. Will you listen to the Good Shepherd?

üôè A Prayer to the Good Shepherd

God, help us, no matter our position in life, to humble ourselves and put ourselves under the chief shepherd. It is right, it is good to humble ourselves.

Help us in our shepherding callings in life to shepherd with justice, fairness, care, and love in a way that emulates what you are like.

Help us to listen to your voice when so many voices in this world are calling to us to chase something else, to leave the fold, to take a different path, to pursue temporary pleasure, only to realize we end up not with you.

May you wreck those other voices of their power in our life. Detach them from our hearts, and may your voice and the power of it grow ever stronger in our hearts.

May we love you, worship you, and follow you, our great shepherd.

In Jesus' name, Amen.
