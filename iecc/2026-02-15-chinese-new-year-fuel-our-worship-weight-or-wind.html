üéµ Are We Too Casual About Worship?

Carrie opens with a provocative observation: sometimes we are a bit too casual about worship. Not the style of music or what the worship leaders are wearing ‚Äî but our attitude and our anticipation.

Last year, I went to the Coldplay concert. Since the day I got the tickets, my anticipation began. I cleared my schedule. I studied every song. When the night came, I sang every song, shouting, yelling, jumping up and down. Then Sunday came, and I worshipped as usual... and I realized I was only about 20% as excited as I was at the Coldplay concert.

‚Äî Carrie Lo

This honest confession sets the stage: if we can be that excited for a concert, why can't we bring even more passion to worshipping the living God?

üìú The Israelites' Story: Ezra 1-3

To understand what fuels passionate worship, Carrie takes us through the historical context of Ezra chapters 1 through 3.

üèõÔ∏è Historical Context: The Exile

  • After King David and King Solomon, the kingdom split into two halves
  • The southern kingdom of Judah lasted ~350 years, then was conquered by Babylon
  • King Nebuchadnezzar destroyed Jerusalem and burned down the temple
  • The Israelites were forced into exile in Babylon
  • Through Prophet Jeremiah, God foretold the exile would last 70 years
  • Through Prophet Isaiah, God foretold Babylon would be overthrown, and a man named Cyrus would lead the rebuilding
üìñ Ezra 1:1-3 (NIV)
"In the first year of Cyrus king of Persia, in order to fulfill the word of the Lord spoken by Jeremiah, the Lord moved the heart of Cyrus king of Persia to make a proclamation... 'Any of his people among you may go up to Jerusalem in Judah and build the temple of the Lord... and may their God be with them.'"
King Cyrus was not even a believer of God ‚Äî yet God moved his heart. The original Hebrew word for "temple" here is actually "house" ‚Äî they were rebuilding the household and community of God's people.

More than 42,000 people embarked on a months-long journey from Babylon to Jerusalem, through the desert. What motivated them? Their hope to return home after 70 years, and their hope to rebuild the temple ‚Äî to once again be in the presence of God.

Key Insight: King Cyrus also brought out the articles belonging to the original temple that Nebuchadnezzar had carried away. The Israelites could not only rebuild ‚Äî they could recover what was lost. This magnified their anticipation enormously.

üóùÔ∏è Element 1: Anticipation of God's Presence

In the Old Testament, God's presence dwelled in the temple. When the temple was destroyed and the Israelites exiled for 70 years, they had no place to encounter God's presence in that way. So when the opportunity came to rebuild, they weren't just anticipating a new building ‚Äî they were anticipating the presence of God.

It's Not the Place ‚Äî It's the Person at the Place: Carrie illustrates: "My desire to go home is much higher when I anticipate that my husband is already at home ‚Äî and even higher if he has done all the dishes and cooked my meal. My desire to fly to another country is much higher if I anticipate that my friend is going to pick me up at the airport." When we anticipate God's presence, our desire to worship is transformed.

God is always present, everywhere, at all times. But precisely because we know this, it doesn't always come to mind. It takes intentionality ‚Äî even for believers ‚Äî to anticipate that God is present with us.

God is present right here as we were singing every worship song. God is present right here as we pull out our phone to check Instagram.

‚Äî Carrie Lo

When we don't anticipate God's presence, worship becomes optional, skippable, adjustable, casual. But with anticipation, we can worship with passion and seriousness.

üèÜ Element 2: The Priority of Worship

üìñ Ezra 3:2-3 (NIV)

After the long journey home, the very first thing they did was not build homes for themselves ‚Äî but offer sacrifices to the Lord. Worship outweighed their fear and preceded their other needs.

They gathered materials, and when the builders laid the temple's foundation, worship erupted:

üìñ Ezra 3:10-11 (NIV)

This was just the laying of the foundation ‚Äî the whole building wasn't even built yet. But worship was their first act in every matter. It was the first building project. It was the first step of that project. God was the center of everything.

Anticipation + Priority = Powerful Worship: These two elements together ‚Äî anticipation of God's presence and the priority of worship ‚Äî are what made the Israelites' worship so passionate. And we can do it too.

üí° Practical Step: Come Early

Carrie offers a simple, practical starting point: come early to church. Not just on time ‚Äî early. For the 11:30 service, try arriving at 11:25 instead of 11:40.

  • You're not rushing, not sweating, not squeezing through people to find your seat
  • You have time to settle in, calm down, and prepare your heart for worship
  • Watch the countdown video ‚Äî it captures church ministries. Pray for them. Build anticipation.
  • We arrive early for things that are important: client meetings, meals with in-laws, flights to Japan

Coming early is just a means to an end. Other ways to build anticipation include reading devotions, going on retreats, hiking while praising God.

‚õ™ We Have Something Better Than a Temple

Carrie closes with a stunning truth. The second temple that the Israelites built would eventually be destroyed too. But we have something better ‚Äî Jesus. Through His death and resurrection, the Holy Spirit now dwells in every believer.

üìñ 1 Corinthians 3:16 (NIV)
"Do you not know that you are God's temple and that God's Spirit dwells in you?"
In the original language, "temple" is singular but "you" is plural. Paul is referring to all believers together as one body ‚Äî the church. God's presence is with and in the church.
We Should Be Even More Excited: The Israelites were overjoyed just at the laying of a foundation. But we are already a temple right here as the church. We don't need to wait for walls and bricks. Our anticipation for God's presence is even more certain. Our priority to worship Him should be even higher.

üê¥ The Year of the Horse

The horse is an image of strength, endurance, and the capacity to carry burdens. But one day, the load becomes too heavy. Many of us carry invisible burdens:

  • Resentment toward a family member ‚Äî your chest tightens when you see them at the dining table

  • Disappointment about your own life, masked by busyness
  • The weight of parenting, caring for aging parents, or just keeping life together

These invisible weights are real, and they prevent us from fully embracing worship.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Movement 1: Laying Down Your Weight ‚Äî Your Personal Altar

It starts in the most private place ‚Äî the altar of your own heart. The Israelites set up the altar on the foundation before the blueprint, before the gates, before any celebration.

Hebrew Word Study: The Hebrew word for altar, mizbeach, comes from the root word meaning "to offer, to present a gift." It's the same root that appears in "peace offering." The altar was about releasing ‚Äî bringing something heavy and releasing it into God's hands.

The Israelites had been in exile for generations. That's a lot of shame, a lot of weight, a lot of failure. At the altar, they could open their hands and release it all. What they received wasn't a transaction ‚Äî it was transformation. A fresh start. A new beginning.

Each burden we have to carry has once been laid on the shoulders of Emmanuel. The very thing that you're afraid to name, the weight you think will crush you ‚Äî Jesus bore it all already. He knows what they are. He is familiar with the shape. He has already carried it for you. So let Him.

‚Äî Charles Fortune, quoted by Pastor Daniel

What is your personal altar? It might be a moment of honesty in the elevator before you enter your home. A journal entry you never post but pray silently. Unclenching your fists from past hurts. That honest, sacred place where you stop holding on and let yourself be held by God.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Movement 2: Reweaving Your Closest Threads ‚Äî The Family Rhythm

The Israelites celebrated the Feast of Tabernacles (Ezra 3:4). This was a family festival ‚Äî building shelters, eating meals together, sharing vulnerability, sharing memories, and just being present.

When you're not busy managing your own baggage ‚Äî when you've given it to God ‚Äî you suddenly have space, both in your heart and in your hands. You stop criticizing. You stop putting people at a distance. You stop having unrealistic expectations.

Last month, my wife and I celebrated 25 years of marriage. We went to Taiwan. At a beautiful dinner, I asked a risky question: "Do you feel loved?" There was silence. Then a single answer: "Yes." She felt love not because of the nice dinner, the tourist areas, or the gifts ‚Äî but because throughout the trip, when she had to work every day, I did not once complain. I didn't roll my eyes. I was simply present.

‚Äî Pastor Daniel

üìñ Galatians 6:2 (NIV)
"Bear one another's burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ."
The question for all of us: with your family, your friends, the people you live with ‚Äî are you weight or are you wind?

Reweaving doesn't have to be dramatic. It's the next stitch in the tear that has been there for years. It's choosing to be curious and not judgmental at the dining table ‚Äî saying "Help me understand why that is so important" instead of "Here's where you went wrong." It's letting go of being right so that you can connect.

This Week's Challenge: Create a few minutes with the people around you. No phones. Just sit and ask: "What felt heavy for you today?" Don't try to fix it ‚Äî just share it. Be wind.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Movement 3: The Harmony of the Community

Finally, the whole community gathered to lay the temple foundation. And there's this stunning verse:

üìñ Ezra 3:12-13 (NIV)
"Many of the older priests and Levites and family heads, who had seen the former temple, wept aloud when they saw the foundation of this temple being laid, while many others shouted for joy. No one could distinguish the sound of the shouts of joy from the sound of weeping."
The older ones cried ‚Äî beautiful memory. The young ones shouted ‚Äî hope realized. This is what makes corporate worship possible: not everyone agreeing, not fake joy, but honest harmony where grief and hope coexist.

When we lay down our personal weight, we become light enough to be wind for others. When enough of us become wind to our closest people, something beautiful happens as a church ‚Äî we begin to move in harmony. And when we move in harmony, we can worship together. That power of worshipping together changes everything.

üìñ Ezra 3:1 (NIV)
"The people assembled together as one man in Jerusalem."
This is what God is building towards ‚Äî not just a temple, not just worship, but a people united to move as one.
The Horse's True Power: A horse's strength was never meant for carrying burdens forever. It is Jesus who carries our burdens. The horse's true power is in running free together ‚Äî and that is how God wants His church to move.
