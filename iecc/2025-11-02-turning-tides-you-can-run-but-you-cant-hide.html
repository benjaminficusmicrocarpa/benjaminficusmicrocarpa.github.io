üåä Sermon Overview

üìö Central Theme

We all have sophisticated ways of running from God, just like Jonah. This sermon explores how we accumulate bitterness, harbor disappointment, leave sin unconfessed, and hold conflicting opinions with God‚Äîall while appearing outwardly obedient. Yet God's mercy is greater still, pursuing us even in our rebellion.

üéØ Key Verse

üó∫Ô∏è Sermon Outline

1. Jonah's Overt Rebellion üèÉ

God calls Jonah to Nineveh (885km), but he flees to Tarshish (4,000km)‚Äîthe furthest point in the ancient world. Distance mattered more than destination.

2. Our Sophisticated Running üé≠

We don't run as obviously as Jonah. We back away quietly through accumulated bitterness, unresolved disappointment, unconfessed sin, and conflicting opinions with God.

3. Strange Decisions While Running ü§Ø

Jonah makes illogical choices: paying a fortune, getting on a boat on the God-made sea, confessing truth while fleeing. In darkness, we all make strange decisions.

4. God's Relentless Mercy üíô

Despite Jonah's rebellion, God shows mercy to the Assyrians, the ship's crew, and to Jonah himself. His mercy is juxtaposed against our disobedience.

5. The Call to Surrender üôè

Like Peter: "Because you say so, I will." Obedience means saying yes even when we don't want to, trusting God's wisdom over our own.

üéôÔ∏è Full Sermon Content

Jonah is probably the most well-known of all the prophets. It's a favorite story to teach children: Jonah disobeys, God sends a whale, swallows Jonah for three days and three nights, spits him up, and then he goes and tells people to repent. Classic story that all our kids upstairs know well.

But today we're doing the adult version. We're going to turn it up and understand the nuance and depth of meaning that God has for us in this story. We're going to avoid the temptation to think that familiarity breeds contempt and look at it with fresh eyes.

"It is a classic story... but we're going to take a chance to do the adult version of this. We're going to turn it up a little bit and understand the nuance."

‚Äî Pastor Brett

üìö Understanding Jonah's Place in Scripture

Jonah is one of the minor prophets in the Old Testament. They're called "minor" only because they're shorter, not because they're less important. In fact, Jonah is only four chapters long‚Äîdepending on your Bible, it's only two pages.

The Old Testament books can be grouped this way:

  • Books of the Law
  • Historical books
  • Books of poetry (Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes)
  • Five major prophets
  • Twelve minor prophets (including Jonah)

üîç Unique Features of Jonah

  • ‚úÖ Very well-known: Probably more famous than any other prophet
  • üìù No other names listed: Only Jonah and his father Amitai are mentioned‚Äîno kings, judges, or other prophets
  • üìÖ No dates or times: Very unusual for prophetic books, leading to skepticism about its authenticity
  • üó∫Ô∏è Lots of geography: Places like Joppa, Tarshish, and Nineveh are prominently featured
  • üïäÔ∏è Name means "dove": Ironic, since doves represent peace and Jonah is anything but peaceful
  • üìñ Mostly narrative: Tells the story of Jonah's life rather than recording God's messages
  • üíî Hard-hearted prophet: Unlike other prophets, Jonah is neither tender toward people nor toward God
  • ‚ùì Cliffhanger ending: Like the prodigal son's older brother, we're left wondering if Jonah ever truly repented

Jonah 1:1-3 (NIV)

"The word of the Lord came to Jonah son of Amittai: 'Go to the great city of Nineveh and preach against it, because its wickedness has come up before me.' But Jonah ran away from the Lord and headed for Tarshish."

üó∫Ô∏è The Geography of Disobedience

God told Jonah to go to Nineveh‚Äîthe great city, capital of Assyria. This was a journey of about 885 kilometers from Joppa (where Jonah would board a ship).

But Jonah said, "Nope. I've got a better idea. I haven't seen Spain." He heads to Tarshish‚Äî4,000 kilometers in the opposite direction. This was the furthest point in the navigable ancient world. This was the end of the world.

üí° Key Insight:

The destination didn't matter to Jonah‚Äîthe distance did. He wanted to get as far away from God's call as humanly possible.

üò† Why Did Jonah Run?

Nineveh was known for being evil and harsh. The Ninevites had perfected the art of skinning human beings alive‚Äîthat's next-level cruelty. This reputation was well-known throughout the region, recorded in extra-biblical historical writings.

Jonah couldn't think of a worse group of people to receive the mercy of God. He decides: "No, no. You can tell me to go here, maybe. But I'm not going there."

"He had limitations on what he would do in response to God, which made him God of his own life. If he chose obedience to God as 'maybe I'll do it, maybe I won't,' he's not really obeying at all."

‚Äî Pastor Brett

For most of us, our running from God is quite sophisticated. We don't do it so overtly like Jonah. We walk. We back away. So as to not cause a lot of attention, so as to not be so rebellious.

We're a little more sophisticated in our ability to manage God's role in our lives. We sing "I'm prone to wander, Lord I feel it, I'm prone to leave the God I love," while simultaneously making plans to avoid Him.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Accumulated Bitterness

Hebrews 12:15 (NIV)

"See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God and that no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many."

Not one massive event, but a stack of slow things that build up over time:

  • A relationship gone bad
  • A friendship broken
  • A misunderstanding with family
  • A promotion not granted at work
  • Small disappointments that accumulate

Our emotional experience in life can affect our spiritual vitality. The author of Hebrews warns us: don't let bitterness stay there, because if it does, it develops roots. It grabs hold and becomes controlling of who you are.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Danger:

Sometimes we like our bitterness. Being a victim can feel soothing. We would rather stay in that than choose to embrace the grace of God.

The Antidote: Identify bitterness and don't give it room. Don't water it, don't feed it, don't allow it to grow.

"I have to do something wonderfully Hong Kong‚Äîgo to a beautiful beach that's five minutes away and go, 'I'm so glad I'm not in Texas right now.' Just to soothe my heart... But see, you have to acknowledge it and what impact it could have on your heart. And you have to put it to death."

‚Äî Pastor Brett, on dealing with distance from family

2Ô∏è‚É£ Unresolved Disappointment

We all have disappointment. If you don't have any disappointment, you're not paying attention. There are things to be disappointed in: finances, health, investments, career, marriage, kids, parents, siblings. Life in a broken world guarantees disappointment.

"The mistake is we tend to think life should be fair because God is fair. But God is not life. And if I confuse God with the physical reality of life, then I set myself up for crashing disappointment."

‚Äî Philip Yancey, "Disappointment with God"

The key question: In this broken world where disappointment will happen, what do you do with it? Do you:

  • Tuck it away as evidence that "life isn't fair"?
  • Feel mistreated and victimized?
  • Believe it's more unfair to you than others?

Key Responses to Disappointment:

1 Peter 5:7 (NIV): "Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you."

Remember: God is El Roi‚Äîthe God who sees. He understands your plight, pain, fear, and insecurity.

Hebrews 12:2 (NIV): "Fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith."

If you don't fix your eyes on Jesus, you'll look around and either become:

  • Smug and superior as you find someone whose life is worse
  • Envious and jealous as you find someone whose life is better

3Ô∏è‚É£ Unconfessed Sin

We all have sin in our lives. The problem? Most of us are very uncomfortable with that word. We don't often get together for dinner and ask, "How are you dealing with sin in your life?" It's not exactly a party starter.

We tend to categorize sin into the "big bad ones" and think: "I'm fine. No murder equals no sin." But sin refers to the depths of our depravity‚Äîour very disposition, our tendency to be selfish. That's all it is.

1 John 1:8-10 (NIV)

"If we claim to be without sin, we deceive ourselves and the truth is not in us. If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us our sins and purify us from all unrighteousness. If we claim we have not sinned, we make him out to be a liar and his word is not in us."

John is simply saying: You've got sin. If you don't know you have sin, you haven't looked hard enough or haven't asked people around you. It's your motives, your ideas, your thoughts‚Äînot just your behaviors.

üí° The Core Issue:

At our core, we want distance from God. We want blessings but not direction. We want God to be kind all the time at no cost or adjustment to us. In fact, we want Santa Claus in the sky. We don't want a God who tells us what to do‚Äîwe prefer to tell Him how to bless us.

Luke 5:8 (NIV)

"When Simon Peter saw this, he fell at Jesus' knees and said, 'Go away from me, Lord; I am a sinful man!'"

Peter recognizes the effect of sin: it causes us to want to walk away from God when we realize how good, great, and forgiving He is. Our tendency is to say, "This is too much. Depart from me."

"The answer to our sin issue is that it was solved once and for all at the cross in terms of forgiveness. But for ongoing sin, it is to confess‚Äîwhich means to simply agree with God‚Äîand to have relationship restored and intimacy welcomed."

‚Äî Pastor Brett

James 5:16 (NIV)

"Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed."

When we put words to our sin and confess to a trusted friend, it makes it real. This is the pathway to healing and great accountability.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Conflicting Opinion with God

Sometimes it's not overt sin, but just a conflicting opinion with God. There are areas where it seems like a judgment call, and we might disagree with God's wisdom or direction.

Luke 5:4-5 (NIV) - Peter's Response

When Jesus told Peter to go fishing at noon (the worst time), Peter replied: "Master, we've worked hard all night and haven't caught anything. But because you say so, I will let down the nets."

Peter had every reason to resist:

  • Physical exhaustion: He'd been fishing all night
  • Vocational expertise: He was the professional fisherman, not Jesus
  • Logical reasoning: Noon is the worst time to fish

üéØ The Most Important Words

"Because you say so, I will."

Reluctance is registered‚Äîit's not because Peter wanted to. But this is where faith is tested: when you obey even when you don't want to.

"Hudson (my son with ADD) finally buckled his seatbelt and said, 'On the outside I'm buckled, but on the inside I'm running everywhere.' He was so Peter-esque: 'Because you say so I will, but I don't want to. Don't think I want to, because I don't want to.'"

‚Äî Pastor Brett

When's the last time your actions reflected "because you say so, I will"? When you forgive but don't want to? When you obey but don't want to? This is where faith is genuinely tested.

Jonah 1:3, 9 (NIV)

"But Jonah ran away from the Lord and headed for Tarshish. He went down to Joppa, where he found a ship bound for that port. After paying the fare, he went aboard and sailed for Tarshish to flee from the Lord."

"He answered, 'I am a Hebrew and I worship the Lord, the God of heaven, who made the sea and the dry land.'"

While running from God, we make some strange decisions. We get distorted. We get weird. Jonah made some wild decisions, and so do we. In our darkness, we can't always see how illogical our choices become.

üö¢ Jonah's Illogical Decisions

1. Geographically Delusional üó∫Ô∏è

He acknowledges: "I worship the Lord, the God of heaven, who made the sea and the dry land." The God who made the sea‚Äîand he's going to get on a boat? The God he has angered controls the very sea he's sailing on. Isn't land safer than water?

2. Economically Wasteful üí∞

He pays money out of his own pocket for the longest, most expensive fare available. There's no price you can put on disobedience. "If I want to do what I'm going to do, I will just be lavish in it," he says.

3. Logistically Absurd üìè

God asks him to go 885km. He chooses instead to travel 4,000km in the opposite direction for weeks at a time. It doesn't make sense‚Äîbut in rebellion, distance matters more than logic.

4. Spiritually Deluded ‚õ™

He's running from God overtly, telling people "I'm running from God," yet he declares truthfully: "I'm a Hebrew and I worship the Lord." Strange way of worshiping‚Äîby disobeying and fleeing!

üé≠ Why Confess Truth While Fleeing?

If Jonah is trying to disobey God so overtly, lying wouldn't be beyond his capacity. So why does he confess the truth about who he worships?

üí° The Pattern of Self-Deception:

We know that when we are disobeying here, we will highlight our obedience there to make ourselves feel better. "Well, we can't be perfect. So, you know, I'm disobeying God here, but by the way, I'm a worshiper of God, the maker of heaven and earth."

He tells the truth to justify his own psyche, to hopefully balance out his other more flagrant sins. He has moments of brilliant truth-telling to compensate for his rebellion.

"I can't tell you how many times I've seen this played out where people will make horrible decision, horrible decision, horrible decision, and then make one small good spiritual one and say, 'Look at this! Notice this!' Deceived through and through."

‚Äî Pastor Brett

‚¨áÔ∏è The Downward Spiral

In the Hebrew language, the text emphasizes Jonah's descent:

  • He went down to Joppa
  • He went down to the ship's hold
  • He went down into the ocean
  • He went down into the whale

The repeated use of "down" highlights that Jonah's physical descent mirrors his spiritual digression‚Äîa downward spiral of the soul.

Juxtaposed against Jonah's overt deceit and rebellion is the mercy of God. Throughout the entire book, we see mercy laced everywhere‚Äîtoward people who seemingly don't deserve it, including Jonah himself.

üåç Mercy to the Ninevites

Jonah 1:2; 3:1-2, 10 (NIV)

"Go to the great city of Nineveh and preach against it, because its wickedness has come up before me."

"Then the word of the Lord came to Jonah a second time: 'Go to the great city of Nineveh and proclaim to it the message I give you.'"

"When God saw what they did and how they turned from their evil ways, he relented and did not bring on them the destruction he had threatened."

These were the people who perfected skinning humans alive. The most cruel, evil people imaginable. Yet God:

  • Wanted to warn them
  • Gave them a chance to repent
  • Sent a prophet (twice!) to reach them
  • Relented from destruction when they turned from evil

‚öì Mercy to the Ship's Crew

Jonah 1:16 (NIV)

"At this the men greatly feared the Lord, and they offered a sacrifice to the Lord and made vows to him."

These unsuspecting passengers and crew members encountered Jonah's God through this storm. The word used is "Yahweh"‚Äîthey came to faith in Jonah's God.

ü§î Powerful Question:

Would witnessing this have been enough for you to believe in God? They did. The very people Jonah didn't want to engage, who he didn't want God's blessing to reach‚Äîa revival breaks out among them on the boat!

üêã Mercy to Jonah

Jonah 1:17: "Now the Lord provided a huge fish to swallow Jonah, and Jonah was in the belly of the fish three days and three nights."

God provided the fish‚Äînot as punishment, but as rescue. Without it, Jonah would have drowned.

Jonah 2:10: "And the Lord commanded the fish, and it vomited Jonah onto dry land."

God commanded the fish to deliver Jonah safely to land‚Äîgiving him another chance.

Jonah 4:6: "Then the Lord God provided a leafy plant and made it grow up over Jonah to give shade for his head to ease his discomfort."

Even when Jonah was angry and sulking, God provided shade and comfort.

Over and over throughout the book, we see:

  • God provides
  • God leads
  • God commands
  • God relents
  • God shows mercy

"We see mercy pouring out, juxtaposed against this prophet who does no good. And God says, 'My mercy is greater still.'"

‚Äî Pastor Brett

‚ú® The Central Truth

Despite all of Jonah's rebellion, disobedience, hard-heartedness, and attempts to flee‚ÄîGod's mercy remains constant, relentless, and greater than we can comprehend. This is the God we serve.

It's a great place for us to ask ourselves: Where can I be like Jonah? We're all a little Jonah-ish. Don't beat yourself up‚Äîbut do examine your heart.

üí° Practical Application

üéØ Central Challenge

Stop running in sophisticated ways. Identify where you're backing away from God, and choose to move closer instead. Remember: you're either moving toward God or away from Him‚Äîthere's no neutral ground.

üîß Four Areas for Action

1Ô∏è‚É£ Deal with Accumulated Bitterness

üìù This Week's Action:

  • Write down 3 things you're bitter about (relationships, circumstances, disappointments)
  • For each one, ask: "Am I holding onto this? Am I watering and feeding this bitterness?"
  • Pray specifically: "God, I confess my bitterness about _____. I choose to release this to you."
  • If needed, confess to a trusted friend for accountability

üí≠ Remember: "See to it that no root of bitterness grows up to cause trouble" (Hebrews 12:15). Don't give bitterness room to grow.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Process Your Disappointment

üìù This Week's Action:

  • List your current disappointments (finances, health, relationships, career, etc.)
  • Ask: "What conclusions am I making? Do I feel victimized? Do I think life is more unfair to me?"
  • Meditate on this truth: "God is not life. God is good even when life is hard."
  • Practice: "Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you" (1 Peter 5:7)
  • Fix your eyes on Jesus instead of comparing your life to others

üí≠ Remember: God is El Roi‚Äîthe God who sees. He understands your pain, but He is not the source of it. Don't confuse God with life's circumstances.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Confess Unconfessed Sin

üìù This Week's Action:

  • Set aside time for honest self-examination (not just behaviors, but motives and thoughts)
  • Ask: "What do I really want? Blessings without direction? A Santa Claus God?"
  • Confess to God: "I don't fully trust you. I'm guarded. I want control. I have limitations on my obedience."
  • If appropriate, confess to a trusted friend for healing and accountability
  • Remember: forgiveness is already granted at the cross‚Äîconfession restores intimacy

üí≠ Remember: "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us" (1 John 1:9). Confession means to agree with God about our sin.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Surrender Conflicting Opinions

üìù This Week's Action:

  • Identify areas where you disagree with God's direction or wisdom
  • Ask: "Where am I saying 'Stay in your lane, God' about my life, career, relationships, or calling?"
  • Practice Peter's response: Write it down and pray it: "Because you say so, I will"
  • Obey even when you don't want to‚Äîespecially when you don't want to
  • Remember: trusting God is a logical decision, not an outlandish one

üí≠ Remember: Faith is tested when you obey but don't want to. "On the outside I'm buckled, but on the inside I'm running everywhere"‚Äîacknowledge the tension, but choose obedience anyway.

üéØ This Week's Challenge

ü§î Questions for Reflection

Set aside time this week to honestly consider these questions. You might journal your responses, discuss them with a friend, or bring them to God in prayer.

1. üèÉ How Am I Running?

Jonah's running was obvious‚Äîhe bought a ticket to the other side of the world. How is my running more sophisticated? Am I:

  • Avoiding people who remind me of God?
  • Making conversations less about God and more about other things?
  • Showing up physically but with a dead or hard heart?
  • Performing outwardly while distant inwardly?

2. üíî What's My Root of Bitterness?

What small disappointments have I allowed to accumulate? What relationships, circumstances, or unmet expectations am I holding onto? Am I watering and feeding bitterness instead of embracing God's grace?

Be specific: Name the bitterness. Write it down. Confess it to God and ask Him to help you release it.

3. üòî How Do I Handle Disappointment?

When life doesn't go as planned, what conclusions do I make? Do I:

  • Feel victimized and mistreated?
  • Believe life is more unfair to me than to others?
  • Confuse God's character with life's circumstances?
  • Compare myself to others and become envious or smug?

Remember: God is not life. God is good even when life is hard. How can I better fix my eyes on Jesus this week?

4. üé≠ What Am I Confessing Truthfully While Fleeing?

Jonah declared "I worship the Lord" while running from Him. Where am I highlighting my obedience in one area to justify my disobedience in another? What "good spiritual decisions" am I using to balance out my rebellion?

5. ü§ê What Sin Remains Unconfessed?

Beyond behaviors, what about my motives, thoughts, and heart attitudes? Where do I want:

  • Blessings without direction?
  • A Santa Claus God instead of a sovereign Lord?
  • To tell God how to bless me rather than surrender to His wisdom?
  • Control over my life instead of trust in His plan?

Consider: Who is one trusted person I could confess this to for healing and accountability?

6. üë• Who Are My "Ninevites"?

  • Can't imagine receiving God's mercy?
  • Refuse to be tender and long-suffering with?
  • Am unreconciled with?

7. üôè Where Do I Need to Say "Because You Say So"?

Like Peter in the boat, where is God asking me to obey even when:

  • I don't want to?
