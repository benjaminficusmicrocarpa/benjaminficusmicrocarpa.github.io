üéØ Sermon Overview

We all have people in our lives who drive us crazy‚Äîthose who sap our energy, rob our joy, and leave us feeling angry, guilty, frustrated, and confused. This message explores what God says about dealing with the annoying people in our lives while maintaining our faith and integrity.

Key Themes

  • Boundaries are Biblical: From Genesis 1 onward, God establishes boundaries to bring order from chaos and peace from disorder.
  • Boundaries Require Balance: We need to avoid being either too rigid or too passive in our relational boundaries.
  • Boundaries Protect Peace: Healthy boundaries honor God while protecting our emotional and spiritual well-being.
  • Self-Examination is Critical: Annoying relationships often reveal our own inadequacies and need for spiritual growth.

üó∫Ô∏è The Path to Proper Boundaries

1. Choose to Rise Above the Offense

Develop thick skin with a tender heart. Practice being "unoffendable"‚Äîdon't let every slight or annoyance take up residence in your mind and rob you of joy.

"It is to one's glory to overlook an offense." (Proverbs 19:11)

2. Don't Stop Forgiving

Forgiveness isn't optional. Jesus commanded us to forgive "seventy times seven"‚Äîmeaning continuously. You need to forgive more than anyone needs to be forgiven.

"Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing." (Luke 23:34)

3. Refuse to Play Games

Don't engage in manipulation, guilt trips, or endless arguments. Like Jesus before Pilate, sometimes the wisest response is no response at all.

"We have renounced secret and shameful ways. We do not use deception, nor do we distort the Word of God." (2 Corinthians 4:2)

4. Disengage When Necessary

Some people thrive on conflict. Don't fuel their fire. Remember: it takes two people to have an argument.

"Just as charcoal and wood keep a fire going, a quarrelsome person keeps an argument going." (Proverbs 26:21)

5. Establish Clear Boundaries

Boundaries are for you, not others. You can't control their behavior, but you can control how you respond and what you allow in your life.

"Have nothing to do with such people." (2 Timothy 3:5)

6. Stay on the High Ground

Don't retaliate. Don't gossip. Don't return insult for insult. You can have boundaries and still be a blessing.

"Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good." (Romans 12:21)

üåÖ God Establishes Boundaries from the Beginning

‚ñº

In Genesis 1, before creation, the earth was "formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep." God's first act was to create a boundary‚Äîseparating light from darkness, bringing order from chaos.

In Genesis 2, God gave Adam and Eve complete freedom except for one boundary: "You are free to eat from any tree in the garden, but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil." This boundary was an expression of God's love, giving humanity the choice to love Him back.

When that boundary was broken, chaos ensued. Throughout Genesis, one boundary became over 600 laws and guidelines‚Äînot because God wanted to restrict freedom, but because more rebellion required more clarity to maintain peace.

The principle: As chaos grows, so does the need for boundaries.

üïäÔ∏è God is Not a God of Disorder

‚ñº

The Apostle Paul wrote to the chaotic church in Corinth: "For God is not a God of disorder, but of peace." Despite all their problems‚Äîincest, drunkenness at the Lord's Supper, spiritual abuse‚ÄîPaul reminded them that behind all the rules is a God who desires peace.

This peace wasn't just individual; it was relational: "As in all the congregations of the Lord's people." God's design is for peace to exist within our relationships and communities.

We're called to "live at peace with all people" as much as it depends on us (Romans 12:18). But here's the tension: what if "all people" includes annoying people who never change? The answer: YOU need to change. Change the dynamics, the frequency, the interaction. Establish boundaries so peace can be maintained.

üé≠ The Kennedy Town Lunch Story

‚ñº

Pastor Brett shared a personal story about developing a friendship with someone who worked in Kennedy Town. Every time they planned lunch, the friend would make reservations in Kennedy Town, requiring Pastor Brett to travel there.

After several lunches, bitterness began to grow. Every MTR ride to Kennedy Town became an opportunity to nurse a wound: "Why am I always going to Kennedy Town? Doesn't he know we have restaurants in Quarry Bay? He's so selfish. He thinks his time is more important than mine."

The turning point came when Pastor Brett finally suggested meeting in Causeway Bay instead. The friend immediately agreed: "Oh yeah, that's great!" In that moment, the entire narrative collapsed. The friend wasn't selfish or entitled‚ÄîPastor Brett had never communicated his preference.

The lesson: Drawing a boundary saved the relationship. It prevented resentment from growing and revealed that the "problem" was actually in the lack of communication, not in the friend's character.

üìß The June Email Story

‚ñº

Early in his ministry at Island ECC, Pastor Brett received weekly critical emails from a well-intentioned woman (pseudonym: June). Each email followed the "sandwich" pattern: compliment, criticism, compliment.

Initially, he responded immediately with long, gracious replies. But this actually fed the pattern‚ÄîJune felt heard and continued sending critical feedback every week. The constant criticism began affecting his preaching; he'd be on stage thinking about what June would criticize next. She "lived rent-free" in his head.

The solution wasn't to attack June or cut her off completely. Instead, Pastor Brett adjusted his boundaries:

  • He shortened his responses significantly
  • He delayed his replies (Thursday instead of Monday)
  • He reduced the emotional energy he invested

The lesson: Boundaries are for YOU, not others. You can't control June's behavior, but you can control how much space she occupies in your life and mind.

‚ö†Ô∏è When to Draw Hard Boundaries

‚ñº

Paul's letters give clear guidance on when hard boundaries are necessary. In 2 Timothy 3, he describes people who will be "lovers of themselves, lovers of money, boastful, proud, abusive, disobedient to their parents, ungrateful, unholy, without love, unforgiving, slanderous, without self-control, brutal, not lovers of the good."

Paul's instruction is direct: "Have nothing to do with such people."

In Titus 3:10, regarding divisive people in the church: "Warn a divisive person once, and then warn them a second time. After that, have nothing to do with them." Two warnings, then a clear boundary for the protection of the community.

Paul also confronted the Corinthian church: "You even put up with anyone who enslaves you or exploits you or takes advantage of you or puts on airs or slaps you in the face." He was astounded that they allowed themselves to be mistreated.

The principle: Being Christian doesn't mean being a doormat. It means establishing boundaries with grace and love, protecting yourself and others from harm while still extending forgiveness.

üíî Hurt vs. Harm

‚ñº

Henry Cloud, author of "Boundaries," makes an important distinction: "There's a big difference between hurt and harm. We all hurt sometimes in facing hard truths, but it makes us grow. It can be the source of huge growth. That is not harmful. Harm is when you damage someone."

Drawing a boundary might hurt someone's feelings. They might feel rejected or confused. But if done with grace and clarity, it's not harmful‚Äîit might actually be helpful. It establishes truth and creates space for both people to grow.

Guidelines for healthy boundaries:

  • Never attack the person: Address the behavior, not their character
  • Never close the door to healing: Always leave room for reconciliation
  • Don't be afraid of temporary hurt: If it promotes long-term health and truth
  • Communicate clearly: Vague boundaries create more confusion and conflict

üéØ Practical Application

For the Too-Rigid Person

If you naturally lean toward strict boundaries and find yourself thinking "finally, a sermon validating my approach," consider:

  • Are your boundaries protecting peace or avoiding relationship?
  • Do you leave room for grace, forgiveness, and second chances?
  • Are you quick to label someone as "divisive" or "annoying" without examining your own heart?
  • Have you communicated your boundaries clearly, or do you expect people to read your mind?

Challenge: This week, extend grace to someone who annoys you. Choose to overlook one offense without mentioning it.

For the Too-Passive Person

If boundaries feel "unloving" to you and you're thinking "but we need to be forgiving 70 times 7," consider:

  • Are you confusing boundaries with unforgiveness? (They're not the same)
  • Are you allowing yourself to be exploited, enslaved, or taken advantage of?
  • Is your passivity actually enabling destructive behavior in others?
  • Are you protecting the peace of your community, or allowing division to spread?

Challenge: This week, establish one clear boundary with someone who consistently drains you. Communicate it kindly but firmly.

Remember: Boundaries Are For YOU

You cannot control:

  • Whether someone calls you after 10 PM
  • Whether someone sends critical emails
  • Whether someone argues or picks fights
  • Whether someone changes their behavior

You CAN control:

  • Whether you answer calls after 10 PM
  • How quickly and lengthily you respond to emails
  • Whether you engage in arguments
  • How much mental and emotional space someone occupies in your life

Steps to Establish Healthy Boundaries

Step 1: Self-Examination

Pray Psalm 139:23-24: "Search me, God, and know my heart; test me and know my anxious thoughts." Ask God to reveal:

  • Am I too rigid or too passive?
  • What wounds am I nursing that don't belong?
  • What false narratives have I created about others?

Step 2: Identify the Pattern

Recognize recurring dynamics that rob your peace:

  • Who consistently drains your energy?
  • What situations trigger disproportionate emotional responses?
  • Where are you being exploited or taken advantage of?

Step 3: Communicate Clearly

Don't expect people to read your mind. State your needs directly but kindly:

  • "I'd love to meet for lunch. How about Causeway Bay this time?"
  • "I appreciate your feedback. I'll consider it prayerfully."
  • "I need to step away from this conversation. Let's talk when we're both calmer."

Step 4: Adjust Your Response

Change what YOU do, not what you demand from others:

  • Delay responses to reduce reactivity
  • Shorten interactions that become draining
  • Exit conversations that turn quarrelsome
  • Limit frequency of contact with toxic individuals

Step 5: Monitor Your Heart

Boundaries should protect peace, not harbor bitterness:

  • Continue forgiving, even as you establish distance
  • Don't gossip about the person
  • Pray for them genuinely
  • Leave room for reconciliation if they change

ü§î Reflection Questions

1. Do you tend to be too rigid or too passive in your relational boundaries?

‚ñº

Signs you might be too rigid:

  • You quickly cut people off without giving grace or second chances
  • You have strict rules about how people should behave toward you
  • You struggle to forgive or let go of past offenses
  • You avoid vulnerable or messy relationships altogether
  • People describe you as "hard to approach" or "judgmental"

Signs you might be too passive:

  • You feel resentful but never communicate your needs
  • You allow people to repeatedly take advantage of you
  • You avoid conflict at all costs, even when necessary
  • You feel drained by relationships but don't know how to change them
  • People describe you as "too nice" or a "pushover"

Journal: Write honestly about which tendency you lean toward. Ask God to show you specific examples from the past month where you've been too rigid or too passive.

2. How does the scripture in today's message inspire you in your relationships?

‚ñº

Consider these scriptures and how they apply to your current relationships:

"It is to one's glory to overlook an offense" (Proverbs 19:11)

What small offenses have you been nursing that you need to overlook? Who in your life deserves the benefit of the doubt?

"Jesus made no reply" (Matthew 27:14)

What arguments or baiting do you need to simply not engage with? Where is silence the wisest response?

"Have nothing to do with such people" (2 Timothy 3:5)

Are there relationships where God is calling you to establish firmer boundaries for the sake of your spiritual health?

"Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good" (Romans 12:21)

How can you maintain boundaries while still blessing those who annoy or hurt you?

3. Which one of your relationships needs more firm boundaries or more clear expectations?

‚ñº

Exercise: Relationship Audit

List the key relationships in your life (spouse, children, parents, close friends, colleagues, church members). For each, honestly assess:

Energizing vs. Draining

  • Does this relationship give me life or drain me?
  • Do I feel peace or anxiety when I see their name on my phone?
  • Am I looking forward to seeing them or dreading it?

Clarity vs. Confusion

  • Have I clearly communicated my expectations?
  • Do they know what bothers me, or am I expecting them to just know?
  • Are we on the same page about the frequency and nature of our interaction?

Balance vs. Exploitation

  • Is this relationship mutual, or am I constantly giving without receiving?
  • Do I feel respected and valued, or taken advantage of?
  • Can I say "no" without guilt, or do I feel obligated?

Action Step: Choose ONE relationship that needs attention. This week, have an honest conversation about expectations, or establish one new boundary to protect your peace.

4. What false narratives have you created about annoying people in your life?

‚ñº

Pastor Brett's Kennedy Town story revealed how we often create entire narratives about people's motives without any evidence. He assumed his friend was selfish and entitled, when the friend was actually just making convenient suggestions.

Common false narratives we create:

  • "They're doing this on purpose to annoy me"
  • "They think their time is more important than mine"
  • "They don't care about my feelings"
  • "They're trying to control/manipulate me"
  • "They should just know better"

Reality check questions:

  • Have I actually communicated my concern, or am I expecting mind-reading?
  • Is there a more charitable explanation for their behavior?
  • Am I projecting my own insecurities onto their actions?
  • What evidence do I have for my interpretation?

"I developed this whole narrative... thinking he was selfish, thinking that he was entitled, thinking that he thought his time was more important than my time. I lived in a lie...
