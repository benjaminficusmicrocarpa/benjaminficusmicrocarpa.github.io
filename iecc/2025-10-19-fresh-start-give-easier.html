Sermon Overview

🎯 Central Message

Generosity isn't merely a religious duty—it's a powerful weapon that breaks us free from money's enslaving grip. By shifting from a scarcity mindset to an abundance mindset, we can give easier and experience true freedom.

🔑 Three Key Elements ▼

1. The Mentality: We struggle between two mindsets—abundance (healthy eyes) and scarcity (unhealthy eyes). An abundance mindset recognizes that God owns everything, making us more generous. A scarcity mindset clutches wealth out of fear and never feels satisfied.

2. The Culprit: Mammon—the god of money—is a rival deity that masters us. Jesus says we cannot serve both God and money; we either despise money (cautious and wary of its corrupting power) or become devoted to it.

3. The Solution: Generosity itself is the weapon. Start small, test God in giving, and watch for the target result: freedom from money's mastery. Each act of generosity chips away at mammon's control over our lives.

📚 Series Context: Fresh Start ▼

This is part five of the Fresh Start series, based on Isaiah 43:18-19. The series focuses on perceiving God's new work in our lives through:

  • Week 1: Wake up from spiritual malaise
  • Week 2: See Him more clearly
  • Week 3: Trust Him deeper
  • Week 4: Pray bigger through surrender
  • Week 5: Give easier through generosity
💡 Practical Takeaway ▼

Start small and test God. Begin with small acts of generosity—$5 to a beggar, then $10, $20, $50. Pay attention to your relationship with money. Do you notice the narrative of "just a little more" quieting down? Continue ramping up: give abundantly to friends in need, to strangers, to the church, to NGOs. Watch as your scarcity mindset shifts and peace increases.

Main Sermon Content

Introduction: The Charity Gala Experience 🎭 ▼

Pastor Kevin shares his personal experience at a charity gala where an auctioneer publicly asked for pledges at increasing financial thresholds—$800,000, $600,000, down to $10,000. As people raised their hands and the room erupted in applause, he felt the pressure to give but ultimately didn't.

"I'd be lying if I said that I was disappointed. If I had to be honest, I was more relieved because generosity has always been a hard thing for me."

Despite knowing that generous people are happier, have better health, stronger relationships, and longer lives, Kevin asks: Why is it so hard for us to give? The answer isn't just economic circumstances—it's something more insidious at work in our hearts.

Part 1: The Mentality — Healthy vs. Unhealthy Eyes 👁️ ▼

Jesus teaches in Matthew 6:19-23 about storing treasures in heaven rather than on earth. But the passage about "healthy" and "unhealthy" eyes seems confusing until we understand the cultural context.

"The eye is the lamp of the body. If your eyes are healthy, your whole body will be full of light. But if your eyes are unhealthy, your whole body will be full of darkness."

Matthew 6:22-23 (NIV)

In Greek culture, "healthy eyes" meant generous, while "unhealthy eyes" meant stingy. This unlocks the passage's meaning.

Abundance Mindset (Healthy Eyes)

Someone with an abundance mindset:

  • Sees the world as plentiful
  • Isn't afraid to be generous or take risks
  • Appreciates what they have rather than fixating on what they lack
  • Fundamentally believes that God owns everything

🎁 Everything Is a Gift

"Until you recognize that all of your life is a gift, you will struggle to do generosity as anything more than religious duty. But when you do recognize that all of your life is a gift, then you will want to bless and be as generous as possible."

Kevin illustrates this with his childhood: when his dad gave him and his brother money for their mom's birthday, they went all out—fancy restaurants, cashmere scarves—because it wasn't their money. Now as an adult paying with his own money, he gives "free hug" coupons. When it's not our money, we're more generous.

Scarcity Mindset (Unhealthy Eyes)

Someone with a scarcity mindset:

  • Isn't appreciative of what they have
  • Constantly focuses on what they don't have yet
  • Can never have enough wealth, comfort, or security
  • Often stems from past experiences of poverty or loss

Jesus warns: "If your eyes are unhealthy, your whole body will be full of darkness." This darkness isn't trivial—it's dangerous and corrupting.

A Monopoly study found that players given more money at the start became meaner, more manipulative, and bullying. The conclusion: "Objectively, money makes people mean."

History confirms this—the Atlantic slave trade, social media's mental health crisis—all driven ultimately by greed and profit at the expense of human wellbeing.

Part 2: The Culprit — Mammon, the Rival God 💰 ▼

"No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other, or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money."

Matthew 6:24 (NIV)

The word "money" here is the Aramaic word Mammon—the name of a god. Matthew preserved this word because he knew its specific nuance: Money isn't just a resource; it's a rival deity.

Matthew, a former tax collector, understood money's pull better than anyone. We face a binary choice: be devoted to money or despise it.

What Does "Despising Money" Mean?

It doesn't mean disgust every time you see your paycheck. Rather, imagine being filled with dread when receiving money because:

  • You know money's controlling nature
  • You're cautious around it because you know its dangers
  • You recognize its corrupting power

This cautious, wary attitude is what Jesus calls "despising" money. The alternative is devotion—and it spirals quickly.

📈 The "Just a Little More" Trap

First job → apartment → bigger apartment → marriage and kids → two bedrooms → third bedroom for a study → parking spot → buy instead of rent → second property → third property...

"Before you know it, Mammon has you in its clutches. That feeling of 'just a little more' keeps you coming back over and over to grovel at the altar of mammon."

So when the charity auctioneer asks for $10,000, you clutch your wallet because your dreams of "just a little more" feel more important than fighting poverty.

Part 3: The Solution — Generosity as a Weapon ⚔️ ▼

Once we recognize that devotion to money brings pain and destruction, we'll want to fight against it. The best weapon is generosity.

"Reframe the way you think about generosity. Not just a duty, not just a tool, but a weapon."

There's a difference between doing something because you're told to versus doing it because you want to and understand its full benefits. It's like going to the gym:

  • If your wife forces you and you don't know why, you won't return
  • If you understand how each exercise works and see results, you'll keep going
  • But if you look for results in the wrong place (bicep curls for a six-pack), you'll get discouraged

Many of us have that relationship with giving—we think it leads to more blessing, provision, or favor. While those may happen, the target result is freedom from mammon's mastery.

"Bring the whole tithe into the storehouse, that there may be food in my house. Test me in this, says the Lord Almighty, and see if I will not throw open the floodgates of heaven and pour out so much blessing that there will not be room enough to store it."

Malachi 3:10 (NIV)

Kevin suggests that embedded in God's "blessing" is freedom from enslavement to money.

Practical Steps: Test God in This

🌱 Start Small, Ramp Up

1. Start giving in small ways: $5 to a beggar. Notice anything in your heart?

2. Ramp it up: $10, $20, $50, $100, $500, your whole wallet. Do you notice your devotion to money fading? Is the "just a little more" narrative quieting?

3. Continue ramping up: Give abundantly to friends in need, beyond basic needs. Give to strangers. Give sacrificially to the church, NGOs, missionaries.

4. Pay attention to the target result: Do you sense greater peace and contentment? Is money's mastery over your life lessening?

Every act of generosity chips away at money's mastery over our lives.

Conclusion: Why 25% of Jesus' Teachings? 🙏 ▼

Over 25% of Jesus' teachings focused on money and generosity. Kevin admits only 1% of his own sermons have touched on this topic. When there's such a discrepancy, we must stop and re-evaluate.

Why did Jesus care so much about this?

  • Not just to support church ministries (though important)
  • Not just to help us understand we're stewards, not owners
  • Because Jesus knew money's power to enslave and corrupt us
"It's easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to get into heaven." — Jesus

Jesus knows generosity is the greatest weapon against money's pervasive force, and He wants us to wield it.

🛡️ Recognizing the Stronghold

Money is a stronghold for many of us—so powerful we don't even recognize it. It has brought destruction, fractured relationships, and strained connections. Some have been on the receiving end; some have committed these wrongs themselves.

It's time to repent and stop serving at the altar of this false god.

The sermon closes with a prayer of repentance and surrender, acknowledging God's grace and love, and submitting all resources—including money—to Him.

Practical Application

🎯 The Big Shift: From Duty to Weapon

Stop viewing generosity as merely a religious obligation. Instead, see it as your primary weapon against money's enslaving power. Each act of giving is a strike against mammon's grip on your heart.

Step 1: Assess Your Mindset 🧠 ▼

Ask yourself these questions:

  • Do I view my possessions as gifts from God, or as things I've earned?
  • Am I constantly thinking "just a little more" about money and possessions?
  • When opportunities to give arise, do I feel excited or anxious?
  • Do I appreciate what I have, or fixate on what I lack?
  • Can I give joyfully, or only out of obligation?

💡 Remember

Your honest answers reveal whether you lean toward an abundance or scarcity mindset. There's no condemnation—just awareness that leads to change.

Step 2: Identify Your "Just a Little More" Pattern 🔄 ▼

Write down your financial/material progression over the last 5-10 years:

  • What did you think would make you content back then?
  • When you achieved it, did you feel satisfied?
  • What did the goalpost shift to next?
  • Can you identify the repeating pattern of "just a little more"?
"That feeling of just a little more keeps you coming back over and over to grovel at the altar of mammon." — Pastor Kevin

Action: Name this pattern specifically in your life. Awareness is the first step to breaking free.

Step 3: Test God — Start Small and Ramp Up 🌱 ▼

Week 1-2: Micro-Generosity

Give $5-$10 to beggars, service workers, or people in need you encounter. Pay close attention to your heart. Do you feel reluctance? Joy? Anxiety? Relief?

Week 3-4: Increasing Stakes

Increase amounts: $20, $50, $100. Notice if the "just a little more" voice in your head gets quieter. Does parting with money feel less painful?

Week 5-8: Sacrificial Giving

Give beyond what's comfortable:

  • Support a friend's need abundantly—beyond what they asked for
  • Give anonymously to a stranger facing hardship
  • Increase your church giving sacrificially
  • Support a missionary or NGO meaningfully

🎯 The Target Result to Watch For

  • Is your relationship with money changing?
  • Do you feel less mastered by it?
  • Is there greater peace and contentment in your life?
  • Do you experience more joy in giving?
  • Has the scarcity mindset begun to shift?
Step 4: Cultivate "Despising" Money 💭 ▼

Practice viewing money with healthy caution:

  • When receiving your paycheck: Pause and acknowledge both its usefulness and its danger. Thank God for the provision while recognizing money's corrupting potential.
  • Before major purchases: Ask yourself, "Am I buying this to meet a genuine need, or to satisfy the 'just a little more' voice?"
  • When tempted to hoard: Remember that you can't take it with you. What eternal impact could this money have?
  • In moments of financial anxiety: Remind yourself that God owns everything and you are merely a steward.
"You can either be devoted to money or you can despise money. Despising money means being filled with dread when you receive it because you know its controlling nature. You're cautious around money because you know its dangers."
Step 5: Make Generosity a Regular Rhythm 🔄 ▼

Don't let generosity be sporadic. Build it into your life:

Weekly Practices:

  • Set aside a specific percentage for giving before you spend on anything else
  • Look for one unexpected opportunity to be generous each week
  • Practice "over-tipping" service workers

Monthly Practices:

  • Review your giving and reflect on how your heart is changing
  • Consider supporting a new cause or person in need
  • Pray for God to show you where He wants you to give

Yearly Practices:

  • Evaluate your overall financial priorities
  • Consider increasing your giving percentage
  • Make one major sacrificial gift that requires faith
Step 6: Accountability and Community 👥 ▼

This battle against mammon is too important to fight alone:

  • Find an accountability partner: Share your struggles with money and your goals for generosity
  • Be honest in your small group: Money is one of the last taboos in Christian community. Break that silence.
  • Learn from generous people: Ask someone you admire for their generosity to share their journey
  • Teach others: Share what you're learning about freedom through generosity

⚠️ Remember

Money is a stronghold for many of us—so powerful we don't even recognize it. Don't underestimate its grip or try to break free alone.

✝️ The Gospel Connection

At its core, generosity flows from understanding the gospel. God didn't give us "just enough" for salvation—He gave us His Son. He gave abundantly, sacrificially, and generously. When we truly grasp this extravagant grace, we can't help but give to others.

"We love because he first loved us." (1 John 4:19) We give because He first gave to us.
