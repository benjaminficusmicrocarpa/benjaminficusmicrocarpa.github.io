📋 Sermon Overview

Central Theme: Palm Sunday commemorates Jesus' entry into Jerusalem — but it was far from the purely joyous celebration we imagine. As the crowds waved palm branches and sang Hosanna, Jesus wept over the city. Pastor Kevin lifts the veil on what was going through Jesus' mind: Jerusalem had willfully turned a blind eye to his teachings, and their rejection of the Prince of Peace would have devastating consequences. The solution is not mere knowledge but active obedience — specifically, dying to self.

Series Context: This is the fifth and final sermon in "The Way" — a Lent series tracing the life and teaching of Jesus. Week 1: Ordinary People. Week 2: The Temptation of Jesus. Week 3: Mercy, Not Sacrifice. Week 4: The Upside-Down Kingdom. This week: the Triumphal Entry — Jesus weeps over Jerusalem's willful blindness and calls us to die to self.

🌟 Main Takeaways

  1. The Triumphal Entry wasn't triumphal for Jesus — While the crowds celebrated, Jesus wept. There was a massive disparity between how Jerusalem felt and how Jesus felt.
  2. Jesus rode a donkey to announce a kingdom of peace — Not primarily to fulfil prophecy or demonstrate humility, but to declare that his reign would bring shalom — everything as it should be.
  3. Jerusalem rejected the real offer — They wanted a political messiah who would overthrow Rome. They got the Prince of Peace offering a kingdom of shalom, and they didn't want it.
  4. Both groups in Jerusalem rejected Jesus — The Pharisees denied him outright; the crowds celebrated him for the wrong reasons. Both amounted to rejection.
  5. Willful blindness is our great tragedy — We intentionally suppress awareness of truth when the cost of change is too great. We do it with finances, relationships, health — and most devastatingly, with Jesus' teachings.
  6. The solution is dying to self — Releasing the desire to have things your way. Not trying to become like Jesus through legalism, but surrendering your comfort, pride, and preferences.
  7. Dying to self doesn't save you — faith does — Salvation is by grace through faith in Jesus Christ alone. But genuine faith is expressed through obedience, and obedience can be simplified to: die to self.

📜 The Foundation: The Parable of the Ten Coins

Before Jesus entered Jerusalem, he told a parable that frames everything that follows. A master goes away to be anointed king and gives each of his servants ten coins. When he returns, the first servant presents ten additional coins — "Well done." The second presents five — "Well done." But the final servant presents nothing. He hid the coins because, he says, "Master, I know that you are a hard man."

His excuse? The standards were too high. He didn't even try. And Jesus' response is startling:

📖 Luke 19:26-27 (NIV)
"He replied, 'I tell you that to everyone who has, more will be given, but as for the one who has nothing, even what they have will be taken away. But those enemies of mine who did not want me to be king over them — bring them here and kill them in front of me.'"
Probably the two most chilling verses in the New Testament. The message: there are people who do not want Jesus as their king — and their rejection is characterised by not doing what he told them to do.

It's after these ominous words that Jesus turns his attention toward Jerusalem. Everything he's just taught — about rejected kingship, about willful disobedience, about severe consequences — forms the foundation of his grief as he enters the city.

🫏 Why a Donkey? The Kingdom of Peace

Traditionally, two reasons are offered for why Jesus rode a donkey into Jerusalem. Pastor Kevin examined both and found them insufficient as the primary explanation:

❌ Theory 1: Fulfilment of Prophecy

📖 Zechariah 9:9 (NIV)
"Rejoice greatly, Daughter Zion, shout, Daughter Jerusalem. See, your king comes to you, righteous and victorious, lowly and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey."
The full Zechariah 9 prophecy describes a Messiah who is lowly, afflicted, and must endure suffering. If the crowds understood this, the tone would have been far more somber.

The gospel writers certainly knew this prophecy — that's why they give so much detail about obtaining the donkey. But the question is whether the people of Jerusalem knew. Bible commentator Michael Steed argues they did not: the joyful celebration is inconsistent with understanding a prophecy about a suffering, afflicted Messiah.

❌ Theory 2: Demonstration of Humility

Many kings of Israel rode donkeys — not as a sign of humility, but on momentous occasions. King David rode a donkey fleeing from Saul. King Absalom rode one into battle. Most tellingly, King Solomon rode a mule into Jerusalem on the day of his inauguration.

✅ The Real Message: Shalom

These kings rode donkeys because the donkey represents peace. Their reign would bring peace. And Jesus does the same — his primary message in riding the donkey is: the kingdom of peace is at hand.

Shalom (שָׁלוֹם): Not merely the absence of conflict, but everything as it should be. A restoration of harmony, hope, and purpose. Relationships no longer characterised by selfishness and pride. Children getting to be children. Systems of government operating benevolently. Our relationship with God the Father finally restored.

And Jesus knows that ultimately, this is not what Israel wants. They want a military messiah who will crush Rome. They want political salvation, not spiritual peace.

I offered my roommate a beautiful, nutritious salad — mixed greens, walnuts, apples, blue cheese, avocado, the works. And he said, "How about a cheeseburger instead?" The people of Jerusalem kept longing for the cheeseburger version of the kingdom. And because of that, they rejected the true offer of a kingdom of peace.

— Pastor Kevin

The Irony: "Jerusalem" in Hebrew — Yerushalayim (יְרוּשָׁלַיִם) — means "foundation of peace" or "city of peace." The city of peace rejected the Prince of Peace and his offer of a kingdom of peace.

🙈 Willful Blindness

Willful blindness is intentionally suppressing awareness of truth that demands costly change.

— Pastor Kevin's working definition

Jerusalem had access to Jesus' teachings — 15 to 20 parables just on the nature of the kingdom of God. They didn't miss it. They willingly turned a blind eye to the truth of Jesus. And we do the same thing.

Pastor Kevin illustrated this with a relatable scenario: getting a WhatsApp from your doctor. The banner notification reads, "Kevin, I am really concerned about your…" — and you don't open it. Because if you do, the two blue check marks appear, and now you're accountable. So you pretend you never saw it.

Money

An absolute statement with no grey area. But we toss it aside: "That's just a hyper-spiritual statement." And we pursue money and justify it because it puts food on the plate.

Before you even worship God, resolve your resentment. We suppress this truth every day: "If I resolved all my conflicts, I might never get to church."

🏦
Finances
Ignoring the bank statement

You know you've overspent. You know you should look. But you suppress awareness because the cost of change — cutting back — feels too high.

💔
Toxic Relationships
Suppressing community concern

Dysfunction obvious to everyone around you, but you retreat into isolation. "You don't see the good parts that I see." Willful blindness under the banner of love.

🍔
Health
"I'll start the diet after this one last steak"

You've been saying "one last time" for five years. You know the truth, but the cost of change keeps you blind.

📖
The Parable Servant
"I knew your standards were too high."

The last servant turned a blind eye to what the master said. His excuse: the expectations were impossible. Isn't that why we ignore Jesus' teachings too?

The great tragedy is not that truth is hidden from us. The great tragedy is that we actively participate in our own blindness. Jerusalem had access to Jesus' teachings and the conviction of the Holy Spirit — and they chose not to perceive.

✝️ The Solution: Dying to Self

If the problem is willful blindness — suppressing truth because the cost of change is too great — then the solution is to obey the truth. But Pastor Kevin simplified this further: focus on dying to self.

Our work isn't to try to become like Jesus. Our work is more difficult than that. It is more fruitful and freeing. Our work is to die to self. There is a canyon-sized gap between trying to become like Jesus and dying to self. One leads to legalism, bondage, and exhaustion. But dying to self leads to the freedom offered by Jesus.

— Steve Cuss

The nuance: we do want to become like Jesus. But when that's your sole focus, it leads to legalism and spiritual burnout. Much better to focus on releasing the desire to have things your way — surrendering comfort, pride, preference. When you die to self, Christlikeness becomes second nature. You naturally drift toward generosity, compassion, kindness, mercy, self-control.

🪢 The Rope Analogy: Grace, Faith, and Action

Even if you successfully practised dying to self every single day, that will not save you. Salvation is always by grace through faith in Jesus Christ alone:

  • Grace: The unmerited gift of God. You cannot work for it, achieve it, or warrant it.
  • Faith: The means of receiving that grace. Picture yourself hanging off a cliff. Someone throws a rope. Faith is reaching up and grasping that rope — not to pull yourself up, but trusting that the rope and the person holding it will save you.
  • In Jesus Christ alone: The exclusivity of the Christian message. Not faith in good works, morality, humanity, or technology.

But faith is not just mental assent — looking at the rope and thinking, "Yeah, I believe it will save me" while doing nothing. Faith is commitment to a belief through action.

📖 James 2:17 (NIV)
"In the same way, faith by itself, if it is not accompanied by action, is dead."
The greatest expression of faith — of reaching up and grasping that rope — is obedience to all Christ has taught. And that obedience can be simplified to: die to self.

Dying to self is the act of grasping the rope. Surrender your comfort so money has no hold on you. Surrender your pride so you can forgive easily. Surrender your preference so you can follow Jesus wherever he leads. It is commitment to a belief. It is the action that solidifies your faith.

🏛️ Historical Context

Pastor Kevin's sermon draws on rich historical background to illuminate the Triumphal Entry. Understanding the symbolism of the donkey, the scholarly debate over Zechariah, and the devastating fulfilment of Jesus' prophecy helps us feel the weight of this passage.

🫏 The Donkey in Israelite Royal Tradition

In the ancient Near East, a king riding a horse or chariot signified war; riding a donkey signified peace. Several Israelite kings rode donkeys on significant occasions:

  • King David — Rode a donkey while fleeing from Saul, during a vulnerable moment, not as a war posture
  • King Absalom — Rode a donkey (mule) into battle, a royal mount
  • King Solomon — Rode a mule into Jerusalem on the day of his inauguration (1 Kings 1:33-40), signalling his peaceful reign
  • King Jesus — Rode a donkey into Jerusalem, declaring: the kingdom of peace is at hand

The donkey was not a sign of lowliness — it was a royal peace symbol. Jesus' choice was a deliberate proclamation.

📜 The Zechariah 9 Debate

Scholars are divided on whether the crowds understood the Zechariah 9:9 prophecy. Pastor Kevin cited Michael Steed, who wrote a full-length study on the fulfilment of Zechariah and argued that the crowds did not know the prophecy:

  • The full prophecy (Zechariah 9) describes a Messiah who is lowly and afflicted and must endure a path of opposition and suffering to achieve salvation
  • The joyful, celebratory response of the crowds is inconsistent with understanding a suffering-Messiah prophecy
  • If the crowds understood, there would have been a far more somber tone — recognition that this king rides toward suffering, not victory
  • The donkey detail was included by the gospel writers for us as readers to recognise the prophetic fulfilment

Two possibilities: either the crowds misunderstood the paradox of the prophecy, or — more likely — they simply didn't know it.

🔥 The Fall of Jerusalem — AD 70

Jesus' prophecy in Luke 19:43-44 was fulfilled literally, only forty years after his death and resurrection. The destruction of Jerusalem by Rome remains one of the most devastating events in Jewish history.

May AD 70
Rome begins its siege of Jerusalem under General Titus. Earthen embankments are constructed around the city — exactly as Jesus prophesied: "Your enemies will build an embankment against you."
Siege Wall
The Romans build a wall of earth around the city to prevent inhabitants from escaping — "encircle you and hem you in on every side." The goal: starvation.
Outer Wall Falls — 15 Days
The outer wall of Jerusalem falls within 15 days of the siege beginning.
Inner Wall Falls — 5 Days Later
The inner wall falls five days after the outer wall. This begins a period of unparalleled suffering for Jerusalem's inhabitants.
500 Crucifixions Per Day
Roman soldiers crucified captured Jews at a rate of 500 per day during the siege. The total death toll: approximately 1.1 million Jews.
Temple Dismantled Stone by Stone
Roman soldiers dismantled every stone of the temple, believing gold was hidden between the cracks — "They will not leave one stone on another."
Caesar's Decree
Historian Josephus records that Caesar decreed the entire city and temple be completely razed to the ground — so that future generations would never know people had lived there.
The precision of fulfilment: Embankments, encirclement, hemmed in on every side, dashed to the ground, not one stone on another — every detail of Jesus' prophecy was realised within a generation. This is not allegory; it is history.

📚 Parable Context: The Ten Minas

The Parable of the Ten Coins (also called the Parable of the Ten Minas) in Luke 19:11-27 was told specifically because "the people thought that the kingdom of God was going to appear at once" (Luke 19:11). Jesus needed to correct their expectation that his arrival in Jerusalem would immediately bring political liberation.

The parable's master goes away to be appointed king — rejected by citizens who send a delegation saying, "We don't want this man to be our king." When he returns, those who invested are rewarded, and those who rejected his reign face judgment. The parallels to Jesus' own journey are unmistakable: departure (death), return (second coming), accountability, and judgment on those who refused him.

🎯 Living Without Willful Blindness

The call is clear: stop suppressing the truth that demands costly change. Start dying to self. This is not legalism — it's the freeing act of grasping the rope of faith. Here are practical ways to begin.

📱
Open the Message

What truth have you been avoiding? Like the unread WhatsApp from the doctor, identify one area where you've been suppressing awareness. This week, "open the message." Face it. Let the blue check marks appear.

💰
Surrender Your Comfort

"You cannot serve both God and money." Where is money — or the security it represents — competing with God for first place in your life? Dying to self means loosening your grip so money has no hold on you.

🤝
Resolve the Conflict

Jesus said to resolve your issues before coming to worship. Is there a broken relationship you've been avoiding? Dying to self means surrendering your pride and taking the first step toward reconciliation.

🪢
Grasp the Rope

Faith is not passive agreement — it's active commitment. Identify one teaching of Jesus you've been intellectually accepting but practically ignoring. This week, act on it. That's grasping the rope.

🕊️
Release Your Preferences

Dying to self means not needing to be seen a certain way or treated a certain way. Love without condition, without expectation of being loved back. When you do, you naturally become generous, compassionate, kind.

🌿
Receive the Peace Offering

Jesus rides in offering shalom — everything as it should be. Don't demand the "cheeseburger" version of the kingdom. Receive what he's actually offering: peace with God, peace with each other, peace in a broken world.
