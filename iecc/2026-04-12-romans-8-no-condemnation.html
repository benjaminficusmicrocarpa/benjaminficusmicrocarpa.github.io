📋 Sermon Overview

Central Theme: Romans 8 opens with one of the most liberating declarations in all of Scripture: "There is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." Pastor Brett explores what condemnation meant for Paul — a man haunted by his murderous past — and what it means for us today. Through personal stories, the woman caught in adultery, and a parable of a king who took the punishment for his daughter, we see that God's justice and love meet perfectly at the cross.

Series Launch: This is Week 1 of a seven-week deep dive into Romans 8 — arguably the most theologically weighty chapter in the entire New Testament. Paul uses the word "Spirit" or "Holy Spirit" 21 times in this chapter alone. Each week the congregation will leave feeling "full and satisfied" with rich biblical truth.

🌟 Main Takeaways

  1. Two laws at war — The "law of the Spirit who gives life" sets believers free from the "law of sin and death." The old system could only expose unrighteousness; the new one produces life.
  2. Paul's honest confession — In Romans 7, Paul describes the universal experience of wanting to do right but doing wrong. He couldn't go a single day without failing his own standard — and neither can we.
  3. Condemnation is our default — Guilt, shame, regret, imposter syndrome — these are the accuser's tools. Revelation 12:10 says he accuses believers before God day and night, 24/7.
  4. The law reveals guilt but can't produce righteousness — The Jewish people had 600+ laws and couldn't fulfil them. A legalistic "try harder, be better" approach ends only in discouragement.
  5. God's solution is a person, not a programme — Paul's cry was not "What will rescue me?" but "Who will rescue me?" God sent his Son in the likeness of sinful flesh to accomplish what the law could not.
  6. Jesus' balance of truth and grace — In John 8, Jesus neither dismisses the woman's sin nor condemns her. "Go and leave your life of sin" — the act was wrong, and you can live in freedom.
  7. The king and the lashes — A parable of justice and love: the king's daughter stole from the treasury, and the king took the 40 lashes upon himself. Justice prevails; love is pronounced.

😖 Paul's Honest Confession

Romans 8 is arguably the most important, theologically weighty chapter of the entire Bible. It begins with a declaration — "There is now no condemnation" — and ends with a promise — "Nothing can separate you from the love of Christ." The implication of "now" is that there was condemnation. There used to be condemnation, but there's a point in time when there was none anymore.

The purpose of the Spirit of God is to remind you that nothing can separate you from the love of God.

— Tim Keller

Paul sets the stage in chapter 7 with what reads like a brutally honest journal entry. Remember, Paul had a past — before becoming a Christian, he was a persecutor of Christians. He murdered people for their faith. Those memories haunted him, and he writes:

📖 Romans 7:15 (NIV)
"I do not understand what I do. For what I want to do I do not do, but what I hate I do."
Paul describes a universal human experience — the gap between intention and action. He knew what was right but couldn't consistently do it. Even his own standards proved impossible to keep.

Pastor Brett shared a personal experience: a creeping criticalness had entered his life — quick to be harsh, judgmental, seeing the negative. He made a secret promise to God to go an entire day without a single critical thought. He couldn't do it. By noon, he'd already failed. Day after day, the promise was renewed, and day after day it was broken.

📖 Romans 7:24 (NIV)
"What a wretched man I am! Who will rescue me from this body that is subject to death?"
Paul's cry is a "who," not a "what." The solution is not to work harder, have more discipline, or more accountability. The solution is a person.

👿 The Accuser's Strategy

Most of us carry guilt, shame, and regret — things we wish we hadn't done or said, activities we wish we could blot out. The enemy of your soul specialises in accusing you.

📖 Revelation 12:10 (NIV)
"For the accuser of our brothers and sisters, who accuses them before our God day and night, has been hurled down."
The accuser never closes shop. 24/7 he whispers reminders of your failures, your inconsistencies, your shortcomings — bringing about shame.
The Accuser's Two-Pronged Strategy:
  • To believers: "You've done so much wrong. Let's review the tape. You're so inconsistent. You think you're generous? Are you really?"
  • To unbelievers: "You've done nothing wrong. You don't need a saviour. Your good outweighs your bad. You're fine."

Different messaging, same goal: pull you away from the truth that God loves you — full stop.

📜 The Law Cannot Save You

📖 Romans 8:1-2 (NIV)
"Therefore, there is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus, because through Christ Jesus the law of the Spirit who gives life has set you free from the law of sin and death."
Paul uses "law" twice, juxtaposed and diametrically opposed: the law of the Spirit gives life; the law of sin and death brings guilt and shame. Paul will use the term "Spirit" or "Holy Spirit" 21 times in Romans 8.

The Jewish people had over 600 specific laws — dietary, clothing, ceremonial, hand-washing, laws for everything. And yet they couldn't fulfil them. Pastor Brett's Jewish neighbour, asked which law was hardest, answered: "On the Sabbath — without thinking, I just turn on a light." Because someone's working to make the electricity flow, and you can't force someone else to work on the Sabbath.

The law doesn't make you guilty. I was guilty when I sped. When I got caught, it just revealed my guilt. The law simply reveals guilt. On a spiritual macro sense, that's what Paul is saying — the law reveals the guilt of every single human being, and it cannot produce righteousness.

— Pastor Brett

Pastor Brett shared his two "Tacon Driving School" certificates — both earned by being "invited" by police officers after accumulating too many traffic points. Both times he slunk into a room of fifteen other people, and both times someone said, "Pastor Brett?" The law condemned his speeding, and the course was the price to keep driving. A humbling, uncomfortable experience of condemnation.

The Legalism Bumper Sticker: A legalistic, religious approach to faith simply says: Try harder. Be better. The result? Be discouraged. Because you can't. You can't be good enough. You can't try hard enough. You're going to fail.

✝️ What God Did

📖 Romans 8:3-4 (NIV)
"For what the law was powerless to do because it was weakened by the flesh, God did by sending his own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh to be a sin offering. And so he condemned sin in the flesh, in order that the righteous requirement of the law might be fully met in us, who do not live according to the flesh but according to the Spirit."
God sent his Son — completely righteous, in the likeness of sinful flesh. Not actual sinful flesh, but looking like sin, taking on sin. The payment for your sin and mine. And God condemned sin — rendered it powerless — so that the righteous requirement of the law is met in us.

🪝 The Offering Box Story

Pastor Brett showed a handle with sticky tape on the end — found inside a church offering box. Someone had put sticky tape on a rod and tried to extract money from the offering box. Kudos for the creativity, but it was theft — from God, no less. And even for that? "There is no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." Even this is forgiven. Because even the smallest infraction is worthy of condemnation, since God's standard is perfection.

👩 The Woman Caught in Adultery

In John 8, a woman is dragged before Jesus at dawn. Religious leaders say she was caught in the act of adultery — which takes two people, but they brought only one. They ask Jesus: "The law says she should be stoned. What do you say?"

Jesus bends down and writes in the dirt. Scholars speculate: maybe the seventh commandment, maybe names of men who'd been with her. Then he stands and says:

📖 John 8:7, 9-11 (NIV)
"Let any one of you who is without sin be the first to throw a stone at her." … Those who heard began to go away one at a time, the older ones first, until only Jesus was left with the woman still standing there. Jesus straightened up and asked her, "Woman, where are they? Has no one condemned you?" "No one, sir," she said. "Then neither do I condemn you," Jesus declared. "Go now and leave your life of sin."
He didn't back away from the fact that she was in sin. He called her out: "This is a life of sin. Leave that." But he also said, "I don't condemn you." The act was wrong, and you can live in freedom.

⚖️ Three Approaches to Sin

🌐
Progressive Modern View

"You haven't really sinned. I'm very tolerant, accepting, open-minded. You do you. You live your truth."

⛪
Conservative Religious View

"You have sinned, and I do judge you." People don't say it, but they do it — unfortunately, quite often within religious circles.

✝️
Jesus' Approach

"Yes, that is sin. There is moral right and moral wrong. But you're not condemned. Go and leave your life of sin."

👑 The King and the Lashes

Imagine a king in a faraway land — the best king. Generous, kind, loving, caring for all his citizens. Known to be fair and just, ruling never with favoritism but by justice.

One day, gold is stolen from the king's treasury. An investigation ensues, and to everyone's dismay, it was the daughter of the king. Now he's caught in an unimaginable situation.

The executioner listens as the king declares justice will prevail: forty lashes across the back — a death sentence. They take the daughter and prepare her for the lashes. And just as the executioner is about to begin, the king interrupts. He goes and places his own body on top of his daughter's, removes his own shirt, and says to the executioner: "Begin."

The king takes the lashes that the daughter deserves, and justice prevails while his love is pronounced. God is loving and just. He sends his Son to receive the punishment so that his justice isn't diminished but his love can remain pronounced.

— Pastor Brett

God has a quandary: his character — holy, perfect, righteous — cannot in any way be diminished in his effort to accept imperfect, sinful people. And so he sends a perfect atonement for our sin in Jesus, so that we can be reunited to a perfect Heavenly Father.

📖 Romans 8:1, 4 (NIV)
"Therefore, there is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus … in order that the righteous requirement of the law might be fully met in us, who do not live according to the flesh but according to the Spirit."
Guilty as we are, undeserving as we are, we still get to experience the acceptance and love of a God who made us and gave his own Son to restore us.

⚖️ Law vs Grace: The Heart of Romans 8

Pastor Brett drew a sharp contrast between two systems — the law that exposes guilt and the grace that sets free. Understanding this contrast is essential to grasping why Paul is so excited about "no condemnation."

📜 The Law: What It Can and Cannot Do

What the law CAN do:
  • Reveal God's moral standard
  • Expose unrighteousness — show us the gap between perfection and ourselves
  • Reveal guilt (Romans 7:7 — "I would not have known what sin was had it not been for the law")
  • Function like a mirror — showing us who we really are
What the law CANNOT do:
  • Produce righteousness — it was "powerless" (Romans 8:3)
  • Remove guilt or condemnation
  • Change the human heart
  • Be fully kept — even Paul, even practising Jews with 600+ laws, couldn't do it

Pastor Brett's Jewish neighbour on Robinson Road illustrated this perfectly. He lived across from the synagogue so he could walk on the Sabbath (no public transport — you can't force someone else to work). Yet the hardest law? "Without thinking, I just turn on a light." Even the seemingly simplest laws trip us up.

🕊️ Grace: What God Accomplished

Paul's language in Romans 8:3 is precise: God sent his Son "in the likeness of sinful flesh" — not actual sinful flesh, but in the likeness. Jesus came as man, looking like sin, but not sin. He took on sin as payment.

And then Paul uses the word "condemned" again — but this time, it's sin that is condemned, not us. God condemned sin in the flesh. The effect of sin in the life of a believer is rendered powerless.

📋
Under the Law

Try harder. Be better. Be discouraged. Because you can't. You can't be good enough.

🕊️
Under Grace

No condemnation. Set free. Walk by the Spirit. Righteous requirement fully met in you.

🚦 The Driving Certificate Analogy

Pastor Brett received two certificates from the Tacon Driving School — not for good driving, but for too many infractions. Twice he was "invited" by police officers after accumulating enough points. The course was the cost of keeping his licence.

Key insight: guilt didn't occur because of the law. He was guilty when he sped. When he got caught, it simply revealed his guilt. On a spiritual macro scale, that's what Paul is saying: the law reveals the guilt of every human being. It doesn't make us guilty — we already are.

And the certificate? It's a bit like what the cross offers — a way to keep driving, to keep living, not because you've earned it, but because a price was paid. Only the price Christ paid is infinitely greater than a Sunday course in Admiralty.

🤴 The King Parable — Unpacked

The parable of the king and his daughter captures the theological tension at the heart of the gospel:

  • The king represents God — loving, generous, kind, but also fair and just. He cannot rule with favoritism.
  • The daughter represents us — loved by the king, yet guilty of real wrongdoing (stealing from the treasury).
  • The 40 lashes represent justice — God's standard is perfection. Any infraction, even the smallest, warrants eternal separation.
  • The king taking the lashes represents the cross — God places himself between his beloved and the consequences of sin. Justice is served; love is demonstrated.

This is the quandary God faced: his character — holy, perfect, righteous — cannot be diminished by communion with unholy beings. The entrance requirement for fellowship with a holy God is perfection. And so he sends a perfect atonement in Jesus, so that imperfect people can be reunited with their perfect Heavenly Father.

🎯 Living in No Condemnation

Understanding "no condemnation" is not merely intellectual — it's meant to transform how you live, how you view yourself, and how you relate to others. Here are practical steps drawn from the sermon.

🔇
Silence the Accuser

When guilt, shame, or condemnation whispers, recognise its source. The accuser works 24/7 to remind you of your failures. Counter every accusation with Romans 8:1 — "There is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus." That is the truth, not the whisper.

🪞
Be Honest Like Paul

Paul didn't pretend to have it all together. He confessed, "I do what I hate." Spiritual maturity isn't the absence of struggle — it's the honesty to name it. What is your "I do what I hate"? Bring it into the light.

🚫
Stop the Try-Harder Trap

A legalistic approach — try harder, be better — only produces discouragement. The gospel is not a self-improvement programme. The righteous requirement of the law is met by Christ, not by your effort. Rest in what's been accomplished.

⚖️
Hold Truth and Grace Together

Jesus told the woman: "That is sin — leave it. And I don't condemn you." Don't dismiss sin (the progressive trap). Don't judge others (the religious trap). Name sin honestly while offering the same unconditional acceptance Christ offers you.

🤝
Extend Forgiveness

If God has hurled your iniquities into the depths of the sea, you can extend that same posture to others. Is there someone you need to actively forgive, as Christ has forgiven you? Freedom from condemnation includes freedom to forgive.
