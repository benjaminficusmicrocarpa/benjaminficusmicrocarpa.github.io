Overview

Jesus declared, "I am the door." This powerful metaphor reveals that Jesus is the exclusive gateway to God and to a life of true meaning and abundance. Through the story of a man born blind and Jesus' confrontation with the Pharisees, we discover what it means to enter through the door that is Christ.

John 10:9 (NIV)
"I am the gate; whoever enters through me will be saved. They will come in and go out, and find pasture."

In John 9, Jesus encounters a man born blind. The disciples ask a shocking question: "Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?"

"In Jesus' day, there was a belief that if something bad happened to you or your family, it must be a consequence of somebody's sin‚Äîeither your own or your parents'."

But Jesus breaks this chain of condemnation with powerful words:

üêë The Sheep Pen Metaphor ‚ñº

Jesus then tells a parable about sheep, shepherds, and gates. In ancient times, there were two types of sheep pens:

Type 1: Public Sheep Pen (Urban Setting)

Multiple shepherds use one common pen with a gatekeeper. Each morning, shepherds come and call their sheep by name. The sheep recognize their shepherd's voice and follow him out.

John 10:3-4 (NIV)
"The gatekeeper opens the gate for him, and the sheep listen to his voice. He calls his own sheep by name and leads them out. When he has brought out all his own, he goes on ahead of them, and his sheep follow him because they know his voice."

Application: How do you recognize God's voice? By hearing it often and frequently. This is why we must read the Bible regularly‚Äîto familiarize ourselves with His voice.

Type 2: Stone Sheep Pen (Rural Setting)

In rural areas, a shepherd has his own stone pen with no gate and no gatekeeper. How does he protect the sheep from lions, bears, and wolves?

"The shepherd sleeps at the entrance. He literally lays down his life at the doorway. The shepherd IS the gate."

Biblical Doors: Genesis to Revelation

Pastor Albert takes us on a journey through Scripture, showing how the metaphor of "the door" appears throughout the Bible.

After Adam and Eve sinned, God drove them from the Garden of Eden and placed a cherubim with a flaming sword to block the way to the tree of life.

"Many think this is the door of punishment. But it's actually the door of mercy. God prevented them from eating from the tree of life and living eternally in their sinful state, separated from Him forever."
