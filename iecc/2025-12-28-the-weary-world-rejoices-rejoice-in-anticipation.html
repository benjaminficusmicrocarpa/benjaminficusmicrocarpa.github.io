The Honest Question: Are We Really Eager?

Pastor Kevin confesses: "We're more excited about the next iPhone release than we are about Jesus coming back." He contrasts this with biblical authors like Paul, who wrote of eagerly awaiting Christ's return, and John Calvin, who spoke of hungering for that great day.

"Do the words hunger and eager describe your anticipation of Jesus?"

üîë Ingredient #1: Keep Working

From Matthew 24:36-51, Jesus teaches about the unknown timing of His return through the parable of the faithful and wicked servants.

Matthew 24:44-46 (NIV): "So you also must be ready, because the Son of Man will come at an hour when you do not expect him. Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom the master has put in charge of the servants in his household to give them their food at the proper time? It will be good for that servant whose master finds him doing so when he returns."

Key insight: The faithful servant keeps working because he expects his master to show up at any moment. The wicked servant stops working because he expects his master won't come at all.

What is our work?

Before Jesus ascended, He commanded: "You will be my witnesses" (Acts 1:8). Our task is to:

  • üì¢ Share the gospel with others
  • üíñ Live out the gospel in love, forgiveness, and humility
  • üçû "Feed His sheep" as Jesus instructed Peter
"The more we do it, the more we will long for his return, the more excited we will be for his return. If you want to have a lifelong anticipation for the return of Jesus, do a good job with the task that God has given to you."

üîë Ingredient #2: Preparation

From Matthew 25:1-13, Jesus teaches through the parable of the ten virgins (bridesmaids).

Matthew 25:1-4 (NIV): "At that time the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and went out to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish and five were wise. The foolish ones took their lamps but did not take any oil with them. The wise ones, however, took oil in jars along with their lamps."

The difference? Both groups fell asleep. But the wise had extra oil‚Äîthey were prepared for a delay. The unprepared ones ran out and missed the wedding feast entirely.

Two Hints About Spiritual Preparation:

Hint #1: It's About Your Heart

The bridegroom says to the unprepared: "I don't know you" (Matthew 25:12). This echoes Jesus' warning in Matthew 7:21-23 about those who prophesy and perform miracles in His name, yet Jesus says, "I never knew you."

"You can do a lot of things for God, but not really know him. If you serve on the worship team, you show up and you play these songs, but these songs are just chords to you... You can do all these things and still not know him, and still be spiritually dry."

Hint #2: It Cannot Be Done for You

The wise bridesmaids couldn't share their oil. Spiritual preparation is personal‚Äîit's between you and God alone.

"If you're being dragged here to church by your family or your spouse, that's great. But your internal heart change, your belief in God, that's on you and you alone. If your prayer life is relegated to when the pastor prays at the end of a sermon, you need to develop your own prayer life. You got to get your own oil."

Summary: Spiritual preparation is about your affection for God that cannot be faked by action and cannot be outsourced to another person.

The Affection Analogy: Visiting Mother

Pastor Kevin illustrates with family relationships:

  • Close relationship: Mom visits ‚Üí through the roof with excitement
  • Complicated relationship: Mom visits ‚Üí nervous anticipation
  • Estranged relationship: Mom visits ‚Üí indifference (don't believe she'll actually come)
"Your affection determines your readiness. Your affection for God determines your readiness and your anticipation."

Practical Advice: Quantity Time is Quality Time

Pastor Kevin shares relationship wisdom he received when dating his wife Emily: "Quantity time is quality time." Grand dates are wonderful, but nothing beats daily presence.

Applied to our relationship with God:

"Nothing beats the day in, day out quantity time that you spend with him. What if reading the Bible wasn't just getting through a Bible reading plan? What if you read the Bible to understand God more? What if you paused after getting to the verse of the day on your phone and you asked God, 'Lord, what do you want to show me about yourself?'"

On prayer:

"What if prayer was also a time when you can enjoy his presence? If you've never had a time of prayer where you just stayed silent, not really think anything, and enjoyed your time with God, I'm telling you, you are missing out."

Approach spiritual disciplines with relationship as the goal, not just completion.

Closing Illustration: The Apple Watch

Pastor Kevin confesses his material desire for an Apple Watch Ultra 3 and his love for the research phase‚Äîthe anticipation itself. When his wife suggested delaying the purchase for a future model with better features, she said:

"The greatest gift I can give you this Christmas, Kevin, is more time for anticipation."

The principle: Anticipating the future changes your present. It gives excitement, purpose, direction, and joy.

"When the focus of our anticipation is the coming of Jesus, you will feel all these feelings more profoundly. They will be more long-lasting. If you struggle with loneliness, with emptiness, you're living a life devoid of joy and hope‚Äîcan I invite you to embrace the anticipation of Christ? To let the hope of Jesus ignite in you a perpetual Advent."
