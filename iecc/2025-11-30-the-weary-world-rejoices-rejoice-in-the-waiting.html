üìúMain Sermon

The Season of Advent

This is the first Sunday of Advent, the easiest season in the Christian calendar to understand: four Sundays before Christmas. Advent is the season when we prepare for and wait for Christmas, a time when our weary world can rejoice as we move toward celebrating Christ's birth. The purpose of Advent is to prepare us for Christmas and to help us wait expectantly.

But there's a problem: we hate waiting. Whether it's a late MTR train, a delayed flight, or waiting for someone who's late to a reservation, waiting frustrates us. Yet waiting is exactly what Advent calls us to do.

Israel's Long Wait

Christmas represents the culmination of the Israelite people's waiting for the Messiah. Their history was marked by periods of waiting: waiting for deliverance from Egyptian slavery, grumbling during their forty years in the desert, crying out during the time of the kings, and lamenting through the prophets. The prophet Habakkuk captured this struggle perfectly when he asked God, "How long, Lord, must I call for help? You do not listen."

After the Old Testament prophets, there were 400 years of silence from God. The Israelites waited and waited. Then, unexpectedly, angels appeared to shepherds proclaiming, "Do not be afraid. I bring you good news that will cause great joy for all the people. Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord."

God's timetable is not the same as ours, yet His timing is always perfect.

Mary's Humble Yes

At the heart of this story is a young virgin named Mary. God chose her to conceive and bear the Messiah through divine intervention. When the angel told her this impossible news, she didn't argue, she simply asked, "How will this be?" After receiving God's explanation, she responded with remarkable humility: "My soul glorifies the Lord, and my spirit rejoices in God my Savior, for he has been mindful of the humble state of his servant."

Mary understood something crucial: even though her name would be blessed through generations, the credit belonged entirely to God. "The mighty one has done great things for me. Holy is his name."

We must understand the social cost Mary faced. In her culture, virginity was a badge of honor and moral faithfulness. When people saw she was pregnant and asked about the father, what could she say? "God did this through divine conception"? Most would have thought she'd lost her mind. There was no way society would understand. Yet Mary accepted this hard road, willing to let God work through her despite the personal cost.

Her pregnancy wasn't easy. She had to travel approximately 90 miles (145 kilometers) from Nazareth to Bethlehem while in late-term pregnancy. That's like walking from TST in Hong Kong to Guangzhou City, or from southern Los Angeles to San Diego. We have no details about that difficult journey, only that Joseph helped her along the way.

The Reality of Childbirth

Pastor Rick shared a personal story about his wife Jane's labor with their son David. He had been playing softball, called to check on her, and she said everything was fine. So he went to the pub with his teammates. When he finally got home, her water had broken and she was ready to give birth. In his panic to get to the hospital, he faced arguments about parking, rushed paperwork, and barely made it to the delivery room.

The point of sharing this story is twofold: First, we have no idea how difficult the journey and delivery were for Mary and Joseph. We only know the highlights at each end. This was an awful, weary time for these two young people bringing forth the person of Jesus. Second, the waiting for birth serves as an analogy for understanding the difficulty of all waiting. Women especially know the agony, pain, and exhausting wait that comes with carrying and delivering a child.

The Many Forms of Waiting

Today, we face many different kinds of waiting. Some in our congregation are waiting for solutions after the devastating typhoon in Hong Kong. Others may be waiting for a wedding, enduring the discipline of abstinence. Some are waiting for a painful divorce to finalize, dealing with the financial and legal complexities. People wait for medical test results, for healing after treatment, for financial provision, for job changes, for their difficult boss to leave, or simply for any employment during a season of unemployment.

Whatever we're waiting for, whether big or small, there are principles in Scripture and insights from human understanding that can help us be more positive during the wait. These principles help us rejoice in the waiting, even in our weary world.

üí°Six Ways to Rejoice in the Waiting

These soundbites are founded on Christian principles and help us move from where we are to where God is, transforming our perspective during seasons of waiting.

1. Keep the End in View

When we keep the end in view, we maintain perspective that what we're doing is worthwhile. For believers, this means keeping eternity in view. Our light and momentary troubles are achieving an eternal glory that far outweighs them all. Whatever we're waiting for, whatever difficulty we face, these are light and momentary compared to eternal glory. Fixing our eyes not on what is seen but what is unseen‚Äîsince what is seen is temporary but what is unseen is eternal‚Äîhelps us endure any waiting with hope.

2. Reframe Your Timetable

Sometimes we need to adjust our expectations about timing. Pastor Rick shared how he learned to reframe his experience in hospital waiting rooms. Instead of anxiously watching the number system, growing frustrated at the wait, he reframed the time as an opportunity to be with his wife. The wait became a gift rather than a burden. When we stop fixating on our preferred timeline and accept the present moment, we can find peace and even joy in the waiting itself.

3. Lament Hard Realities to God

As believers, we're entitled to acknowledge hard realities and voice them to God. Lamenting doesn't mean complaining to others endlessly; it means bringing our honest struggles directly to God. The book of Lamentations models this: "My endurance is gone... I remember my affliction and my soul is downcast within me. Yet this I call to mind and therefore I have hope." When we lament to God, He either reminds us He understands our pain or shows us perspectives we're missing. This practice builds our belief in God's character and helps us persevere.

4. Search for Blessings

Even in difficult seasons, we can find unexpected gifts. Pastor Rick shared how when his wife was recovering from a stroke and couldn't do crossword puzzles alone anymore, helping her became a daily blessing that brought them closer together. Sometimes the search for blessings requires intentionality, but those small discoveries of goodness can sustain us through long waits and bring more joy to our present circumstances.

5. Connect with Love

This isn't just about receiving God's love (though that's vital), but about actively giving love to others. There's a paradoxical truth: when we unselfishly help others, our own spirits improve. Scripture tells us to value others above ourselves, looking not only to our own interests but to the interests of others. Hong Kong has demonstrated this beautifully in response to recent tragedy‚Äîpeople connecting with strangers out of love. This outward focus, this active compassion, lifts our own hearts even as we wait through our own difficulties.

6. Know Whose You Are

This isn't about knowing who you are (your identity) or who God is (His character), though both matter. It's about knowing whose you are‚Äîunderstanding your belonging. You are adopted and welcomed into God's loving family. You are beloved by Jesus Christ Himself. Mary understood this profoundly when she said, "The Sovereign Lord is my strength." When we remember whose we are, that we belong to God and Christ, it transforms our waiting from endurance into worship. Worship changes us‚Äîit doesn't change God, but it moves us from where we are to where God is, giving us His perspective on our circumstances.

All of us are waiting for something. Consider these common areas of waiting that people in our congregation might be experiencing:

This Advent Season

Advent is a time to wait and prepare for Christmas with purpose. Use this season intentionally:

Center on Christ: Like the church practiced through communion in this service, make Jesus the centerplace of your church community, your personal life, and certainly your Christmas celebration.

Practice Waiting with Worship: Instead of letting waiting frustrate you, let it become an opportunity for worship. As Martin Radford said, "Worship changes us. It doesn't change God. Rejoicing moves us from where we are to where God is."

Choose One Soundbite: Select one of the six ways to rejoice in waiting that resonates most with your current situation. Practice it intentionally throughout Advent.

Remember Mary's Example: When facing difficult circumstances, recall how Mary humbly accepted God's plan despite the social cost and personal hardship, trusting in God's sovereignty and goodness.
