Sermon Overview

Repairing relationships is one of the toughest things we'll do in life. And yet, it's required from us all. Though we love building new relationships, maintaining damaged relationships can be daunting. This sermon explores practical, biblical skills to navigate conflict and pursue reconciliation in our relationships.

Eight Ways to Resolve Disagreements

1. Make the First Move üöÄ

Stop waiting. Stop keeping score. God made the first move toward you‚Äîlet that empower you to pursue reconciliation, regardless of who's "more wrong."

"If you, while going, remember that someone, you have an angst against someone, there's unresolved conflict against someone, leave your offering there. And then first go and be reconciled to them." ‚Äî Matthew 5

Reconciliation is more important than worship. Better to work it out in the lobby than sing with bitterness in your heart.

2. Reject Your Excuses üö´

We all have excuses: "I tried before," "They're more mature," "It won't work." Don't let fear win. Fear leads to hiding, but you are secure in God's love.

Their rejection may sting, but it doesn't define you. Pursue peace anyway.

3. Recognize Your Lack üôè

Conflict makes everything fuzzy. You need God's wisdom to see clearly, to respond well, to know what to say.

James 1:5
"If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you."

Conflict is a blessing when it increases your dependence on God.

4. Consider Acceptance ‚úÖ

Not everything is worth fighting about. Paul addressed the early church's debate about meat sacrificed to idols by calling some matters "disputable."

Romans 14:1
"Accept the one whose faith is weak, without quarreling over disputable matters."

John Gottman's research: 69% of marital conflict is perpetual‚Äîit won't be resolved. You can fight about it forever, or you can accept.

This isn't denial or avoidance. It's a decision: I release this. I accept you as you are. Forgiveness granted, reconciliation established.

5. Own Your Part ü™û

Even if you think it's only 1% your fault, own it. Most of us think the problem is 80% them, 20% us. Guess what? They think the same thing.

James 4:1
"What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you?"

If you are at peace with God, very little can disrupt that. The source of quarrels is often what's happening within us.

6. Listen Beyond the Words üëÇ

Listen to the heart. Understand the woundedness, the baggage, the context. If we saw what they're carrying, we'd be more gracious.

James 1:19
"My dear brothers and sisters, take note of this: Everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak and slow to become angry."

Two ears, one mouth‚Äîuse them proportionately. Listening well automatically makes you slow to speak and slow to anger.

7. Guard Your Mouth üó£Ô∏è

Words create. God spoke the world into existence; you're made in His image. Your words have power to wound or heal.

Ephesians 4:15
"Speak the truth in love."

Weapons of Mass Destruction (WMD):

  • The "D" word (divorce) ‚Äî never use it
  • Comparisons ‚Äî "You're just like your dad" or "Be more like your sister"
  • "Always" and "Never" ‚Äî rarely true, always destructive

Don't hide behind your personality. "I'm just direct" isn't an excuse. Neither is "I'm just quiet." We all need to speak truth in love.

8. Focus on Reconciliation, Not Resolution ü§ù

You may never agree. But you can agree to disagree and still be reconciled.

Forgiveness was not God's goal.

God sent Jesus to die so we could be forgiven‚Äîbut that was a means to an end. Reconciliation was the goal: intimacy, connection, relationship restored.

Forgiveness can happen from a distance. Reconciliation requires proximity. As much as it depends on you, pursue reconciliation.
