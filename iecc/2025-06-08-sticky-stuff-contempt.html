üíî The Crisis of Disconnection ‚ñº

We all want connection. This is a fundamental desire that resides in all of us. We want to belong to a person, matter to a group of people. When we come into a room, we not only want them to acknowledge us, we want them to celebrate us and make us feel like we're part of something bigger.

But sadly, that is not the experience most of us have. Instead, most of us feel loneliness and disconnection. There's a global phenomenon of loneliness spreading around the world.

üí° At the heart of why we are feeling so lonely is because we don't know how to repair. We don't know how to repair our relationships. When things go wrong in our relationships, we don't know how to fix it.

Real Stories of Broken Relationships

A husband whose wife was really upset at him. He didn't know why, tried to defend himself, which made her more upset. They started shouting, went to separate rooms, and didn't talk for days. He stayed at work longer to avoid the situation, hoping it would blow over. Later he said, "Now we're okay. We're not fighting anymore." But there was clearly more to work out.

A lady with a strained relationship with her parents. Every visit, the same questions: "You gotten promoted yet? Why aren't you married? Why aren't you dating? You gained weight?" This constant barrage became a source of pain, bitterness, and resentment. Her solution? Move away from Hong Kong to avoid dealing with her parents.

These are real stories in our church - real broken relationships that feel stuck because there's no movement towards reconciliation. These relationships that should be sources of joy and connection have instead become sources of pain and regret.

Here's the truth: These difficulties await every single close relationship. We can choose to repair these relationships (which takes a lot of work), or we can let them fall into disrepair. If you're anything like me, you tend to take the path of least resistance - letting relationships fall into disrepair and moving on when they get beyond saving.

üîß How to Repair: The Three-Step Apology ‚ñº

üí¨ Pastor Kevin: "I've become an over-apologizer without much sincerity - a 'false apologist.' Here are mistakes to avoid..."

‚ùå Three Types of False Apologies to Avoid

1. The Deflector

Example: "Sorry I raised my voice at you, BUT I wouldn't have had to if you had gotten ready on time."

What you've done: Dismissed the other person's feelings, shifted the blame to them or someone else, or minimized the whole situation.

How to spot it: If you use the word "but" - "I'm sorry, BUT it's your fault / his fault / their fault / it's really not that big of a deal."

2. The Generalizer

Example: "I'm sorry if I've upset you in any way."

Why it fails: You use the generalizer when you don't know what you're sorry for. You just know someone's mad at you and you hate it, so you throw out a catch-all clich√©.

Better approach: Sit down and figure things out through conversation and personal reflection. What is it that you've done? How have you contributed to the rupture of this relationship? Think through those things BEFORE you apologize.

3. The Backhander

Example: "I'm sorry I've had this grudge against you, but I've never respected you for the way you work."

What you've done: Whether intentional or not, you've used the apology as a further way to insult the person. You've used it to prop yourself up and rid yourself of guilt.

Why it's devastating: It breeds further disconnection. The point of apology is to facilitate and foster reconnection, but the backhander just makes you feel better about yourself.

‚úÖ The Three-Step Apology That Works

Becky Kennedy (parenting expert): Three elements for effective repair, applicable to all relationships:

Important note: No single apology will right all the wrongs of the past, especially when dealing with something as deep as contempt. But this type of apology can at least move you in the right direction.
Step Action Purpose
1. Name It Address and name what happened Ensure both parties agree on what caused the rupture
2. Take Responsibility Own your part and acknowledge their feelings No deflecting, over-generalizing, or backhanding
3. State Your Correction Verbalize what you'll do differently Make it concrete, clear, and spoken aloud so both parties agree

üìù Example of the Three-Step Apology

Step 1 - Name It:

"The other day when we were fighting, and I rolled my eyes... I shouldn't have done that. I saw the way that it made you feel right away. I saw the way you clammed up and shut down when I rolled my eyes."

Step 2 - Take Responsibility:

"So I'm sorry. I take full responsibility for that. It wasn't a compassionate thing to do. Instead it was judgmental, condemning. It made you feel like nothing. It was a rude thing to do."

Step 3 - State Your Correction:

"Next time we have an argument, I will listen to you without judgment. Next time we have a disagreement, I will listen to you and try to understand fully where it is you are coming from."

That's it. Pastor Kevin tried this several times this past week. His honest reflection:

"It feels very scientific at first - you're thinking 'Okay, what's step one, step two?' But keep at it. Even if you get the order mixed up, just make sure these three elements are there. You name it. You know what the situation is. You take responsibility for it. Then you state your correction. Do those three things and you will see - again, it won't solve everything, but it will move you in the direction of repair and reconciliation."

üéØ The Goal: We all want relationships that are thriving - not just partners getting through life together, but truly connected, having fun, thriving. To do that, we need to hone in on the target (contempt), attack it with priority and urgency (like plugging a hole in a ship), and use this three-step approach to move toward repair.

üî¨ John and Julie Gottman - The Science of Contempt in Marriage ‚ñº

John Gottman and his wife Julie Gottman are experts in marriage who have studied marriages for over two decades, even three decades. They're able to discern to 90% accuracy which couples would end their marriage in divorce and which couples will survive.

The Love Lab Study: They created this "love lab" where they invited couples to come in and just talk to each other - talk about their day, something good in their relationship, or a point of conflict. They would just observe that discussion, that conversation. They invited couples back five years later and then every subsequent year until the end of the study, which took place over 15 years.

The Finding: After 15 years, a quarter of the couples had ended their marriage in divorce. What they noticed in the couples that divorced: they showed small signs, small microaggressions of contempt - rolling your eyes, rolling your head, sarcasm, name-calling. Those were the couples that most often ended their marriage in divorce, at least to a 90% clip.

‚Äî John and Julie Gottman (paraphrased from sermon)

Sermon Application: The question for us, whether married or not, is where does that contempt come from? This research demonstrates that contempt isn't just a theological concept - it's a measurable, observable phenomenon that destroys relationships with scientific predictability.

üß† Dr. Arthur Brooks - The Psychology of Contempt ‚ñº

Dr. Arthur Brooks is a professor at Harvard who teaches a really popular course about happiness. There's a huge massive wait list for this class because Harvard students really, really want to be happy.

Four Base Negative Emotions: Every human being has four base negative emotions. When I say negative, I don't mean they are bad emotions - these are emotions activated when your body senses a threat:

  • Fear and Anger: Activated when your body senses a situation where you need to fight back or run away
  • Sadness: Activated when you experience loss of someone or something significant to you
  • Disgust: Activated when your body senses a pathogen that is dangerous to you. That's why you should feel grossed out by it - when you're grossed out, you avoid it. Anytime you're grossed out by something, that's your brain protecting you.

Dr. Brooks's Theory: Contempt is a mixture of anger and disgust.

‚Äî Dr. Arthur Brooks (paraphrased from sermon)

Why This Matters

Anger by itself is not bad - it's a hot emotion showing you really care. If you're angry with someone, you care about the relationship and want things to change.

But disgust is dangerous. Disgust is to be relegated to pathogens. People are not pathogens. People are not diseases and viruses to be avoided. When we direct disgust toward people, we dehumanize them.

üë©‚Äçüëß Becky Kennedy - The Three-Step Repair Framework ‚ñº

Becky Kennedy is a parenting expert who's given many talks and speeches around the world about parenting. In one of these talks, she spoke specifically about apologizing from parent to child.

Three Elements to Repair:

  1. Name it: Address and name what it is. Make sure both parties are in agreement with what it is that has caused the rupture of the relationship.
  2. Take responsibility for it: Instead of deflecting or over-generalizing or backhanding, you take responsibility for what you've done and you also acknowledge how the other person feels.
  3. State your correction: You verbalize it. You say what it is you will do differently. Make it concrete, make it very clear. Most importantly, verbalize it so that both parties are in agreement with what will change in the future.
