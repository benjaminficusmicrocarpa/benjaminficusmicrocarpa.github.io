üìú The Promise to Abraham

At 75 years old, Abram received God's call to leave his homeland with an incredible promise: he would become the father of nations. There was just one problem ‚Äì he had no children. For nine years, Abram and Sarai (65 years old when called) wandered, waiting for this promise to be fulfilled.

üí° Sarai's Solution

Sarai proposed a culturally acceptable solution: Abram could have a child through her Egyptian slave Hagar. In ancient Near Eastern culture, any child born to a slave belonged to the master and mistress. Though this seems shocking to us, it was reasonable in their context.

"Abram, you're to be the father of nations, but I cannot bear children. Take Hagar ‚Äì she's young. Any child born to her will be my child."

‚ö° Conflict and Mistreatment

When Hagar became pregnant, she began to disdain Sarai (slavery, forced marriage, forced pregnancy provide pretty clear reasons). The power dynamic shifted, and Sarai became angry. When she complained to Abram, he responded: "Your slave, your problem." Sarai's solution was to mistreat Hagar so severely that a pregnant slave with no resources chose to flee to the desert.

üëÅÔ∏è God Sees Hagar

Genesis 16:7-13 ‚Äì The angel of the Lord found Hagar in the desert and asked, "Where have you come from, and where are you going?"

God asked this question not because He didn't know, but to bring Hagar clarity. She knew where she'd come from, but had no idea where she was going. But God did.

God promised Hagar: "I will increase your descendants so much that they will be too numerous to count... You will give birth to a son. Name him Ishmael, for the Lord has heard your misery."

This was the same promise given to Abram! A lowly slave girl would become the mother of nations. Her son would be "like a wild donkey" ‚Äì meaning free. In a culture where slave children were slaves for life, this was miraculous.

Remarkably, Hagar didn't name God "El Shema" (God who hears), even though God said He heard her affliction. Instead, she named Him El Roi ‚Äì "You are the God who sees me."

"This is even more personal because when she names God 'God sees me,' what she's saying is that God has looked at her heart. He knows the deepest desire, the longing that she wants. God has seen her personally. He knows her pain and he knows her heart, and he knows that she desires freedom."

üö™ God Works for Freedom

Years later, after Sarah gave birth to Isaac, she demanded that Abraham dismiss Hagar and Ishmael. Though this troubled Abraham, God told him to follow through. Here we see God's beautiful work: He used Sarah's disdain and even injustice to grant Hagar and Ishmael the freedom she longed for.

However, Abraham sent them into the desert with only one skin of water (perhaps 15 liters) and bread ‚Äì barely enough for three days in the desert heat. When the water ran out, Hagar put Ishmael under a bush because she couldn't bear to watch him die.

üíß God Sees and Provides

El Roi comes in our deepest vulnerabilities and pain. He doesn't just see ‚Äì He enters into it and provides what we need.

üéØ Abraham's Journey of Faith

Abraham and Sarah were called to leave their homeland at 75 and 65 years old ‚Äì a huge act of faith. But their journey included moments of doubt: lying to Pharaoh about Sarah being his sister, taking matters into their own hands with Hagar, and laughing at God's promise of a son in their old age.

When Isaac (whose name means "one who laughs") was finally born, Abraham was 100 years old. After waiting a century for this promise, they finally had their son.

Genesis 22:1-2 ‚Äì "After these things, God tested Abraham and said... 'Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go to the land of Moriah and offer him there as a burnt offering.'"

Notice the parallel language: "Your son, your only son, whom you love" echoes John 3:16 ‚Äì "God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son." This test foreshadows God's ultimate provision of Jesus.

üåÖ Immediate Obedience

"Abraham rose early in the morning" and immediately obeyed. Through all his experiences ‚Äì even when he tried to take control ‚Äì he had learned that God ultimately provides. His experience proved God's faithfulness.

Isaac, old enough to carry his own wood and understand what was happening, noticed they had everything except a lamb. Abraham's response revealed complete faith: "God will provide a lamb for us, my son."

Abraham believed two things simultaneously:

  1. God would spare Isaac ‚Äì He would provide a substitute
  2. Even if Isaac died ‚Äì God would resurrect him, because God's promise was "through Isaac shall your offspring be named" (Hebrews 11:17-19)

This wasn't about proving Abraham had faith ‚Äì Genesis 15 already said he "believed God and it was credited to him as righteousness." This test revealed how much his faith had grown. As James writes, "faith without works is dead" ‚Äì genuine faith produces fruit.

üîó The Connection: Vision & Provision

God's vision leads to His provision.

Two different sons in two different situations: Hagar with Ishmael needing water and survival, Abraham with Isaac facing the ultimate test of faith. In both stories, we see the same truth: God sees what we need and provides at exactly the right time.

El Roi and Jehovah Jireh are not separate attributes but two sides of the same divine nature. First God sees, then He sees to it that our needs are met.

1. God Sees You Even When You Feel Unseen üëÅÔ∏è

Practical Step: In your moments of feeling invisible or abandoned, pause and remember Hagar's story. God saw her in the desert, knew her deepest longing for freedom, and provided for her. He sees you with that same intimate knowledge and care.

2. God Provides Even When You Can't See It üôå

Sometimes we see God's provision clearly; other times we don't recognize it until much later. With Hagar, there was a well provided. With Abraham, there was a ram in the thicket. God always makes a way.

3. God Cares for You No Matter Your Place in the Story üíù

Whether you're the patriarch of nations or a slave girl with an "illegitimate" son, God sees and cares for you. From the highest high to the lowest low, wherever you place yourself in the story, God will see you and provide for you.

Practical Step: If you feel insignificant or unworthy of God's attention, meditate on Hagar's story. God not only saw a slave woman but allowed her to name Him ‚Äì the only human in Scripture given this privilege. You matter to God.
