😰 The Surprise Nobody Saw Coming

We celebrate today arguably the most famous week in the Christian calendar — Easter. We join with over two billion people throughout the world, roughly thirty percent of the global population, that would name the name of Jesus as their Lord and Saviour.

But when we look back at the story, we discover something remarkable: the initial disciples were completely surprised by the resurrection. Jesus had actually predicted it. He prophesied that he would, like Jonah in the belly of the whale for three days, be in the tomb for three days and then out. They heard it — and yet they didn't believe it.

C. S. Lewis calls this "chronological snobbery" — the tendency to look back in history and say, "Oh, I would have been smarter; I would have responded better." But evidently all of those disciples, without exception, were surprised.

🔒 The Fearful Huddle

📖 John 20:19 (NIV)
"On the evening of that first day of the week, when the disciples were together, with the doors locked for fear of the Jewish leaders, Jesus came and stood among them."
This is not a bold faith. This is not a courageous group of men. This is not a burgeoning movement. These are cowards who believe it's over — that their three years following Jesus ended at the cross.

Right after the crucifixion, the disciples gave up. They resumed their old occupations — they went back to being fishermen. They gathered in fear, believing they would be arrested for their association with Jesus.

🪦 The Empty Tomb

At least four women, probably more, went to the tomb bringing burial spices to anoint the body of Jesus. Their intent was to complete the burial rites. Instead, they are told by angels:

📖 Luke 24:6-8 (NIV)
"He is not here; he has risen! Remember how he told you, while he was still with you in Galilee: 'The Son of Man must be delivered over to the hands of sinners, be crucified and on the third day be raised again.' Then they remembered his words."
Only when the angels quoted Jesus' own prediction did the women remember. They had heard the words but hadn't internalised them.

The women verify the empty tomb and rush back to the disciples, fully anticipating joy and excitement. But that's not what happens:

📖 Luke 24:11 (NIV)
"But they did not believe the women, because their words seemed to them like nonsense."
Even with actual eyewitness validation, the disciples dismiss the report. They were so locked in fear and grief that resurrection seemed impossible.
The movement doesn't start off well. Nobody expected it. They thought his body would still be there. Nobody historically had ever predicted their death and resurrection and conquered death. So you can give them that — nobody before had done this.

🔄 The Resurrection Changes Everything

It would be this single fact — the resurrection — that would change the trajectory of the entire Jesus movement, what we know as Christianity. The disciples were huddled in fear, resuming their old vocations, counting it as a loss. And then: the resurrection.

Suddenly they are galvanised, renewed. They go from cowering in fear to boldly preaching the gospel. The majority of those fearful disciples would go on to live and give their lives for what they believed in — most would be martyred for their faith.

They were so convinced of it, they would give their lives for this truth. And the difference was the resurrection. That's what made a difference in their lives.

— Pastor Brett

So today we ask the question: the resurrection made a difference in their lives — what about yours and mine? So what? Does it make a difference in our lives? Is that even God's intent — for the resurrection to actually change you, to inform you, to give you hope, to give you purpose?

Pastor Brett proposed that Easter practically answers two big questions — questions you probably don't ask out loud, but deep in your soul you actually do:

  1. Is there a personal God? — A God that can be known, not just some distant deity. A God I can know personally, who knows me.
  2. What does that mean for me personally? — How does it change my life, my perspective, my marriage, my family, my career? How does that reality ooze into every crevice of my daily life?

🏛️ Paul at Athens: The Unknown God

Fast-forward to about 50 AD — just under twenty years after the crucifixion. Most people alive at that time would have had either first-hand experience or at least second-hand information about this Jesus who lived, walked, performed miracles, and was risen from the dead. After his resurrection, Jesus appeared to more than 500 people — eating with them, talking with them, showing his pierced hands and side.

The Apostle Paul — once the most anti-Christian man alive, a persecutor of the church who sought to find and kill Christians — had a face-to-face encounter with the risen Jesus, and his life was forever changed. He went on to write much of the New Testament and plant churches throughout the Mediterranean.

🏙️ A City Full of Idols

📖 Acts 17:16 (NIV)
"While Paul was waiting for [Silas and Timothy] in Athens, he was greatly distressed to see that the city was full of idols."
Athens was the intellectual capital of the ancient world — home to the movers and shakers, the intelligentsia. And it was covered in shrines: to Athena, Poseidon, Hermes, even a temple to the seven Muses.

Paul spoke to the people, asking about their beliefs, and presented his case about Jesus and the resurrection. The responses were mixed:

📖 Acts 17:18 (NIV)
"A group of Epicurean and Stoic philosophers began to debate with him. Some of them asked, 'What is this babbler trying to say?' Others remarked, 'He seems to be advocating foreign gods.'"
Paul's conviction and his powerful personal testimony earned him an invitation to address the Areopagus — Mars Hill — the centre of Athenian intellectual discourse.

🗿 Filling in the Gap

📖 Acts 17:22-23 (NIV)
"Paul then stood up in the meeting of the Areopagus and said: 'People of Athens! I see that in every way you are very religious. For as I walked around and looked carefully at your objects of worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: TO AN UNKNOWN GOD. So you are ignorant of the very thing you worship — and this is what I am going to proclaim to you.'"
Historians record that about a hundred years earlier, a plague hit the region. When their many gods didn't prevent it, the Athenians built an altar "to an unknown God" — just in case they'd missed one. Paul seizes this gap in their certainty.

Paul's strategy is remarkable. He doesn't attack their beliefs — he reads the room and redirects their spiritual curiosity. Their elaborate religious life reveals that God has stamped eternity on the heart of every human being: all people in all places at all times have a longing that cries out, "There's got to be more. This can't be it."

🌅 The God Who Gives

📖 Acts 17:24-25 (NIV)
"The God who made the world and everything in it is the Lord of heaven and earth and does not live in temples built by human hands. And he is not served by human hands, as if he needed anything. Rather, he himself gives everyone life and breath and everything else."
Paul looks out at the temples surrounding him — built by human hands, maintained by human offerings — and declares that the true God is above all of it. He doesn't take; he gives.

Paul graciously navigated a religious minefield. He could have been incredibly offensive, but instead he pointed out: "What you're doing is trying to find God because God — the God — put that yearning within you."

📖 Acts 17:26-27 (NIV)
"From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth; and he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands. God did this so that they would seek him and perhaps reach out for him and find him, though he is not far from any one of us."
God started the seeking process. No one truly seeks after God on their own — God initiates it in everyone. Your curiosity, your invitation to church, your spiritual questions: all God things.

God is not playing a game of hide and seek. He's not hiding, hoping nobody will find him. He's wide open — "Please find me." Here's a sunrise. Here's a sunset. Here's an ocean. Here's northern lights. I'm going to show you my creativity, and I'm going to point to the fact that you're really small, that this universe is really big, and it's going to give you a sense of awe that makes you go, "Is there more?"

— Pastor Brett

The beautiful promise of God is that he always answers the prayer, "God, show me who you are." He jumps on that prayer. He will bring people, invitations, awareness, books, experiences — he does all things to answer that prayer sincerely prayed. And when that prayer is answered, there's more to know. It's an inexhaustible place of discovery.

✝️ The Proof: Raised from the Dead

📖 Acts 17:30-31 (NIV)
"In the past God overlooked such ignorance, but now he commands all people everywhere to repent. For he has set a day when he will judge the world with justice by the man he has appointed. He has given proof of this to everyone by raising him from the dead."
Paul's climax: we've reached a pivotal moment in history. God has invaded history through the person of Jesus Christ. The resurrection is the proof — the exclamation point on God's statement to all of humanity.

Paul didn't preach the parables, the Sermon on the Mount, or the miracles of feeding thousands. He went straight to the big one — the resurrection. That's what sets Jesus apart from all other faiths and belief systems. Other teachers spoke of love and forgiveness — but only Jesus was resurrected. Only Jesus predicted his death and resurrection and saw it become reality.

Three Responses

😏
Some Sneered

They mocked. It was too unreal, too unprecedented. Whether genuine unbelief or a convenient excuse — "Faith is for emotionally weak people who need a crutch."

🤔
Some Were Curious

"Tell me more." It's a lot to take in. It would mean a complete overhaul of my belief system. But what you say — I'm interested.

🙏
Some Believed

Convinced by the evidence, transformed by the truth. Including Dionysius, a member of the Areopagus, and a woman named Damaris.

These three categories still describe our world today. Some people think it's ridiculous that you're at church right now. Some of you might think it's ridiculous that you're at church right now. Some are on a journey — "I just want to learn a little more." Others are absolutely convinced.

💛 Does Easter Matter?

Does it matter that a resurrection actually happened two thousand years ago? Does it matter now?

For Paul, the resurrection was incredibly relevant — it changed his whole life. For Christianity as a belief system, it makes or breaks the validity of the claims of Christ. But what about for you?

The resurrection demonstrates the power of God. God sent his Son because he loved you and me, allowed him to die a torturous, unfair death on a cross, and raised him from the dead — to prove the love of God.

If you're convinced that God loves you — which is what he wants you to be — it changes everything. The way you relate to people, the way you keep score or don't, the way you value yourself as a human being. It changes the things that are important to you. It changes everything slowly but certainly.

— Pastor Brett

The resurrection power — which is God shouting his love for you — will change every aspect of who you are for the better. The tomb is empty, which is good news for our empty souls, because he fills us up.

🏛️ Historical Evidence for the Resurrection

Pastor Brett emphasised that the Christian faith is historically grounded — we don't "check our brains at the door." The resurrection is not blind faith; it's a conclusion supported by reasoning, observation, and study. Here is the historical context and evidence that undergirds the Easter message.

📜 The Transformation of the Disciples

Perhaps the most compelling evidence for the resurrection is the radical, documented transformation of Jesus' followers:

Friday — Crucifixion (AD 33)
Jesus is crucified. All disciples scatter. Peter denies knowing Jesus three times. They retreat to a locked room in Jerusalem, terrified of the Jewish authorities.
Sunday — Empty Tomb
Women discover the empty tomb. Angels remind them of Jesus' prediction. They rush to tell the disciples — who dismiss their report as "nonsense" (Luke 24:11).
40 Days — Post-Resurrection Appearances
Jesus appears to the disciples, to more than 500 people at one time, eats with them, shows his wounds. He appears in bodily form — not as a vision or ghost.
Pentecost — Bold Proclamation
The same men who were cowering behind locked doors now stand in public, boldly proclaiming the resurrection. Peter preaches to thousands. The church is born.
AD 33–67 — Martyrdom
The majority of the original disciples would be martyred for their faith. People die for what they believe to be true — but people do not die for what they know to be a lie.

🏛️ Paul's Conversion and Strategy at Athens

Paul (originally Saul of Tarsus) represents an "enemy witness" — someone whose testimony is particularly credible because he had every reason not to believe. He was a Pharisee, a zealous persecutor of the early church, who actively sought to arrest and execute Christians.

His encounter with the risen Jesus on the Damascus Road (Acts 9) produced one of history's most dramatic conversions — from chief persecutor to chief proponent of Christianity.

🗺️ Athens in the First Century

When Paul arrived in Athens around AD 50, he encountered a city that was:

  • The intellectual capital of the ancient world — home to the philosophical traditions of Plato, Aristotle, Socrates, and the Stoic and Epicurean schools
  • Saturated with religious sites — temples to Athena (the Parthenon), Poseidon, Hermes, Hephaestus, and many others; a temple to the seven Muses (arts, music, architecture)
  • Home to the Areopagus (Mars Hill) — the council that evaluated new teachings and religious ideas; Paul was invited to present his case there
  • A city with an "Unknown God" altar — built approximately 100 years earlier during a plague, when existing gods failed to prevent it; the Athenians hedged their bets by honouring any deity they might have overlooked
Paul's Rhetorical Strategy: Rather than condemning the Athenians' polytheism, Paul commended their spiritual curiosity ("I see that in every way you are very religious") and used their own "Unknown God" altar as a bridge to the gospel. He quoted their own poets ("For in him we live and move and have our being") before presenting the resurrection as the decisive proof.

⚖️ Why the Resurrection Demands Consideration

🪦
The Empty Tomb

The tomb was empty — a fact acknowledged even by opponents. If the body could have been produced, Christianity would have been stopped in its tracks. No one — Roman, Jewish, or otherwise — ever produced the body.

👁️
500+ Eyewitnesses

Paul records that Jesus appeared to more than 500 people at once (1 Corinthians 15:6), and notes that most were still alive — inviting verification. This is not hearsay; it's a testable claim.

🔄
Transformed Lives

Fearful men became fearless martyrs. The pendulum swing — especially in Paul — was massive. Something extraordinary must have happened to produce such a radical change.

👩
Women as First Witnesses

In first-century Jewish culture, women's testimony was not admissible in court. If the gospel writers were inventing the story, they would never have chosen women as the first witnesses — unless that's what actually happened.

⚡
Enemy Conversions

Paul (a persecutor) and James (Jesus' sceptical brother) both became leaders of the early church. Their conversions are difficult to explain apart from a genuine encounter with the risen Christ.

📅
Rapid Emergence

The resurrection claim emerged immediately — not decades or centuries later. Paul's letter (1 Corinthians 15) contains a creed that scholars date to within 3-5 years of the crucifixion. This is remarkably early testimony.

We don't check our brains at the door when we believe in Christ. We in fact reason, we think, we observe, we study, and the conclusion that makes the most sense is that you are right — is that you are true.

— Pastor Brett

🎯 The Resurrection Makes a Personal Difference

The resurrection is not merely a historical event to acknowledge — it is proof of God's love that is meant to transform how you live. Pastor Brett urged us to move beyond intellectual assent and let the reality of the risen Christ change everything.

🔍
Examine Your Response

Of the three responses to the resurrection — sneering, curiosity, or belief — which describes you right now? If you're in the "curious" category, lean into that. Ask God to show you who he is. He promises to answer that prayer every time.

💛
Receive God's Love

The resurrection is God shouting his love for you. This is not the love of a boyfriend or girlfriend — this is the love of a cosmic Creator whose very definition is love. Let that truth settle in: he could not possibly love you any more than he already does.

🙏
Pray the Brave Prayer

If you're seeking, trying to make sense of it all, simply pray: "God, help me make sense of it. Show me who you are." God promises to answer that prayer — bringing people, invitations, awareness, and experiences to reveal himself to you.

🔄
Let It Change Everything

If you're convinced God loves you, let that conviction transform the way you relate to people, the way you value yourself, the things you prioritise. The resurrection power changes every aspect of who you are — slowly but certainly, for the better.

📚
Study the Evidence

Faith is not blind. If you have doubts, pursue them. Study the historical evidence for the resurrection. Engage with the questions honestly. As Pastor Brett said, believing in Christ requires less faith than not believing.

Take the Next Step

Whether it's Alpha, a newcomers lunch, a small group, or simply coming back next week — take one concrete step. God has been behind every invitation and moment of curiosity in your life. Respond to his initiative.
