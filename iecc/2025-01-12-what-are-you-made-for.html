🎤 Main Sermon Content

🚀 Introduction: What Are You Made For? ▼

Pastor Brett begins by asking a fundamental question that every person asks at some point: "What are you made for?" He illustrates this with a personal story about finding a mysterious object in his drawer—a mic stand attachment—and not knowing its purpose. This mirrors how many of us go through life without understanding our unique purpose.

The sermon references ikigai, a Japanese concept of finding your life's purpose at the intersection of what you love, what the world needs, what you can be paid for, and what you're good at. While this framework is helpful, Pastor Brett emphasizes that from a Christian perspective, discovering your purpose goes much deeper.

"Let me tell you why you are here. You're here to be salt seasoning that brings out the God flavors of this earth." Matthew 5:13 (MSG paraphrase)
⛪ Foundation: Saved by Grace, Created for Works ▼

Pastor Brett emphasizes the foundational truth of salvation before discussing purpose and calling:

"For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast." Ephesians 2:8-9 (NIV)

He stresses that salvation is entirely by grace—not earned through our efforts. When you stand before God and He asks, "Why should I let you into heaven?" your answer is simply: Jesus. Nothing you've done, no church attendance, no good deeds—only Jesus is perfect, and we trust in Him, not ourselves.

However, Pastor Brett points out that the Greek text doesn't stop there. The translators added a period, but Paul continues:

"For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do." Ephesians 2:10 (NIV)

This verse reveals that while works don't save us, God has prepared specific good works for each believer. You are God's poiema—His masterpiece, His work of art. James reinforces this: "Faith without works is dead" (James 2:26). The assumption is that genuine faith naturally produces good works.

🎯 The SHAPE Framework: Discovering Your Design ▼

Pastor Brett introduces the SHAPE acronym to help believers understand how God has uniquely designed them:

🎁 S - Spiritual Gifts

Every believer receives at least one spiritual gift when they come to faith. These are supernatural abilities given by the Holy Spirit to build up the church and advance God's kingdom.

"Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God's grace in its various forms." 1 Peter 4:10 (NIV)

Paul strongly emphasizes: "Now concerning spiritual gifts, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant." (1 Corinthians 12:1). What a tragedy to be highly educated but ignorant about the greatest gift you'll ever receive!

Pastor Brett shares that his spiritual gift is teaching. This doesn't mean he's the best teacher in the world, but he likes it, is competent at it, enjoys it, and feels gifted at it. Importantly, recognizing your gift isn't bragging—God gave it to you.

❤️ H - Heart (Passion)

Your heart represents what you care about more than others do. It's a passion or concern that God has placed within you, sometimes for things that aren't explicitly commanded in Scripture.

Pastor Brett uses the Apostle Paul as an example. Paul was so consumed with spreading the gospel that he suggested everyone should remain single like him to avoid distractions! Of course, he quickly retreated, acknowledging that "if you cannot control yourself, then they should marry, for it is better to marry than burn with passion" (1 Corinthians 7:9). But his heart's burning passion was clearly church planting and gospel advancement.

Pastor Brett confesses: "I think everybody should adopt children." With 153 million orphans worldwide, he secretly believes everyone should adopt—even though he knows God hasn't called everyone to this. Similarly, one church member felt passionate about reducing plastic in the ocean and gave him a reusable water bottle. While Pastor Brett cares about environmental issues, it's not his burning passion.

The key indicator: You secretly think everyone should care about your passion as much as you do. What issue makes your heart beat faster? Justice? Human trafficking? The elderly? Prison reform? The environment? Children's education? That's a clue to your heart.

💪 A - Aptitude (Talents)

This is simply what you're good at—your natural talents and proficiencies. No false humility here; everyone is good at something.

Pastor Brett shares a powerful story of a man who approached him and said: "Pastor Brett, I'm not good at leading Bible studies or teaching and preaching. But you know what I'm good at? Making money." He wasn't bragging; he recognized his unique ability to close deals and envision business opportunities. He asked Pastor Brett to tell him about needs in the church or ministry opportunities where he could help financially.

Later, when Pastor Brett met a young man in mainland China who wanted theological education but couldn't afford it, he connected him with this businessman. The result? This couple funded 100% of a four-year theological degree so this young man could return and serve the church in China more effectively.

The worship team is another example: they're good at music. While you may love music, if you're not good at it, that's not where you should serve. Pastor Brett jokes about begging Byron to let him sing: "It's not gonna happen, right?"

👤 P - Personality

Your unique wiring matters. Understanding your personality helps you serve in roles that fit who God made you to be, not what drains or frustrates you.

Key personality dimensions:

  • People-oriented vs. Task-oriented: Neither is better; they're just different
  • Fast-paced vs. Slow-paced: Pastor Brett's wife makes decisions instantly while he needs data and time to compare options
  • Outgoing vs. Introverted: Do people energize you or drain you? Do you restore by socializing or by reading alone?

Pastor Brett and his wife are opposites: she's fast-paced, he's slow. In restaurants, she hands back the menu immediately ("I already know what I want"), while he wants to study all the options. She finishes eating while he's still deciding what to order! But together they make "informed and timely decisions"—her speed balanced by his thoroughness.

The church uses personality assessments (Myers-Briggs, DiSC, Enneagram, StrengthsFinder, etc.) for staff to understand who would be good at what tasks, what work environments suit different people, and how to build effective teams. You should know yourself too.

📚 E - Experience

Everything God has allowed in your life—successes, failures, disappointments, heartache, trauma—happened under His sovereign hand. Nothing is wasted in God's economy; He wants to redeem it all for good.

"The Father of compassion and the God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our troubles, so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God." 2 Corinthians 1:3-4 (NIV)

Notice "comfort" appears four times in this passage. God comforts you so that you can comfort others with the comfort He provides. This is why Island ECC runs a Divorce Care ministry—led by people who have been divorced. How fitting! God uses your experiences for good.

Your experiences make you who you are right now. Perhaps there are others yearning to be seen, to be understood, who just need to know peace—to feel empathy from someone who has been there. Your experiences matter and can bring healing to others.

🦉 Three Wisdom Principles for Your Calling ▼

1️⃣ God's Commands Are Not Optional

There is a general blueprint for all Christians: to become more Christ-like. This means developing compassion, forgiveness, kindness, generosity, and other character qualities. You don't get to say, "I'm not wired to be compassionate." These are God's commands, not suggestions.

This framework isn't meant to help you opt out of certain character qualities God wants to develop in all believers. The SHAPE assessment helps you understand your unique calling, but it doesn't excuse you from Christlikeness.

2️⃣ Manage Your Passion with Wisdom

Just as Pastor Brett needs to realize not everybody needs to adopt children, you must recognize that your passion shouldn't become others' burden. When you're passionate about something, the tendency is to think everyone should be equally passionate.

You can present your passion, care about it, recruit people for it, and inform others—but don't demand it takes front and center in their lives as well. Respect that God has given different people different hearts and callings.

3️⃣ Calling Provides Focus and Boundaries

When you know who you are and what you're called to do, it helps you respond appropriately to what you shouldn't do or don't need to do. If you don't love kids, you can cross out children's ministry from your options—the church wants people who love kids serving there.

Knowing yourself is freeing. You're supposed to live within your calling, make decisions based on it, refine it, schedule around it, care about it, and investigate ways to be faithful to what you care about. This clarity prevents burnout and enables joyful, effective service.

🔥 Conclusion: Fan Into Flame ▼

As Paul wrote to his young disciple Timothy:

"I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God." 2 Timothy 1:6 (NIV)

God has gifted you, but sometimes that gift can die down. You procrastinate, put it off, let good intentions win over good decisions. Paul says: Fan that flame. Let it grow. Make sure it takes preeminence in your life.

But also fan into flame the gift of God in other people. When you see people operating in their gifts, encourage them! Pastor Brett shares an example of attending a dinner where the hostess had an incredible gift of hospitality—not just setting a beautiful table, but caring about the people at the table, meeting dietary needs, creating conversation by seating the right people together. He told her: "You're really good at hospitality."

This kind of encouragement helps people see their gifts and do more of what God created them for. Your affirmation might be the catalyst someone needs to step into their calling.

  • Align your schedule and decisions with your SHAPE
  • Fan into flame your own gifts through intentional practice and development
  • Encourage others by helping them see and develop their gifts

📖 Key Scripture Passages

Ephesians 2:8-10 - Saved by Grace for Good Works ▼
"For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God— not by works, so that no one can boast. For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do." Ephesians 2:8-10 (NIV)

Context: This is the foundational passage of the sermon. Paul is writing to the church in Ephesus, a predominantly pagan city. He explains the essence of salvation and immediately connects it to purpose. While salvation is entirely by grace through faith (not works), believers are created as God's masterpieces (Greek: poiema) to do good works that God prepared beforehand.

Matthew 5:13 - Salt of the Earth ▼
"You are the salt of the earth. But if the salt loses its saltiness, how can it be made salty again? It is no longer good for anything, except to be thrown out and trampled underfoot." Matthew 5:13 (NIV)

Eugene Peterson's Paraphrase (The Message): "Let me tell you why you are here. You're here to be salt seasoning that brings out the God flavors of this earth."

Context: Jesus is teaching the Sermon on the Mount, explaining to His disciples their purpose and identity. Being "salt" means being a preserving, flavoring influence—bringing out God's character in the world around you.

1 Peter 4:10 - Using Your Spiritual Gifts ▼
"Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God's grace in its various forms." 1 Peter 4:10 (NIV)

Context: Peter is writing to persecuted Christians scattered throughout Asia Minor. He emphasizes that each believer has received a gift and is responsible for using it to serve others. These gifts are expressions of God's grace in different forms.

1 Corinthians 12:1 - Don't Be Ignorant About Gifts ▼
"Now concerning spiritual gifts, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant." 1 Corinthians 12:1 (NIV)

Context: Paul is addressing the Corinthian church's confusion about spiritual gifts. He strongly emphasizes that believers should not be ignorant about this crucial topic. The chapter goes on to explain the diversity of gifts and their purpose in the body of Christ.

James 2:26 - Faith Without Works Is Dead ▼
"As the body without the spirit is dead, so faith without deeds is dead." James 2:26 (NIV)

Context: James, the brother of Jesus, is writing to Jewish Christians scattered abroad. He addresses the relationship between faith and works—not contradicting Paul's teaching on salvation by grace, but emphasizing that genuine faith naturally produces good works as evidence.

1 Corinthians 7:8-9 - Paul's Heart for Gospel Work ▼
"Now to the unmarried and the widows I say: It is good for them to stay unmarried, as I do. But if they cannot control themselves, they should marry, for it is better to marry than to burn with passion." 1 Corinthians 7:8-9 (NIV)

Context: Paul is answering questions about marriage. His personal preference for singleness stems from his consuming passion for gospel ministry—not because marriage is wrong. This illustrates how a person's unique "heart" (passion) can shape their perspective, though Paul wisely acknowledges not everyone shares his calling.

2 Corinthians 1:3-4 - God Comforts to Comfort ▼
"Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of compassion and the God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our troubles, so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God." 2 Corinthians 1:3-4 (NIV)

Context: Paul is writing about God's redemptive purposes in suffering. He emphasizes that God comforts us not just for our sake, but so we can comfort others. This passage appears four times with the word "comfort," showing God's cyclical pattern of redemption—using our experiences to minister to others.

2 Timothy 1:6 - Fan Into Flame Your Gift ▼
"For this reason I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands." 2 Timothy 1:6 (NIV)

Context: Paul is writing his final letter to his spiritual son Timothy, encouraging him to remain faithful in ministry. The metaphor of "fanning into flame" suggests that spiritual gifts can diminish if neglected and need to be actively cultivated and exercised.

Matthew 5:16 - Let Your Light Shine ▼
"In the same way, let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven." Matthew 5:16 (NIV)

Context: Continuing the Sermon on the Mount after calling believers "salt" and "light," Jesus emphasizes that good deeds should be visible and observable—not for self-glorification, but so that others will glorify God when they see believers living out their faith.

🤔 Reflection Questions

1. What is your current understanding of your life's purpose? ▼

Take time to journal your honest answer. Have you been living with clarity about why you're here, or have you been drifting through a cycle of duty and relief? How does your current life align (or not align) with God's purposes for you?

Dig Deeper: If someone asked you, "Why are you here on this planet?" what would you say? How much of your answer reflects God's purposes versus cultural expectations or personal ambitions?
2. What activities make you feel most alive and aligned with God's purposes? ▼

Think about times when you've felt deeply fulfilled—not just happy, but purposeful. What were you doing? Who were you with? What needs were you meeting? These moments often reveal clues to your SHAPE.

Practical Exercise: List five times in the past year when you felt energized and purposeful. Look for common threads—these patterns reveal how God has wired you.
3. What issue or need in the world breaks your heart or stirs your passion? ▼

This question addresses the "H" in SHAPE—your heart. What injustice makes you angry? What suffering moves you to tears? What need do you secretly wish everyone cared about as much as you do? This passion is God-given and points toward your calling.

Consider: Pastor Brett cares about orphans and adoption. Someone else cares about plastic in the ocean. Another person cares about prison reform. What's your thing? Don't dismiss it as insignificant—God placed it in your heart for a reason.
4. How has God used your past experiences (including pain) to shape who you are today? ▼

Reflect on the "E" in SHAPE—your experiences. God wastes nothing. Even hardships, failures, and trauma can be redeemed to comfort others who face similar struggles. How has God comforted you in ways that uniquely equip you to minister to others?

Scripture Reminder: "God comforts us in all our troubles, so that we can comfort those in any trouble with the comfort we ourselves receive from God" (2 Corinthians 1:4). What comfort have you received that you can now share?
5. Are you currently serving in roles that drain you or energize you? Why? ▼

Jesus said, "My yoke is easy and my burden is light" (Matthew 11:30). If you're constantly exhausted and resentful in your service, you might be in a misfit role. Conversely, when you serve according to your SHAPE, it should feel life-giving, even when it's challenging.

Self-Check: Make two lists—"What drains me" and "What energizes me." Be honest. Then prayerfully consider: How can you move toward more of what energizes you and less of what drains you?
6. Who in your life needs encouragement to discover or develop their gifts? ▼

Part of living out your purpose is helping others discover theirs. Think of someone you know who has a gift they may not recognize. How can you "fan into flame" their gift through specific, encouraging words?

Action Challenge: This week, identify one person and write them a note or have a conversation specifically affirming a gift you see in them. Be specific about what you observe and how it impacts others.
7. What would change if you fully embraced that you are God's masterpiece? ▼

Ephesians 2:10 says you are God's poiema—His masterpiece, His work of art. How would your life look different if you truly believed this? What insecurities would lose their power? What risks might you take? What opportunities might you pursue?

Meditate: You are not an accident. You are not average. You are not inadequate. You are God's unique creation, designed with intention for specific purposes. Let this truth sink deep into your identity.
8. What is one concrete step you will take this week to align your life with your SHAPE? ▼

Knowledge without action is incomplete. After reflecting on these questions and the sermon content, commit to one specific, measurable action this week. It could be taking the SHAPE assessment, having a conversation, saying "yes" to something aligned with your gifts, or saying "no" to something that isn't.

Make It Specific: Don't just say "I'll think about it more." Write down exactly what you'll do, when you'll do it, and how you'll know you've completed it. Share your commitment with someone who can help keep you accountable.

📖 Full Context: 1 Corinthians 12:1

"Now concerning spiritual gifts, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant." 1 Corinthians 12:1 (NIV)

Paul is addressing the Corinthian church, which was confused about spiritual gifts. He uses the strong word "ignorant" (Greek: agnoeo, meaning "to be without knowledge or understanding") to emphasize how crucial it is for believers to understand this topic.

The chapter continues to explain that there are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit; different kinds of service, but the same Lord; different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in everyone. Every believer receives a manifestation of the Spirit "for the common good" (v. 7).

Pastor Brett's point: What a shame to be highly educated in worldly matters but ignorant about the greatest spiritual gift you'll ever receive—your God-given purpose and calling.
