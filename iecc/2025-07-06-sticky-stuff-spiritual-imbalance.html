Understanding Corinth: A City Like Hong Kong

Imagine a city that is culturally diverse, religiously diverse, a port city facilitating trade‚Äîan economic hub. It's highly urbanized, politically complex, with a spectrum of social classes. You might be thinking Hong Kong, but the answer is ancient Corinth.

Corinth existed on a narrow strip of land (an isthmus) between two seas. Ships would dock there, unload cargo, and take three or four days to cross‚Äîcreating a "Wan Chai situation" with sailors waiting around. This created a booming, ethically complicated city with diverse people from all backgrounds.

"Paul, in his second missionary journey, left Athens and went to Corinth and stayed there for about a year and a half, 18 months. While he was there, he met Aquila and Priscilla, who had fled Rome, and they all joined in a trade together‚Äîthey were tent makers."

The church in Corinth was beautifully diverse and complicated. You had people from Jewish backgrounds who had sought holiness their whole life, and people who were basically not Jewish, who had no faith or had worshipped other gods. They were coming together around a table trying to explore what it meant that Jesus died and resurrected.

Paul's Heart: The Foundation of Relationship

Before we dive into principles, we need to understand Paul's motivation. He writes:

"For though I am free from all, I have made myself a servant to all. I have become all things to all people that by all means I might save some. I do it all for the sake of the gospel, that I may share with them in its blessings." (1 Corinthians 9:19, 22-23)

Paul's heart was that they would share in the blessings of the gospel together‚Äîexperience joy and wholeness, being renewed and made new together. That's probably your heart too for the relationships that come to mind as you think about imbalance.

Be kind to yourself. These conversations can be tough. You've had missteps, spoken too soon, weren't as patient. But at the root of it is a heart saying, "I feel like we're missing it. There's more. There's good that awaits us."

Principle 1: Open Your Heart

Posture: I'm For You

Paul says, "We have spoken freely to you, Corinthians. Our heart is wide open" (2 Corinthians 6:11). As the authority figure, Paul took the first step of vulnerability. He didn't have to do this‚Äîhe was in charge. But he opened his heart and shared his life with them.

Even this hardcore man‚Äîwho was dragged outside cities by mobs, stoned, shipwrecked, snake-bitten‚Äîthis man had tears as he wrote a difficult letter to the Corinthians. He regretted sending it momentarily, wondering if it would be too harsh.

"For even if I made you grieve with my letter, I do not regret it, though I did regret it. For I see that the letter grieved you, though only for a while. As it is, I rejoice, not because you were grieved, but because you were grieved into repenting." (2 Corinthians 7:8-9)

Opening your heart is important for making connection and preventing defensiveness. When you open up your heart, it's a soft start to a difficult conversation that helps it go much further.

I've found through experience that when I get frustrated or angry and stew in it, conversations don't go well. But if I step back and look beyond that layer to see what I'm actually sad about‚Äîdisappointment or "I wish things were this way"‚Äîconversations go much better.

We need to have conversations because we have a God who has conversations. Our God loves us more than anyone else, and yet He is the most direct and straightforward. He lovingly tells us what is and what isn't, doesn't sugarcoat it‚Äîand He sticks around. We need to emulate that.

Principle 2: Close Your Mouth

Posture: I Am Not God

This may seem opposite, but it is. The book of Proverbs says three times that it's better to live on a corner of the roof, in a desert, or underneath a drip than to live with a nagging wife. Being annoying is probably one of the least Christ-like things you can do.

The idea is: when you open your heart and share, then it's known. Sometimes after that, the best thing you can do is to be quiet and not pester. You are not accountable for anyone's actions. You are not in control. You cannot change anyone. This has to be a work of God to open the heart.

Repeat after me: I am not in control. Repeat after Scripture: Be still and know that He is God.

Paul says, "We've spoken freely to you Corinthians, our heart is wide open. You are not restricted by us, but you are restricted in your own affections. In return, would you widen your hearts also?" (2 Corinthians 6:11-13)

Sometimes your heart-sharing is not reciprocated‚Äîthat's real life. God is the only one that changes hearts.

The Rescuer vs. Ambassador Paradigm

A lot of us in relationships think we're the rescuer, the hero of our own story. But that's not our job in relationships, care groups, or marriage. Our job is not to change people‚Äîthat's God's job. Our job is to come alongside someone as their advocate, their ambassador, their supporter, and help lift them up and point them to the One who can truly do the work from the inside out.

When you can step back, pray for someone, show up and be faithful without nagging or criticizing, you're showing that you trust God is in control. You want change to come from God doing a work in their heart, not from behavior modification because they fear your retribution.

Principle 3: Use Your Freedom to Free Others

Posture: My Freedom Is a Gift to Others

Paul speaks to a debate in the first century about meat sacrificed to idols. Some people were okay with eating it; others felt it went against their conscience because it drew their heart back to what they'd been saved from.

"Now, concerning food offered to idols, we know all of us possess knowledge, but knowledge puffs up, but love builds up... Take care that this right of yours does not somehow become a stumbling block to those who are weak." (1 Corinthians 8:1, 9)

Use your freedom not to say, "I have the right to do this," but to ask, "How can I bless? Love builds up, knowledge puffs up."

Have you ever protected someone, helped someone when you knew they were vulnerable or sensitive? How good does that feel? That's the kind of love Paul is talking about. We should always be moving toward greater humility and servanthood in how we serve others in relationships.

Many of us are in friendships, care groups, or relationships with people who have been hurt by the church, or who have sensitive backgrounds, or who are vulnerable to certain triggers. We need to say, "It doesn't matter if it doesn't matter to me or I'm good with God with this. How can I come to your level to protect you, serve you, love you in a way that shows I'm sensitive to that?"

Principle 4: Celebrate Different Expressions of Faith

Posture: God's Work in Others May Look Different

In 1 Corinthians, the church was arguing: "I follow Paul." "I follow Apollos." "I follow Cephas." Paul responds, "Is Christ divided?" People were drawn to different teachers, different expressions of Christianity. They grew differently. Their hearts were touched by different aspects of God.

The way I connect with God is very different than the way my wife connects. There's overlap, but there's difference too. This happens in care groups. We gather with 12 people and may all want or need different things in the moment.

We have to listen and be sensitive. We can't assume, "Oh, that person didn't memorize the book of James this week, so they're not really a Christian." God is not our own box. He is bigger. His faith is bigger. He is doing way more work than we're aware of.

Our calling is to be active in looking for God at work and celebrating that. That is a posture of generosity‚Äîrelational generosity, humility‚Äîthat opens your heart to God to say, "God, how are you at work in this person? Show me. I'm not looking for what they did wrong or criticism opportunities. I'm looking to see where You are at work, and I'm going to celebrate that."

That posture is good for your soul, especially if there's sticky stuff you're navigating in relationships.

Historical Context: Ancient Corinth

A City Much Like Hong Kong

To understand Paul's wisdom to the Corinthians, we need to understand the city itself. Corinth was located on a narrow strip of land (an isthmus) between the Aegean Sea and the Ionian Sea in southern Greece. This strategic location made it a booming economic hub.

üö¢ The Port City Economy

Instead of sailing around the dangerous southern coast of Greece, ships would dock at Corinth and unload their cargo. It would take three or four days for the cargo (and sometimes the ships themselves) to be transported across the six-kilometer channel. This created a thriving trade economy.

In the late 19th century, the Corinth Canal was finally carved through, but in Paul's day, the overland transport system meant sailors had several days to wait‚Äîleading to the development of a morally complex city with numerous vices and temptations.

‚öîÔ∏è Political & Social Complexity

Corinth had a diverse population with multiple social classes, from wealthy merchants to slaves. After Jerusalem was ransacked in 70 A.D., the Roman Emperor Nero brought about 6,000 Jewish slaves to Corinth to attempt building the canal‚Äîadding another layer of cultural complexity to an already diverse city.

üèõÔ∏è Religious Diversity

The city was filled with temples to various Greek gods. The Temple of Aphrodite, located on Acro-Corinth (a hill overlooking the city), employed about 1,000 women‚Äîmany of them slaves‚Äîas temple prostitutes. This was considered a form of worship in the pagan religious system.

This created an environment where sexual ethics, religious practices, and cultural norms were vastly different from the Jewish monotheistic tradition that some in the church came from.

üéÆ The Isthmian Games

Every two years, Corinth hosted the Isthmian Games, similar to the Olympics. These weren't casual sporting events‚ÄîGreeks were passionate about athletics, and the games drew huge crowds requiring massive amounts of temporary housing (hence Paul's tent-making trade).

This athletic culture influenced Paul's language. When he writes to the Corinthians about "striving to win the prize," "gaining a wreath as a victory," and "disciplining and beating your body for a certain goal," he's using metaphors everyone in Corinth would immediately understand.

‚õ™ The Church's Challenge

Into this complex, diverse, morally complicated city came the gospel. The church in Corinth was made up of:

  • Jewish believers who had sought holiness their whole lives and had deep religious traditions
  • Gentile converts who had no faith background or had worshipped other gods and had very different practices
  • Different economic classes from slaves to wealthy merchants
  • Various ethical backgrounds including those saved from sexual immorality and temple prostitution

They were trying to figure out together what it meant that Jesus died and resurrected and they could have new life. They had different ways of expressing and understanding their faith, different gifts, different beliefs, different pasts‚Äîand they were all trying to follow Jesus together in the same community.

"Paul was coming around a church in a big city trying to unify under Jesus a beautifully diverse and complicated group of people who had different pasts and different understandings. And he was trying to bring them toward faith further in Jesus and to grow and to meld together."

üìç Why This Matters for Us

Just like ancient Corinth, Hong Kong (and many modern cities) is:

  • Economically thriving and diverse
  • Culturally complex with multiple worldviews
  • Religiously pluralistic
  • Fast-paced and opportunity-driven

The challenges the Corinthian church faced‚Äîspiritual imbalances, different expressions of faith, cultural tensions, moral confusion‚Äîare challenges we still face today. Paul's wisdom remains remarkably relevant for navigating relationships in our complex, diverse world.
