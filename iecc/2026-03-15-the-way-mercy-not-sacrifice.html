📋 Sermon Overview

Central Theme: In every society there are outcasts — people at the fringes, ignored, or despised. While most move away from them, Jesus moves towards them. Through the calling of Matthew the tax collector, Jesus reveals a heart that prioritizes mercy and compassion over religious duty. God declares: "I desire mercy, not sacrifice." Our calling is not just to bring gifts to the table, but to bring people to the table.

Series Context: This is the third sermon in "The Way" — a Lent series exploring how we follow Jesus as we make our way toward Easter. Week 1 explored how Jesus called ordinary people; Week 2 examined His temptation in the wilderness. This week: how Jesus moves toward those the world rejects.

🌟 Main Takeaways

  1. Jesus moves toward those the world moves away from — When everyone excluded tax collectors and sinners, Jesus deliberately sought them out. Matthew was a social outcast; Jesus made him a disciple.
  2. Jesus took the initiative — Counter-culturally, it was Jesus the rabbi who approached Matthew, not the other way around. Grace initiates.
  3. You don't need to clean up before following Jesus — Matthew didn't spend a year reforming his life first. He followed Jesus, and transformation came by grace.
  4. Jesus sees himself as a doctor, not a judge — The Pharisees operated as self-righteous judges. Jesus said: "I came for the sick." Good doctors run to the sick, not away from them.
  5. Grace always comes first — In the original Greek of John 1:17, the word order matters: it's "grace and truth" — grace precedes truth.
  6. Mercy (chesed) is active, covenantal love — The Hebrew word means loyalty, loving-kindness. It seeks out its object. God's heart is for us to go and find the lost.
  7. "Who do you bring to your table?" — The practical shift: not just what you bring to the table with your gifts, but who you bring — colleagues, friends, neighbours who need Jesus.

❓ Question 1: What Is Jesus' Way?

We are in the middle of a Lent sermon series called "The Way." Jesus said, "I am the way, the truth, and the life." Before Christians were called "Christians," they were called followers of the way. So we study a passage from Jesus' early ministry, asking three questions: What is Jesus' way? Why is it this way? And how do we follow the way?

📍 The Setting: Capernaum

Jesus was in Capernaum — the headquarters of His ministry, located at the northern tip of the Sea of Galilee. Israel sat on a crucial international trade route — goods moving between Syria to the north and Egypt to the south passed through here. This made it a prime location for collecting taxes and tariffs.

🏦 The Tax Collector's Booth

As Jesus walked through Capernaum, He saw a man named Matthew sitting at the tax collector's booth — like a toll booth on a highway. Tax collectors charged tariffs on international trade, land purchases, and personal goods. They routinely inflated values to pocket the difference. And there was no appeals process — you paid again and again.

Two critical facts made tax collectors universally despised:

  1. They worked for the Roman Empire — Israel was not an independent state but an occupied territory. Rome auctioned off the right to collect taxes to the highest bidder. Matthew, as a tax collector, was viewed as a political traitor collaborating with the oppressor.
  2. They were dishonest — They routinely cheated people by inflating the value of goods and land. Matthew was seen as morally corrupt.

Tax collectors were social outcasts. Everyone hated them. No one wanted to be their friend.

🤝 Jesus Moves Toward Matthew

And yet — Jesus saw Matthew. He didn't walk past. He didn't move away. He moved toward him and said two words: "Follow me." And Matthew immediately got up, left everything behind, and followed.

This was counter-cultural on multiple levels:
  • No reputable man would talk to a tax collector — yet Jesus sought him out
  • In Jesus' time, students sought rabbis, not the other way around — yet Jesus initiated
  • A student would prove himself for months before being accepted — yet Jesus called Matthew immediately

🎉 Matthew's Party

Matthew was so transformed by this encounter that he threw a party. And who came? "Many tax collectors and sinners came and ate with him and his disciples." Matthew's new life in Christ was so remarkable that he brought his entire network to the table to introduce them to Jesus.

I want to believe in Jesus, but look at me — I'm in a mess. I want to clean up my act before I believe in Jesus.

— A common sentiment Pastor Albert encounters in counseling

Pastor Albert addressed this directly: Did Matthew spend a year cleaning up his act and then follow Jesus? Or did he follow Jesus first, and then by the grace of God, transformation came? The answer is clear. Follow Jesus first. He will transform your life.

Jesus' Way: When the world moves away from people ignored by society, Jesus moves toward them. He moved toward the mess — spiritual mess, moral mess, social mess — because that's where a doctor is needed most.

🔍 Question 2: Why Is It This Way?

The Pharisees saw Jesus eating with tax collectors and sinners, and they asked His disciples: "Why does your teacher eat with tax collectors and sinners?" A good question — it could have sparked a theological, social, or philosophical debate. But Jesus changed the entire paradigm.

🏥 Doctor, Not Judge

⚖️
The Pharisees
Saw themselves as self-righteous judges. They ran away from sinners to preserve their own holiness.
vs
🩺
Jesus
Saw himself as a doctor. He ran toward the sick to bring healing and life.

Jesus responded calmly but authoritatively: "It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick." Every good doctor runs to the sick, not away from them. If you've been to an emergency room, you know — doctors and nurses literally run toward the patient.

💝 Grace Always Comes First

📖 John 1:17 (NIV)
"For the law was given through Moses, but grace and truth came through Jesus Christ."
In the original Greek, word order matters. It is "grace and truth" — not "truth and grace." In Jesus, grace always comes first.

The law given through Moses was not bad — it was given by God. But look at the contrast: grace and truth came through Jesus. Our Savior is a doctor full of grace. That's why we have forgiveness, and that's why we can share it with others.

📜 "Go and Learn What This Means"

Jesus then told the Pharisees: "Go and learn what this means: 'I desire mercy, not sacrifice.'" The phrase "go and learn" was an idiom — it basically meant: You totally missed the point. You think you have a PhD in the Old Testament? Go home and study it again.

Jesus was quoting Hosea 6:6, where God Himself spoke: "I desire mercy, not sacrifice." In Greek, the word for mercy can be translated as compassion. In the original Hebrew, the word is chesed — meaning loyalty, loving-kindness, covenantal love. It is a kind of active love that seeks out its object.

Sacrifice = a vertical relationship between you and God (religious duty)
Mercy / Compassion = a horizontal relationship between you and other people

God desires both — but the horizontal matters more than we think.

⚖️ The More Important Matters

Later in Matthew 23:23, Jesus confronted the Pharisees again: they tithed even their herbs — mint, dill, and cumin — but neglected justice, mercy, and faithfulness. Jesus called these "the more important matters of the law."

Notice the objects: What is the object of justice? People. What is the object of mercy? People. What is the object of faithfulness? People. In God's eyes, people have value.

At the heart of the issue is actually the issue of the heart. If our heart is not in the right place, our acts become meaningless.

— Pastor Albert

The Pharisees were not wrong in obeying the law. They were wrong because their hearts were in the wrong place. They thought getting the outward actions right was enough. But God looks at the heart.

🍽️ As Individuals: Who Do You Bring to Your Table?

The common question in business and church is: "What do you bring to the table?" — focusing on your gifts, skills, and contributions. Pastor Albert changed the question from what to who:

Who do you bring to your table?

You are in touch with people that nobody else in this church is. God has uniquely placed you in your social circle. A colleague going through a difficult time. A friend who just had a miscarriage. A guy who lost his job. A family with financial needs. They are in your network.

Pastor Albert shared examples: a friend who played golf brought the caddy to a local church. Another friend who frequented a restaurant brought the waiter. Jesus brought Matthew to the table. Matthew brought his friends to the table. Who can you bring?

I am created to do something or to be something for which no one else is created. I have a place in God's counsels, in God's will, which no one else has. Whether I be rich or poor, despised or esteemed by man, God knows me and calls me by name. God has created me to do him some definite service. He has committed somewhere to me which he has not committed to another. I have my mission.

— John Henry Newman

You are uniquely placed by God — in your network, in your neighbourhood, in your workplace — because He has an assignment for you. Ask yourself: Who can I bring to my table so that I can introduce them to Jesus?

🗺️ Historical Context: Capernaum & Tax Collectors

📍 Capernaum: The Town of Jesus

Capernaum sat at the northern tip of the Sea of Galilee. While Jesus grew up in Nazareth ("Jesus of Nazareth"), Capernaum was the headquarters of His ministry — called "the town of Jesus." He performed many miracles, gave many teachings, and based much of His public ministry in this area.

🌍 Israel's Geopolitical Position

Israel occupied a critical position on the international trade route. Go north — you hit Lebanon and Syria. Go south — you reach the Sinai Peninsula and Egypt. This made Israel (and Capernaum especially) a prime location for trade — and for taxing that trade.

🛤️ The Via Maris: Highway Through Capernaum

The Via Maris (Latin for "Way of the Sea") was one of the ancient world's most important trade routes, dating from the early Bronze Age. It linked Egypt with the northern empires of Syria, Anatolia, and Mesopotamia. The route followed the Mediterranean coast before turning inland through the Jezreel Valley, passing along the western shore of the Sea of Galilee through Magdala and Capernaum, then crossing the Jordan River near Hazor before climbing northeast into Damascus.

This explains why Capernaum — a seemingly modest fishing town — had a tax collector's booth. It sat directly on this major international highway. Goods flowing between Egypt and Mesopotamia passed through here, making it a strategic point for the Roman tax system. Matthew's booth was likely positioned to collect tariffs on this very traffic.

💰 The Roman Tax System

Israel was not an independent state — it was an occupied territory under the Roman Empire. Rome sent governors to rule and imposed taxes on the population. But they didn't collect taxes themselves. Instead, they auctioned off the right to collect taxes to local businesspeople who bid the highest price.

🏛️
Trade Tariffs
Goods moving north to Syria or south to Egypt were taxed at checkpoints
🏠
Property Tax
Purchased land? Taxed. Tax collectors inflated values to pocket the difference
👜

"Nice handbag! I charge you tax." Even personal items were subject to levies

🚫 Who Were the "Outcasts"?

The "sinners" and outcasts in Jesus' time fell into several overlapping categories:

💰
Tax Collectors
Political traitors working for Rome; dishonest profiteers
⚖️
Criminals
Murderers, thieves, and others who broke the law
🚷
Morally Dubious
Prostitutes and those living immoral lifestyles
📜
Ceremonially Unclean
Violated purity laws — wrong handwashing, handling dead bodies, bodily fluids
🏥
The Sick
Mental illness, leprosy, skin diseases — driven out of town

The Jewish society had strict rules governing sacrifices, tithing (not just money — flock, crops, everything), and ceremonial purity. It was very easy to become "unclean" — and once you were, you were excluded.

📚 The Hebrew Word: Chesed (חֶסֶד)

When Jesus quoted Hosea 6:6 — "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — the word translated "mercy" in the Hebrew original is chesed. This is one of the richest words in the Old Testament:

  • Loyalty — faithful commitment that doesn't waver
  • Loving-kindness — tender compassion in action
  • Covenantal love — love rooted in relationship and promise
  • Active love — love that seeks out its object rather than waiting passively

This is not just feeling sorry for someone. Chesed is love that moves — love that goes to find the lost, comfort the broken, and invite the excluded to the table.

🎯 Following the Way of Mercy

This sermon doesn't just teach us about Jesus — it gives us a pattern to follow. Jesus moved toward those the world moved away from. How can we do the same?

🚶
Move Toward the Mess

When you see someone struggling — socially, emotionally, spiritually — resist the instinct to look away. Jesus didn't need to earn anything from Matthew. He simply moved toward him. Ask: Who in my world is being ignored?

🤲
Take the Initiative

Jesus didn't wait for Matthew to approach Him. Grace initiates. Don't wait for someone to ask for help. Reach out to the colleague who's been quiet, the neighbour who seems overwhelmed, the friend who disappeared. Be the one who moves first.

🍽️
Bring People to Your Table

Shift from "What do I bring to the table?" to "Who do I bring to my table?" God has uniquely placed you in your network. Who is a colleague, friend, or neighbour you could invite into community — and ultimately introduce to Jesus?

🩺
Be a Doctor, Not a Judge

When you encounter people in moral, spiritual, or social trouble, check your posture. Are you approaching as a judge ("they should know better") or as a healer ("how can I help")? Grace leads; truth follows. Both matter, but the order matters too.

❤️
Check Your Heart

"At the heart of the issue is the issue of the heart." The Pharisees' outward obedience was impeccable — they even tithed their herbs. But their hearts were wrong. Ask God to examine not just your actions but your motives.

🙌
Come as You Are

If you're the one who feels like an outcast — messy, unworthy, needing to "clean up" first — hear this: Matthew didn't clean up before following Jesus. He followed first, and grace transformed him. Come to Jesus as you are.

🎯 This Week's Challenge

Identify one person in your network who is going through a difficult time — a colleague, a neighbour, a friend. Someone you might normally pass by or feel unsure about approaching. This week, take the initiative: invite them for a meal, send a message, offer to listen. Move toward them. And pray about whether this is someone God is calling you to bring to your table — and eventually to introduce to Jesus.

🔍 Going Deeper

  • Matthew was a social outcast — a traitor and a cheat — yet Jesus called him first. Is there someone in your life whom you've written off as "too far gone"? What would it look like to move toward them?
  • Many people feel they need to "clean up their act" before coming to Jesus. Have you ever felt that way? How does Matthew's story challenge that thinking?
  • Jesus distinguished between being a doctor and being a judge. In your daily interactions — at work, at church, in your family — which role do you tend to default to? Why?
  • "Grace and truth" — not "truth and grace." How does the order change your approach to people who are struggling or living in ways you disagree with?
  • The Pharisees' outward obedience was impressive — they tithed even their herbs. But Jesus said they neglected "the more important matters." Where in your life might outward religious activity be masking an inward heart problem?
  • Chesed means active, covenantal love that seeks out its object. How is this different from the way our culture typically thinks about love? How does it change what "loving your neighbour" looks like?
  • Pastor Albert asked: "Who can you bring to your table?" Think concretely — name one or two people. What's one step you can take this week?
