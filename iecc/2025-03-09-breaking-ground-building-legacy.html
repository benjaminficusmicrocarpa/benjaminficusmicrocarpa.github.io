Outside the temple gate sat a man who had been lame from birth‚Äîa familiar figure to all who passed by. In that culture, many believed his condition was divine punishment. Rabbis would even say, "Blessed be the righteous judge," effectively blaming the beggar for his own suffering.

With a crowd gathered, Peter preaches again: "You killed the author of life, but God raised him from the dead. We are witnesses of this."

Brought before the religious leaders, Peter and John are warned to stop teaching in Jesus' name. But they respond with unwavering conviction:

When Peter and John returned to the church and reported what had happened, the believers prayed together. But their prayer wasn't what you might expect.

What Do You Pray About? ü§î

Pastor Brett asked a convicting question: If all of your prayers were answered, who would benefit the most?

For most of us, the honest answer is: me. Our prayers revolve around our comfort, our promotion, our well-being, our problems.

But the early church prayed differently. They prayed from a macro view‚Äîacknowledging God's sovereignty over all things‚Äîand then brought their specific circumstances to him. And instead of asking for safety or convenience, they asked for boldness to proclaim the gospel.

Peter and John faced imprisonment, threats, and potential execution. But they couldn't stop talking about what they had seen and heard.
