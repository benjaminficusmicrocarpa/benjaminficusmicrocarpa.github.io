The Lord is Peace

Understanding God's Names

We're beginning a six-week series called Known, exploring different names God uses to reveal aspects of His character. This premise is foundational: what you think about God is the most important thing about you.

Most of us lead with other labels ‚Äî our career, marital status, achievements. But fundamentally, what you think about God drives your motivation, contentment, and understanding of how the world works.

Here's the catch: What you think about God is probably wrong.

Why? Because God is infinite and you are finite. Your capacity to comprehend Him accurately is limited. Plus, we live in a fallen world tainted by sin and imperfection, which clouds our conclusions about how life works.

The challenge ‚Äî and opportunity ‚Äî is to examine what we think about God and let Scripture shape our understanding more accurately.

The Names of God

El or Elohim appears over 2,000 times in the Old Testament, a common word for God. Then in Exodus 3, Moses asks God, "Who should I tell them you are?" God responds: "Tell them I AM THAT I AM" ‚Äî meaning "I am present, I always have been, I always will be."

From that encounter came Yahweh, often abbreviated and replaced with Adonai (Lord) by Jewish people who believed saying the name would minimize His character.

In the 4th century BC, Jewish scribes created Jehovah by inserting vowels from Adonai into Yahweh. While not an original biblical word, it's accurate and meaningful.

God uses many names because no single name can fully describe Him:

  • El Roi ‚Äî The God Who Sees
  • El Shaddai ‚Äî God Almighty
  • Jehovah Jireh ‚Äî The Lord Will Provide
  • Jehovah Nissi ‚Äî The Lord Our Banner
  • Jehovah Shalom ‚Äî The Lord is Peace

Gideon's Story: From Fear to Faith

Today's focus is Jehovah Shalom. The Hebrew word shalom means more than just "peace" as absence of conflict. It's a deep, abiding contentment and wholeness in the midst of chaos.

This name is revealed in the Book of Judges, characterized by the phrase: "Everyone did what was right in his own eyes." Israel cycled through this pattern for 400+ years:

Prosperity ‚Üí Idolatry ‚Üí Oppression ‚Üí Deliverance ‚Üí Prosperity...

Enter Gideon, one of twelve major judges. We find him hiding in a winepress, threshing wheat to save it from Midianite plunderers. He's fearful, lacking courage, totally afraid.

Then an angel appears and says:

"The Lord is with you, mighty warrior."

Let's be clear: nothing in Gideon's life says "mighty warrior." He's hiding in fear. But God speaks prophetically into his life. Gideon must now choose: Will I step into this label? Will I believe what God says about me? Or will I let circumstances rule my life?

You Are What God Declares

You are what God declares you to be. God says so much about who you are. Rather than embracing His label, we often reject it or try to achieve something different ourselves.

Some settle for less, playing a negative tape: "I'm insecure, I don't belong." But God says: "You are My daughter, My son. You have dignity, value, and worth because you're made in My image."

Others create more than what God says: "I got this, God. I'll carve my own way, create my own dignity." This leads to anxiety: What's my next trick? How long can I impress? What happens when my achievements are past?

This is why many face depression at retirement ‚Äî their worth was tethered to career achievement rather than God's declaration.

Gideon's Honest Questions

Gideon responds with brilliantly honest pushback:

"Pardon me, my lord, but if the Lord is with us, why has all this happened to us? Where are all His wonders our ancestors told us about? The Lord has abandoned us!"

He looks at circumstances and bases God's presence on whether things look favorable. Sound familiar? We look at world chaos, financial markets, relationships, careers and think, "God, are You on the throne? Have You noticed?"

Gideon makes three mistakes we often make:

1. Looking at Circumstances

‚ñº

He judges God's presence by visible outcomes. But God's presence isn't contingent on favorable circumstances. The irony? He's speaking to an angel of God while saying "Where is God?"

2. Making Comparisons

‚ñº

"Where are the wonders like our ancestors experienced?" Comparison is a black hole ‚Äî it breeds envy or pride. We're told repeatedly to fix our eyes on Jesus, not on others' blessings or lack thereof.

3. Becoming Critical

‚ñº

His feelings turn into criticism of God's ways, timing, and character. He brings his baggage to the conversation: "You've abandoned us!" This is a raw journal entry we get to witness.

God's Response

God doesn't rebuke Gideon's honesty. Instead:

"Go in the strength you have and save Israel out of Midian's hand. Am I not sending you?"

Gideon protests: "My clan is the weakest in Manasseh, and I am the least in my family!" He's resisting the call, pointing out to God (as if God doesn't know) why he's the wrong choice.

Then God speaks words that change everything:

"I will be with you, and you will strike down all the Midianites, leaving none alive."

The Sign and the Peace

Gideon asks for verification ‚Äî a sincere request God always answers. (Though we don't get to determine how He answers!)

God provides an extravagant sign: fire consumes Gideon's offering. Then God says:

"Peace! Do not be afraid. You are not going to die."

So Gideon built an altar and called it Jehovah Shalom ‚Äî The Lord is Peace.

Gideon found peace when he realized God was with him. Not in a general "God is everywhere" way, but in a particular, specific, and personal way. It's not enough to know God exists ‚Äî we must know God is with us and for us.

How Do We Know God Is With Us?

If God's presence leads to God's peace, how can we be assured? Romans 8 gives us clarity:

"Those who live according to the flesh have their minds set on what the flesh desires. But those who live according to the Spirit have their minds set on what the Spirit desires. The mind governed by the flesh is death, but the mind governed by the Spirit is life and peace."

Peace requires a mindset shift. It's taking our faith from a Sunday hobby to a 24/7 perspective. Not secluding ourselves into monasteries, but bringing God into every area ‚Äî work, relationships, schedules, hobbies.

We do our work "heartily as unto the Lord rather than people." It's seeing life through the lens of the One who made life. This isn't burdensome ‚Äî it's clarity, a beautiful light way to see everything.

Isaiah promises:

"You keep him in perfect peace whose mind is stayed on You, because he trusts in You."

Peace in Tribulation

Jesus gives us gritty reality:

"I have told you these things, so that in Me you may have peace. In this world you will have trouble. But take heart! I have overcome the world."

Following Christ isn't an escape from trouble ‚Äî it's the presence of God and the shalom of peace in the midst of chaos.

The Orchestra Illustration

Think of an orchestra warm-up: utter chaos, screeching, discordant noise. You paid money and think, "Couldn't you have done this before I came?"

But then the conductor walks out. Instruments are stilled. He commands attention. Harmony occurs. Suddenly, chaos becomes a symphony.

That's what God does in our lives. The chaos, noise, anxiety, stress ‚Äî they don't disappear. But when He takes the stage, when we set our minds on what He says, He makes music from it. He infuses peace in the midst of it. Even the most troublesome things gain purpose and meaning.

Life isn't easy. But there can be shalom in the midst of it.

Understanding God's Names

God reveals Himself through many names, each unveiling a different aspect of His character. No single name can fully describe Him because He is infinite and we are finite.

El / Elohim

Meaning: God (general term)
Used: Over 2,000 times in the Old Testament
Significance: The foundational name for deity, used for both the true God and pagan gods

Yahweh (YHWH)

Meaning: "I AM THAT I AM" ‚Äî the self-existent one
Origin: Exodus 3, God's revelation to Moses
Significance: God's personal, covenant name declaring His eternal presence and faithfulness

Jehovah

Origin: Created in 4th century BC by combining YHWH consonants with Adonai vowels
Significance: While not original, it's accurate and meaningful, preserving reverence for God's name

Adonai

Meaning: Lord, Master
Usage: Substituted for YHWH out of reverence
Significance: Emphasizes God's sovereignty and our submission

Revealed in Judges 6:24 when Gideon built an altar after God assured him, "Peace! Do not be afraid." This peace (shalom) is deep, abiding contentment and wholeness even in chaos ‚Äî not merely absence of conflict.
Jehovah Jireh ‚Äî The Lord Will Provide
Abraham named the place where God provided a ram in place of Isaac (Genesis 22:14). Reveals God as our ultimate provider in impossible situations.
Jehovah Nissi ‚Äî The Lord Our Banner
Moses built an altar after victory over the Amalekites (Exodus 17:15). God is our rallying point, our victory, our identity in spiritual warfare.
El Roi ‚Äî The God Who Sees
Hagar named God this in Genesis 16:13 when fleeing Sarah. God sees us in our distress, isolation, and desperation ‚Äî we are never invisible to Him.
El Shaddai ‚Äî God Almighty
Used in Genesis 17:1 when God made covenant with Abraham. Emphasizes God's all-sufficiency, power, and ability to accomplish His promises.

Why Multiple Names Matter

Each name reveals a layer of God's character that our finite minds can grasp. Together, they paint a fuller picture of who God is:

  • Provider when we lack
  • Peace when we're anxious
  • Victory when we're defeated
  • Observer when we're alone
  • Almighty when we're weak

As you encounter different seasons of life, different names of God will resonate more deeply. This is by design ‚Äî He meets us exactly where we are.

Living in Jehovah Shalom

How do we move from knowing about God's peace to actually experiencing it in our daily chaos?

1. Embrace Your God-Given Identity

You are what God declares you to be ‚Äî not what circumstances suggest or what you achieve for yourself.

Practical Steps:

  • Identify the negative tapes playing in your mind (insecurity, unworthiness, comparison)
  • Counter each with what Scripture says: "I am loved" (1 John 3:1), "I am chosen" (1 Peter 2:9), "I am God's workmanship" (Ephesians 2:10)
  • Write these truths down and speak them aloud when anxiety rises
  • Stop striving to create your own dignity through achievement or approval

2. Shift Your Mindset

"The mind governed by the Spirit is life and peace." ‚Äî Romans 8:6

From Sunday Faith to 24/7 Perspective:

  • At work: Do your tasks "heartily as unto the Lord" (Colossians 3:23) ‚Äî excellence becomes worship, not reputation management
  • In relationships: See people as image-bearers of God, not as means to your ends
  • Facing decisions: Ask "What does God think about this?" not just "What do I want?"
  • In crisis: Remember God is the conductor ‚Äî He makes music from chaos

3. Stop the Comparison Trap

Gideon compared his present to Israel's past. We compare ourselves to others' blessings or achievements. Both destroy peace.

Instead:

  • Fix your eyes on Jesus (Hebrews 12:2), not on others' journeys
  • Celebrate others' wins without diminishing your own value
  • Practice gratitude for what God has specifically given you
  • Recognize that your season is unique ‚Äî God's timing is perfect

4. Ask for Signs (Sincerely)

If you sincerely want to know God is with you, He will always show you. Every single time.

But remember:

  • God answers on His terms, not ours ‚Äî don't bargain ("If you give me X, I'll follow")
  • Be open to unexpected answers ‚Äî He may reveal Himself in ways you didn't anticipate
  • Journal your requests and watch for answers ‚Äî you'll build faith muscles
  • God's goal is revealing Himself for who He is, not giving goodies

5. Bring Honest Doubts to God

Gideon's raw honesty didn't offend God. Neither will yours.

Practice Authentic Prayer:

  • "God, where are You in this situation?"
  • "I'm afraid and I don't feel like a mighty warrior"
  • "The world is chaotic ‚Äî are You still on the throne?"
  • "I want to believe, but I need help with my unbelief"

6. Expect Trouble, Experience Peace

"In this world you will have trouble. But take heart! I have overcome the world." ‚Äî John 16:33

Reframe your expectations:

  • Faith doesn't exempt you from hardship ‚Äî it anchors you through it
  • Shalom isn't circumstances improving; it's your soul being at rest regardless
  • When chaos comes (and it will), remind yourself: "God is still with me, God is still for me"
  • The conductor is on the stage ‚Äî trust Him to make harmony from discord
