📋 Sermon Overview

Central Theme: God's help in suffering is not limited to immediate circumstantial rescue. Romans 8:18-27 gives believers two durable tools: a hope anchored in the final redemption of all creation, and the constant intercession of the Holy Spirit who prays when we cannot.

Series context: Week 4 of seven in Romans 8. Week 1 declared no condemnation, Week 2 taught life governed by the Spirit, Week 3 showed believers are adopted by God. This sermon asks: how do adopted children endure present suffering while waiting for future glory?

🌟 Main Takeaways

  1. Present suffering is real — Paul does not deny pain, weakness, sickness, hardship, or frustration.
  2. Christian hope is larger than escape — the hope of Romans 8 is the redemption of humanity and creation itself, not merely circumstantial relief.
  3. Creation groans because of the curse — nature is not merely scenery; it waits for the revealing of the children of God.
  4. False hopes break hearts — hope fixed only on a job, healing, marriage, or deadline cannot bear the full weight of suffering (the Stockdale Paradox).
  5. The Spirit prays for believers — even when pain is too deep for words, the Holy Spirit intercedes according to God's will.
  6. The Spirit redirects desire — over time, his intercession reshapes our prayers toward God's deeper purposes and ultimate redemption.

🛟 Why We Long for Rescue

Pastor Kevin opened with the miracle of Dunkirk. In May 1940, 400,000 British soldiers were trapped at the coast of a small French town, surrounded on all sides by German tanks, the sea at their back, with no real way to defend themselves. The King of England called for a nationwide prayer: "Let us with one heart and one soul, humbly yet confidently, come before the Lord and present our case before him."

Four miracles followed: Hitler inexplicably halted his attack for three days; a massive storm grounded German aircraft; the English Channel became unusually calm for nearly a week; and over 850 civilian boats — fishing vessels, private yachts — crossed to ferry soldiers home. Over 338,000 soldiers were rescued from almost certain defeat.

The story names a familiar human desire. When we are cornered by sickness, job loss, or hardship, we long for God to intervene and change the circumstance. Romans 8 does speak of God's help — but it presents a help that is constant even when circumstances do not immediately change.

⭐ Hope Bigger Than Circumstances

📖 Romans 8:18 (NIV)
"I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us."

Paul writes to Roman believers who knew poverty, sickness, social pressure, and the ordinary fragility of ancient life. Many lived at or below subsistence levels. High infant mortality, low life expectancy, fatal diseases — these were their daily realities. Paul does not minimize their present sufferings, but he places them beside a coming glory so large that comparison fails.

This glory is not merely private relief. Paul says creation itself waits in eager expectation for the children of God to be revealed. The hope is cosmic: because human sin brought the curse upon the ground (Genesis 3), the redemption of humanity signals the redemption of the entire world.

🌍 Creation Groaning

📖 Romans 8:19-22 (NIV)
"For the creation waits in eager expectation for the children of God to be revealed. For creation was subjected to frustration, not by its own choice, but by the will of the one who subjected it, in hope that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay... We know that the whole creation has been groaning as in the pains of childbirth right up to the present time."

Who cursed the world? God did — as an act of justice, not evil, in response to the fall. Three curses followed: upon the serpent, upon the woman (pain in childbirth, relational distortion), and upon the ground (thorns, thistles, and death). All of creation was subjected to frustration because of human sin.

The Greek word for "groaning" describes an intense cry of pain — and in ancient literature, often a death cry. Paul's allusion to childbirth is not merely metaphorical: in ancient Rome, many mothers did not survive delivery. Nature is crying a death cry because of us.

Were it not for the sovereign pleasure of God, the earth would not bear you one moment, for you are a burden to it. The sun does not willingly shine upon you to give you light to serve sin and Satan. The earth does not willingly yield her increase to satisfy your lusts... God's creatures are good and were made for men to serve God with, and do not willingly subserve to any other purpose, and groan when they are abused to purposes so directly contrary to their nature and end.

— Jonathan Edwards, Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God (1741)

Pastor Kevin noted how even natural disasters find some of their origin in human sin — overuse and abuse of resources wrecks havoc on ecosystems and changes weather patterns. Nature is groaning because of the pain we put it through. The only hope for nature is that humanity would be redeemed.

⏳ The Redemption of Our Bodies

📖 Romans 8:23-25 (NIV)
"Not only so, but we ourselves, who have the firstfruits of the Spirit, groan inwardly as we wait eagerly for our adoption to sonship, the redemption of our bodies. For in this hope we were saved. But hope that is seen is no hope at all. Who hopes for what they already have? But if we hope for what we do not yet have, we wait for it patiently."

Believers already have the firstfruits of the Spirit — adoption has begun — but the full redemption of their bodies remains future. When bodies no longer decay and die, it signals that the curse of death has been lifted. And when that curse is lifted, so too all the other curses: on the animal kingdom, on childbirth, on the ground.

The physical resurrection of our bodies is the first domino in the cosmic restoration of all things.

⚠️ The Stockdale Paradox

What we hope for matters. Pastor Kevin introduced the Stockdale Paradox, named after Admiral James Stockdale, a prisoner of war for seven years in Vietnam who was tortured daily but survived.

When asked about the soldiers who did not survive, Stockdale said:

They were the optimists. They were the ones who said, "We're going to be out by Christmas." And Christmas would come and Christmas would go. Then they'd say, "We're going to be out by Easter." And Easter would come and Easter would go. And then Thanksgiving, and then it would be Christmas again. And they died of a broken heart.

— Admiral James Stockdale

Those who endured held an unwavering faith that they would prevail in the end, while simultaneously confronting the brutal reality of their situation. Romans 8 presents the same paradox: if you keep hoping only for circumstantial deliverance — a job by Christmas, a marriage by Easter, healing by Thanksgiving — you risk dying of a broken heart. The hope that actually sustains is the hope of final, eternal, cosmic redemption.

🙏 The Prayer of the Spirit

📖 Romans 8:26-27 (NIV)
"In the same way, the Spirit helps us in our weakness. We do not know what we ought to pray for, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us through wordless groans. And he who searches our hearts knows the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for God's people in accordance with the will of God."

The Holy Spirit who resides in believers prays for them constantly. The phrase "wordless groans" can be read two ways: (1) the Spirit prays with incomprehensible words, which is the basis for some charismatic traditions of speaking in tongues; or (2) the Spirit understands the wordless groans that we have — the pain too deep to articulate — and gives them full expression before the Father. The Greek is ambiguous enough for both readings.

Either way, the Spirit always prays in accordance with the will of God. If any groaning on our heart is contrary to God's will, the Spirit will not pray for it.

God will either give us what we ask or give us what we would have asked if we knew everything he knew.

— Tim Keller

🔄 Transformed Prayers — Pastor Kevin's Story

Pastor Kevin shared how the Spirit's intercession gradually reshaped his own prayers over several years:

  1. The office prayer (2015-2017): Working at Arapahoe Road Baptist Church in Dallas, he walked past a beautiful corner office every day to reach his tiny cubicle. For four years, his prayer was to get that office — to become the youth pastor.
  2. The Asia prayer (2017): After visiting his brother in Japan at Christmas and feeling the absence of Jesus, he suddenly felt a passion for Asia he never expected. His prayer shifted: "Lord, if at all possible, can you make it so that I can minister in Asia?"
  3. The international community prayer (2018): Realizing he was terrible with languages — fifteen years in Hong Kong without speaking Cantonese — the only way he could be effective was among the international community. His prayer narrowed: "Lord, let me minister to the international community in Asia."
  4. The Hong Kong prayer (2019): After a painful four-hour conversation walking away from the corner-office path, Pastor Brett emailed the very next morning asking if he was interested in a pastoral role. His prayer shifted again: "Lord, could I minister to the international community in Hong Kong?"
  5. The young adults prayer (2019): During the summer of 2019, scrolling through headlines about Hong Kong's young people, he felt a specific stirring. His prayer became: "Lord, could I minister to the young adult population in the English-speaking community in Hong Kong?" The offer that came was exactly that: Pastor of Young Adult Ministry.

"I have an office now," Pastor Kevin said. "But actually tomorrow I'll have to vacate it for our growing staff team. I don't even care. Because I have a heart for people, for you, a love for you."

This is the power of the Spirit's intercession: he changes the content of our prayers and the content of our desires, redirecting our hope from small circumstantial wishes to the ultimate hope of redemption.

🕊️ Two Helps God Gives

Romans 8:18-27 presents two primary tools God gives to help believers endure suffering. Neither is about changing circumstances on demand — both are constant, consistent, and always available.

⭐

1. The Hope of Redemption

What it is: The hope that all creation — every person, animal, mountain, valley, nation — will be liberated from bondage to decay when God's children are fully revealed.

Why it matters: Circumstantial hopes (job by Christmas, healing by Easter) break the heart when they fail. The Stockdale Paradox shows that only an unwavering hope in the ultimate outcome sustains people through prolonged suffering.

What it produces: Patient endurance, an expanded time horizon, freedom from despair when immediate relief does not come.

🙏

2. The Prayer of the Spirit

What it is: The Holy Spirit intercedes for believers constantly, articulating the groans of our hearts that we ourselves cannot put into words, always in accordance with God's will.

Why it matters: God always answers the Spirit's prayers, because the Spirit always prays according to the Father's will. Tim Keller says God gives what we ask or what we would have asked if we knew everything he knew.

What it produces: Transformed desires, redirected hope, prayers that shift from circumstantial wishes to kingdom purposes — as Pastor Kevin's personal story illustrates.

🔄 How the Two Helps Work Together

The Spirit's intercession does not merely sustain us through suffering — it actively cultivates the hope of redemption within us. As the Spirit prays 24/7, our hope for the eternal redemption of the world grows stronger. The result is a "holy yearning" for God's kingdom to be present, an eager expectation for the completion of our adoption through the resurrection of our bodies.

Practical check: If you want to know how the Holy Spirit is praying for you right now, pay attention to how you are changing. Take inventory, take stock, reflect on how you are changing. That is how you will know the types of prayers the Holy Spirit is praying for you.

⚠️ What These Helps Are Not

  • They are not a guarantee that every circumstance will be resolved on our timetable.
  • They are not a reason to stop praying specific prayers — we still bring our needs to God.
  • They are not escapism or denial of present pain — Paul says "present sufferings" are real.
  • They are a constant, consistent help that does not depend on whether we are having a good day or a bad day.

🎯 Living Liberated This Week

Application flows from the two helps: anchor your hope in God's final redemption, and trust the Spirit's ongoing intercession to reshape your desires from the inside out.

⭐
Audit Your Hopes

Write down what you are currently hoping for. Ask: is this a circumstantial hope or an eternal one? Circumstantial hopes are legitimate prayers, but they cannot bear the full weight of your endurance. Place them inside the larger hope of redemption.

⏳
Expand Your Time Horizon

Like planting an olive tree that takes seven to ten years to bear fruit, invest in something whose results you may never see. Give, serve, mentor, or build with eternity in view rather than immediate gratification.

🙏
Pray With Honesty

Bring your wordless groans to God — the pain you cannot articulate, the confusion you cannot resolve. Trust that the Spirit is translating those groans into perfect prayers before the Father.

🔍
Notice How You Are Changing

Take inventory this week: where have your desires shifted? What do you care about now that you did not care about a year ago? This is evidence of the Spirit's intercession at work.

🌍
Let Suffering Evoke Longing

When you see atrocities, wars, natural disasters, or personal sin — let it stir longing for the ultimate redemption rather than despair. Use global suffering as a prompt to pray: "Come, Lord Jesus."

🤲
Surrender a Specific Prayer

Name one prayer you have been holding tightly. Tell God: "I trust the Spirit to pray for me on this. Give me what I ask, or what I would ask if I knew everything you know."

  • Paul says "hope that is seen is no hope at all." What does patient endurance look like practically in the situation you are facing right now?
  • How does knowing the Spirit prays "in accordance with the will of God" comfort you when your own prayers feel inadequate or confused?
  • The sermon says the physical resurrection of our bodies is the first sign that all curses are lifted. How does that connect to the way you think about your body, aging, and mortality?

  • For hope: Father, anchor my hope not in circumstances but in the coming redemption of all things. Help me wait patiently.

  • For transformation: Spirit, continue to reshape my prayers and desires. I want to want what you want.
